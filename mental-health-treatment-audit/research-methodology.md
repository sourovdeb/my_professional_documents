# Mental Health Treatment Audit — Investigative Research Methodology

## 1. Claim Identification
Are selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors and related antidepressants clinically
effective for treating depression, or does the underlying trial evidence base
overstate their benefit.

## 2. Verification Disclosure
Live web search was used to verify all sources listed below on July 5, 2026.
Claims about trial design, sample size, and funding rest on the published methods
and disclosure sections of the cited papers. No statistic in this document is
invented. Where a figure could not be independently confirmed it is omitted rather
than estimated.

## 3. Source Hierarchy
1. Primary: peer reviewed meta-analyses and systematic reviews using regulatory
   trial data and published randomized controlled trials.
2. Secondary: peer reviewed methodological reanalyses and critiques of the primary
   sources.
3. Tertiary: not used for this audit.

## 4. Sources Reviewed

1. Cipriani A, Furukawa TA, Salanti G, et al. (2018) Comparative efficacy and
   acceptability of 21 antidepressant drugs for the acute treatment of adults with
   major depressive disorder: a systematic review and network meta-analysis. The
   Lancet, 391(10128), 1357 to 1366. Network meta-analysis of 522 double blind
   randomized controlled trials, 116,477 participants. All 21 antidepressants
   studied were more effective than placebo, with effect sizes described as modest
   to moderate. Funded by the National Institute for Health Research Oxford Health
   Biomedical Research Centre and the Japan Society for the Promotion of Science.
   Source: https://www.thelancet.com/article/S0140-6736(17)32802-7/fulltext

2. Kirsch I, Deacon BJ, Huedo-Medina TB, Scoboria A, Moore TJ, Johnson BT (2008)
   Initial severity and antidepressant benefits: a meta-analysis of data submitted
   to the Food and Drug Administration. PLoS Medicine, 5(2), e45. Reanalysis of
   published and unpublished trial data submitted to the FDA for four
   antidepressants. Found drug placebo differences below accepted clinical
   significance criteria at moderate initial severity, reaching significance only
   in the most severely depressed patients. Academically funded, no pharmaceutical
   industry sponsorship declared.
   Source: https://journals.plos.org/plosmedicine/article?id=10.1371/journal.pmed.0050045

3. Turner EH, Matthews AM, Linardatos E, Tell RA, Rosenthal R (2008) Selective
   publication of antidepressant trials and its influence on apparent efficacy. New
   England Journal of Medicine, 358(3), 252 to 260. Compared FDA registered
   outcomes against matching journal publications for 74 trials of 12
   antidepressants, 12,564 participants. Found 31 percent of trials, representing
   3,449 participants, were never published, and that published effect sizes were
   11 to 69 percent larger than the same trials' FDA data, an average inflation of
   32 percent for the drug class.
   Source: https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMsa065779

4. Moncrieff J, Cooper RE, Stockmann T, Amendola S, Hengartner MP, Horowitz MA
   (2022) The serotonin theory of depression: a systematic umbrella review of the
   evidence. Molecular Psychiatry. Umbrella review of studies through December 2020
   across genetics, receptor and transporter studies, depletion studies, and
   serotonin metabolite research. Found no consistent evidence that depression is
   caused by lowered serotonin concentration or activity. Publicly and
   academically funded through University College London. The lead author is a
   founding member of the Critical Psychiatry Network, a professional advocacy
   stance disclosed here as a potential non-financial conflict of interest.
   Source: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41380-022-01661-0

5. Munkholm K, Paludan-Muller AS, Juul S (2019) Considering the methodological
   limitations in the evidence base of antidepressants for depression: a
   reanalysis of a network meta-analysis. BMJ Evidence-Based Medicine, 25(4).
   Reanalysis arguing that trial level risk of bias and small effect sizes were
   understated in the certainty ratings of source 1 above. No retraction resulted;
   the original authors published a reply, and the disagreement remains an open
   methodological dispute.
   Source: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31248914/

## 5. Conflict of Interest Checklist
- Is the source funded by an organization that benefits from promoting certain
  outcomes. The four primary analyses above were funded by public research bodies
  and universities, not by pharmaceutical manufacturers. No direct financial
  conflict of interest is disclosed in these papers.
- Does the source have professional affiliations that might bias the information.
  Yes for source 4. The lead author's public advocacy position against biological
  models of depression is disclosed above.
- Are there commercial relationships between the source and entities mentioned.
  Not for the analyses themselves. The underlying evidence base analyzed by all
  four, meaning published antidepressant trials, is predominantly conducted and
  funded by the manufacturers of the drugs being tested. This structural
  conflict is documented directly by source 3, which found sponsor associated
  publications overstated benefit relative to the same trials' regulatory data.
- Has the source been involved in controversies or retractions. Source 1 was the
  subject of a published methodological critique, source 5 above. No retraction
  occurred on either side.

## 6. Balance Check
The strongest case for antidepressant efficacy: source 1 is the largest and most
current network meta-analysis, finding a statistically significant advantage over
placebo for every antidepressant studied, across 116,477 participants.
The strongest counter-argument: sources 2 and 3, using regulatory data that
includes unpublished trials, found that the published literature substantially
overstates drug placebo differences, and that the clinical significance of the
average effect is doubtful outside severe depression.
Both bodies of evidence are current, peer reviewed, and draw on overlapping trial
data. Neither has been withdrawn or shown to involve fabrication. The
disagreement concerns how much weight to give unpublished data and how clinical
significance should be defined, not data integrity.

## 7. Micro-blog Post

HEADLINE: Do Antidepressants Work? Two Rigorous Meta-Analyses Reach Different Verdicts

LEAD: The largest antidepressant meta-analysis on record finds every studied drug
beats placebo, but two independent reviews of the same regulatory evidence say the
real-world benefit is smaller than headlines suggest.

FINDINGS:
- A 2018 network meta-analysis of 522 trials and 116,477 patients found all 21
  antidepressants studied were more effective than placebo for major depressive
  disorder [Source: Cipriani et al., 2018, The Lancet]
- A 2008 reanalysis of the same drugs' FDA regulatory data found the drug placebo
  difference fell below the threshold for clinical significance except in the
  most severely depressed patients [Source: Kirsch et al., 2008, PLoS Medicine]
- A companion study found 31 percent of FDA registered antidepressant trials were
  never published, and that the trials that were published overstated benefit by
  an average of 32 percent compared with the same trials' FDA data
  [Source: Turner et al., 2008, New England Journal of Medicine]
- A 2022 umbrella review found no consistent evidence that depression is caused by
  lowered serotonin activity, undercutting the mechanistic story often used to
  explain how these drugs work, independent of whether they help
  [Source: Moncrieff et al., 2022, Molecular Psychiatry]

FUNDING AND CONFLICT OF INTEREST:
All four cited analyses were funded by public research bodies or universities, not
by pharmaceutical manufacturers. The trials underlying all of them, however, are
predominantly conducted and funded by drug manufacturers, and Turner et al.
documented that sponsor-published results ran markedly higher than the same
trials' regulatory data. The lead author of the 2022 review has a disclosed
professional affiliation with a psychiatry-reform advocacy network.

ALTERNATIVE INTERPRETATION:
Clinicians favoring the Cipriani analysis argue that a consistent, statistically
significant advantage across 116,477 patients and 21 drugs is not a small signal,
and that average effect sizes understate benefit for individual patients who
respond well. This is a legitimate reading of the same evidence base.
