# THE KITE WITHOUT A STRING
## A Life of Sourov Deb
### Chittagong to La Réunion, 1986–2026

*A documented biography drawn from medical records, regulatory filings, correspondence, and the subject's own testimony. Names of third parties appear as they stand in the official appeal record. The subject's child is not identified.*

---

## PROLOGUE — LE TAMPON, 19 MAY 2026

A psychiatrist signs a letter through Doctolib at his office on rue Paul Herman. The patient sits across from him. He is thirty-nine. He weighs ninety-seven kilograms and stands one hundred sixty-five centimetres. His blood pressure reads 144 over 108. His heart runs at 103 beats a minute.

The psychiatrist writes the diagnoses in order. Bipolar affective disorder, type one, active. Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder. Complex post-traumatic stress disorder, to confirm. Major depression. Clinical insomnia. The letter goes to the patient's general practitioner, Dr. Pauvert, on Vallon Hoarau, a few streets away.

The patient carries the letter out into the afternoon. He has waited thirty-nine years for these words. He has spent most of his life without them.

His name is Sourov Deb. This is the record of how he arrived at that office.

---

## PART ONE — CHITTAGONG

### 1986–2000

Sourov Deb is born in 1986 in a village near Chittagong, Bangladesh. The family moves into the town. His father and mother live inside an arranged marriage. They fight. The fights stop short of sustained violence but never resolve.

His mother carries water to the top floor of the building while she is pregnant. His father does not strike her. He removes her choices instead. Years later she tells her son she has lived as a mental health patient her whole life, undiagnosed, because her husband refuses the diagnosis.

The family splits its children by school. The brother attends private school. Sourov and his sister attend public school. The household runs short of money.

Sourov moves between schools — one town, one district, then a public school. He reads books with the appetite of a strong mind. He cannot hold his attention on the lessons. Teachers mark him as a boy who misses details. He does not yet have the word for what happens inside his head. The word arrives in 2024. It is ADHD.

A teacher abuses him. A neighbour abuses him. Two others abuse him whose faces he no longer holds. The abuse repeats across his childhood. He tells no one who can stop it.

He runs from home. He steals money. He enrolls in a music school and does poorly there too. He absorbs the frustration of the house — his mother's labour, his father's control, the noise between them — and he carries it forward in his body.

## PART TWO — AUSTRALIA

### 2005–2016

Sourov leaves Bangladesh for Australia. He calls it his escape. He arrives independent and without a guide.

The first months work. He labours, saves, pays rent, studies. Then the structure fails. He cannot concentrate on coursework he finds easy. He tells himself the work is simple, that he holds the liberty to do it on his own terms, that he can wait until the last moment. Discipline leaves. Responsibility leaves.

He wants the society around him to take him in. He drinks. He brings people to bars. He earns more money and spends it. He starts cocaine. He uses alcohol and cocaine for two years.

The money runs through his hands. He stops paying rent. He spends rent money and electricity money on other things. He tells one lie, then the next. He keeps friends and loses the thread of relationships. He says, of this period, that he hurt no one except himself and the trust of his family. He describes himself as a kite without a string.

The same years carry his competence. He learns to work and to work well. He becomes a sommelier. He becomes a barista. He manages teams of up to fifteen people inside fully English-speaking rooms. He works the floor at the Star Casino in Sydney and inside the Merivale Group. He negotiates with international suppliers. He handles VIP clients in rooms that demand precision. He speaks four or five languages.

He performs when others hand him the task. He cannot stand at the front and choose the task himself. He says the front frightens him. He arranges his life so other people decide, and he tells himself he prefers it.

He meets the woman who becomes his wife. He takes the leap toward the right direction.

Then his body and his brain go into shock. His father dies. His child is born. The two events arrive close together. He falls into depression.

---

## PART THREE — FRANCE AND THE ISLAND

### 2017–2024

Sourov reaches France at the end of 2017. After the COVID-19 pandemic he settles on La Réunion, the French island in the Indian Ocean. His wife works as a nurse's aide. They raise a daughter.

In 2024 a clinician diagnoses his ADHD. He starts methylphenidate — Ritalin, then Concerta. The word that explains his childhood and his Australian years lands at thirty-eight.

He teaches English in a Réunion primary school across 2024 and 2025. He applies the methods he is studying. He builds lessons for different ages and levels and fits them into the existing curriculum.

A conflict with an employer at a coffee shop ends his work. He goes on sick leave at the end of 2024. He does not return.

He builds a YouTube channel with photography and videography across four software tools. He plans a second channel on mental health. He plans a blog. He decides to become a teacher of English and enrolls toward the Cambridge CELTA — the certificate that the English-teaching world recognises above the rest.

## PART FOUR — THE COURSE

### 5 January – 6 February 2026

Sourov registers for the CELTA at the ELT Hub, centre FR023, course C1/2026. The course runs online from 5 January to 6 February 2026. He teaches eight assessed lessons of forty-five minutes each — three hundred sixty minutes of teaching practice — at two levels, to classes that average seven students.

At interview the centre asks whether he holds any health issue that could affect his performance. He answers no. The centre sends him the Candidate Agreement form. The form explains why a candidate benefits from declaring a disability even when he withholds it at interview. He signs the form without comment.

He teaches. Tutors grade four lessons to standard — TP1, TP3, TP4, TP8 — and four below standard — TP2, TP5, TP6, TP7. He passes all four written assignments, three on resubmission, one on first submission.

On 22 January the tutor conducts the Stage Two Progress Record. The tutor grades him to standard for that stage. Sourov grades himself below standard. In the box that invites candidates to raise problems — attendance, resources, workload, need for support — he writes that he has no issues with the assignments and that the workload is manageable. He suggests a short break would have helped after the shock and fatigue of the first week.

On 25 January, twelve days into the course, with eight days to run, Sourov writes to his tutor, Simon Brooks. He describes limitations in his brain and a weak prefrontal cortex. He writes that he sends the email not to ask for special consideration regarding health. He does not name the ADHD diagnosis or the depression or the medication. The tutor acknowledges the message within twelve minutes and proposes that they meet to discuss outstanding issues.

On 27 January the tutor conducts the Stage Three Progress Record. He has taught two consecutive below-standard lessons, TP5 and TP6. The tutor lists thirteen criteria below standard across planning and teaching. He grades the stage below standard. He writes that in the final two lessons every listed criterion must reach standard. Sourov signs that he has read and agrees with the comments.

On 30 January the course assessor completes a remote assessment. She observes TP7 and agrees it sits below standard. She writes that the course was well designed, the candidates satisfied, the trainers attuned. She notes fast teacher talk, confusing slides, tasks students could not follow. She records the impression of a weak candidate who shows some training but not the standard expected in the penultimate lesson.

On 31 January the tutors issue a warning letter. The letter lists twelve criteria still below standard after TP7. It states that to hold a possibility of passing, he must pass his final lesson and bring every listed criterion to standard. He signs to acknowledge receipt. Six days have passed since his disclosure.

He teaches TP8, his final lesson. The tutor highlights the planning criteria from TP7 as met. The tutor highlights most teaching criteria as met. The tutor records three criteria — 3a, 5c, 5f — as not met. The tutor grades the lesson to standard. On the feedback sheet the tutor writes that he felt Sourov deserved to pass TP8, and that he based this on significant improvement in planning and progress in presentation and practice. The tutor writes that key areas remain below standard, and that across the whole course Sourov has not achieved enough to recommend a pass.

The tutors and the assessor confer. They conclude that the progress in the final lesson does not lift the whole course to a pass. They record the grade as Fail. The Joint Chief Assessor confirms it.

## PART FIVE — THE APPEAL

### February – April 2026

On 26 February, after the centre confirms the final grade, Sourov asks for the complaints procedure. The centre responds. He remains dissatisfied.

On 4 March he submits an appeal letter. He writes that his intention is not to dispute professional judgement but to request documented clarification on how the centre applied its assessment framework and its own published policies to the complete record of the course. He sets out seven procedural questions. He asks where the threshold between progress noted and criterion met is defined. He asks how the line "I feel that you achieved enough to Pass" sits beside a Fail recommendation. He asks whether the centre opened a support review after his 25 January disclosure, under its published policy and its CPF and Qualiopi obligations. He asks whether the assessor reviewed the complete portfolio, including TP8, before approving the recommendation.

The centre answers each question. On the disclosure, the centre writes that it asked about health at interview, that he answered no, that it sent the agreement form explaining the value of declaring a disability, that he signed without comment, and that on 25 January he described limitations in his brain without revealing the ADHD and depression diagnosis or the medication.

The Chief Moderator reviews the documentation. The Chief Moderator finds one error in the record — the assignment marked pass on resubmission passed on first submission — and a heading on the TP7 feedback that names the wrong stage, and a typing correction on page 31 of the CELTA5 left without formal annotation. The Chief Moderator confirms the substance of the centre's response on every question.

On 23 April the Independent Reviewer issues the Stage One Appeal Report. The report runs eighteen pages. The Independent Reviewer does not re-mark the work. The Independent Reviewer confirms that Cambridge English used procedures consistent with the regulator's code, applied them fairly, and reached a correct outcome. The report states that the grade of Fail should stand.

## PART SIX — THE NAME FOR IT

### 19 May 2026

Three weeks after the appeal report, Sourov sits in Dr. Romain Padovani's office at Le Tampon.

He describes the symptoms. Emptiness and loss of hope through most of the day. Physical fatigue. Insomnia that pushes sleep late and then holds him down past waking. Fifteen kilograms gained. Worthlessness and guilt. Lost interest. He cannot concentrate on tasks he knows.

He describes the highs. Periods longer than seven days when energy floods him, when he needs little sleep, when ideas race and speech runs and he spends without restraint, when his sexual conduct slips its limits and a physical fight comes close. He describes derealisation, gaps in memory around the worst of his past, the sense that he is not always the same person, the tremor in his limbs when he comes out of freezing.

He reports the history. The childhood abuse. The two years of alcohol and cocaine, now ended. The abstinence he holds.

Padovani runs the scales. The depression scale returns moderate. The anxiety scale returns moderate. The insomnia scale returns clinical. The daytime sleepiness scale returns severe. The brain scan, the heart trace, the EEG — all normal.

Padovani writes the conclusion. Bipolar disorder type one. ADHD. Complex PTSD tied to the abuse and the conflict of his childhood, to confirm. He maps the plan: continued psychiatric care, education on trauma, a referral to the trauma unit, a sleep study, no change to the medication that day.

The diagnoses describe the boy who could not hold his attention, the young man who could not stand at the front, the worker who performed under pressure and collapsed without it, the candidate who disclosed his condition twelve days into a course and watched the threshold rise around him.

## PART SEVEN — THE RECORD

### 24 May 2026 onward

On 24 May Sourov files a complaint with Ofqual, the regulator of qualifications in England. The reference reads SJ3XP35D. The complaint names the CELTA course, centre FR023, the ELT Hub.

He assembles the documents. The medical records. The Candidate Agreement form that names the centre's disability contact. The centre's published equal opportunities policy. The disclosure email and its twelve-minute acknowledgement. The warning letter dated six days after the disclosure. The CELTA5 portfolio. The teaching practice feedback that records "I feel that you achieved enough to Pass." The appeal letter and the centre's response and the eighteen-page report that rejects it.

He builds a case from the gap he sees: a centre that asked about health, received a disclosure mid-course, acknowledged it, and changed nothing in the assessment that followed.

He turns toward what comes next. He looks into French disability recognition — the RQTH, the allowance, the free care that bipolar disorder unlocks. He drafts letters to journals and newspapers and publishers. He plans the research papers, the blog, the channel on mental health. He decides to put his name and his record where others can read them.

His wife works. His daughter turns eighteen months. He is tired from the medication. He says that in his mind, these days, he is doing okay.

---

## EPILOGUE — THE STRING

For most of his life Sourov Deb moved without a string — the kite he named in his own account, pulled by wind he could not feel or fight. The abuse he could not stop. The attention he could not hold. The moods he could not place. The certificate that failed him after he asked, late and unclearly, for help.

The string is the record. The diagnoses give names to the wind. The complaint files the failure where a regulator must read it. The documents — every email, every grade, every signed page — fix in place what happened and when.

He has not been cured. The conditions run for life. The treatment runs for months and years. But the boy who missed the details now holds the complete file, and he has decided what to do with it.

He has decided to tell it.
