## Slide 2 — The Hook (0:00–0:15)

**Say:** "You know how in Excel or Word you can record a macro that repeats a boring task for you? Now imagine that macro could actually *look at the situation*, decide what to do next, and finish the whole job — without you clicking a single button. That's what people mean by an 'AI agent.'"

**[DOODLE]** Left: a tired stick figure clicking a stack of buttons over and over. Right: the same figure relaxing with a coffee while a small robot arm finishes the stack of buttons in one smooth sweep.

**[USER NOTE]** Pause half a beat after "click a single button" — let the contrast land before moving on. If recording live, a 3-second screen capture of recording a real macro works great here.

---

## Slide 3 — Plain Definition (0:15–0:40)

**Say:** "Here's the plain-English definition: an AI agent is a program that pairs an AI model with a goal and a set of tools, then lets it figure out the steps to reach that goal — mostly on its own. Think 'AI that *does*,' not just 'AI that *answers*.'"

**[DOODLE]** Three icons connected by arrows: a brain (Think) → a checklist with checkmarks (Plan) → a gear/robot hand (Do).

**[USER NOTE]** This is the one slide people will screenshot — keep the three words (Think / Plan / Do) large and uncluttered.

---

## Slide 4 — Analogy #1: Office Suite (0:40–1:15)

**Say:** "Picture three tools you already know. Autocorrect just fixes one word — a single, tiny decision. Mail Merge takes a template and a list and produces a stack of letters — useful, but it always does the exact same steps in the exact same order. Now imagine a macro that opens your inbox, reads today's sales emails, decides which ones actually matter, drafts a summary, and drops it in a doc for you — only checking in when it's unsure. That *string of decisions*, not just steps, is what makes it agent-like."

**[DOODLE]** Three-panel comic strip:
1. A red squiggly-underlined word gets fixed with one click.
2. A template + a list of names merges into a stack of letters.
3. An inbox icon → a thought bubble → a finished summary doc, with a small robot helper running between the three panels.

**[USER NOTE]** Emphasize the word "decisions" — that's the whole difference between automation and an agent. Slow down on panel 3, it's the payoff.

---

## Slide 5 — Analogy #2: Web Browser (1:15–1:40)

**Say:** "Or think about your browser. A basic tool might just highlight a word you select — that's it, one job. An agent-like extension could notice you're comparing flights across five open tabs, pull all the prices together, and even fill out the booking form for the cheapest one — making its own calls along the way."

**[DOODLE]** A browser window with five tabs; a magnifying glass hops from tab to tab, then lands on a filled-in checkout form with a green checkmark.

**[USER NOTE]** Good spot for a quick hand-drawn "hop, hop, hop" arrow animation across the tabs if this becomes a real video.

## Slide 6 — Mistral Studio in Action (1:40–2:20)

**Say:** "Here's what this looks like in a real tool: Mistral Studio, Mistral AI's builder platform. When you build an agent there, you set three things — a **goal** (what you want done), **tools** it's allowed to use (web search, code execution, file or data connectors), and a **model** — the 'brain' doing the reasoning. Once it's built, you can ask it something like 'summarize this week's sales emails and draft a report,' and the agent reads, decides, drafts, and hands you the result. Same idea as our office macro — just powered by an AI model instead of a fixed set of steps."

**[DOODLE]** A simple three-box builder panel — "GOAL" box, "TOOLS" box (wrench icon), "MODEL" box (chip icon) — all three funneling into a robot character holding a finished report.

**[USER NOTE]** If recording live, a 4–5 second real screen-recording of the Mistral Studio agent-builder screen slots in perfectly here — swap the doodle for the real screenshot if you have one.

## Full spoken script (continuous, for teleprompter/voiceover use)

> Welcome back to AI Concepts Made Simple. Episode one: what is an AI agent?
>
> You know how in Excel or Word you can record a macro that repeats a boring task for you? Now imagine that macro could actually look at the situation, decide what to do next, and finish the whole job — without you clicking a single button. That's what people mean by an "AI agent."
>
> Here's the plain-English definition: an AI agent is a program that pairs an AI model with a goal and a set of tools, then lets it figure out the steps to reach that goal — mostly on its own. Think "AI that does," not just "AI that answers."
>
> Picture three tools you already know. Autocorrect just fixes one word — a single, tiny decision. Mail Merge takes a template and a list and produces a stack of letters — useful, but it always does the exact same steps in the exact same order. Now imagine a macro that opens your inbox, reads today's sales emails, decides which ones actually matter, drafts a summary, and drops it in a doc for you — only checking in when it's unsure. That string of decisions, not just steps, is what makes it agent-like.
>
> Or think about your browser. A basic tool might just highlight a word you select — that's it, one job. An agent-like extension could notice you're comparing flights across five open tabs, pull all the prices together, and even fill out the booking form for the cheapest one — making its own calls along the way.
>
> Here's what this looks like in a real tool: Mistral Studio, Mistral AI's builder platform. When you build an agent there, you set three things — a goal, the tools it's allowed to use, and a model, the "brain" doing the reasoning. Once it's built, you can ask it something like "summarize this week's sales emails and draft a report," and the agent reads, decides, drafts, and hands you the result. Same idea as our office macro — just powered by an AI model instead of a fixed set of steps.
>
> So here's the short version: a plain AI chat answers a question. An AI agent takes a goal, uses tools, and gets the whole multi-step job done — the difference between spellcheck and a macro that runs your entire morning report for you.
>
> Next time: what actually powers that "brain" inside the agent. We'll unpack what an AI "model" is — using the same everyday analogies. See you then.
