### CORRECTION 1 — CELTA Fail Rate (Section 2.6)
**Status: 🔴 Critical — and your argument becomes STRONGER with the correction**

**Current text:**
> "Industry sources confirm a CELTA pass rate of approximately 95% (DC Teacher Training; Jo Gakonga, 2025). A fail recommendation places a candidate in the bottom 5% of all candidates."

**What the data actually shows:**
According to Cambridge University English Assessment's own 2023 data (cited by IH World, 2024):
- Pass: 67.4%
- Pass B: 23.0%
- Pass A: 5.3%
- **Fail rate: approximately 0.4%**

The ~5% figure likely conflates *withdrawal before completion* with *failure after completion*. These are legally and evidentially distinct categories.

**Why this correction strengthens your argument:**
A fail recommendation does not place the candidate in the bottom 5% — it places them in the **bottom 0.4% of all candidates who complete the course**. This dramatically amplifies the statistical anomaly argument. Being in the bottom 0.4% while simultaneously passing 4/8 TPs, achieving 4/4 written assignments, and receiving "To Standard" at two mid-course tutorials requires not merely "exceptional justification" — it requires justification that the existing documentary record demonstrably fails to provide.

**Replacement language:**
> "Cambridge Assessment English's own data (IH World, 2024, citing Cambridge University English Assessment, 2023) confirms a completion-based fail rate of approximately 0.4%: of candidates who complete the CELTA, 67.4% receive a Pass, 23.0% a Pass B, and 5.3% a Pass A. A fail recommendation therefore places a candidate in the bottom 0.4% of all completing candidates — an outcome of profound statistical rarity requiring commensurate evidentiary justification."

**New citation to add to references:**

### CORRECTION 3 — "1962-Origin 120-Hour Model" (Abstract, Sections 1.1, 1.2)
**Status: 🔴 Critical — factually imprecise and legally weakens your claim**

**Current framing:**
> "whose 120-hour intensive format... has remained largely unchanged since its introduction in 1962"

**What the historical record shows:**
- **1962**: IH London introduces a teacher training certificate (originally **100 hours**, not 120), under John Haycraft
- **1977–1988**: RSA takes over administration as the RSA PrepCertTEFLA
- **1988**: Rebranded CTEFLA (Certificate in Teaching English as a Foreign Language to Adults) under RSA/Cambridge joint management — this is when the modern assessed format begins
- **1996**: Renamed RSA/Cambridge CELTA
- **2001**: Renamed simply Cambridge CELTA
- **2011**: Blended/online delivery introduced

The **120-hour minimum** and the **current assessment framework** date primarily from the 1988–1996 period, not 1962. Additionally, the course *has* undergone revisions — online delivery, syllabus updates, the 2011 changes. Claiming "largely unchanged since 1962" is vulnerable to a single Wikipedia search by a reviewer.

**Why this matters beyond accuracy:**
Your broader argument (CELTA as a Nokia/Kodak-style institution resistant to change) is actually *better supported* by the accurate history: CELTA's predecessors took 26 years (1962–1988) to formalize, and the current framework has been substantively unchanged for approximately 35 years (since 1988–1996). That is still a compelling critique of institutional inertia, and it is defensible.

**Replacement language:**
> "whose 120-hour intensive assessment framework, formalized under the RSA/Cambridge CTEFLA in 1988 and bearing a continuous institutional lineage to International House London's 1962 precursor certificate (IH World, 2024; Wikipedia, 2025), has undergone no substantive structural reform in approximately three decades"

This framing: (a) is historically accurate, (b) preserves the rhetorical force of your argument, and (c) is not vulnerable to factual rebuttal.

### CORRECTION 4 — Kahneman (2011) Misattribution (Sections 1.2 & 2.7)
**Status: 🔴 Critical — misattribution of an intellectual concept**

**Current text:**
> "attributable to what Kahneman (2011) terms 'survivor bias'"

**The problem:**
Kahneman does not *coin* or *develop* survivor bias in *Thinking, Fast and Slow*. The concept's intellectual origins trace to WWII statistician Abraham Wald (1943), and its rigorous academic treatment appears in finance (Elton, Gruber & Blake, 1996) and organizational studies long before Kahneman. Kahneman discusses *related* availability heuristics and sample bias, but citing him as the source of "survivor bias" will signal to reviewers that you are using a secondary popular science reference as if it were the primary conceptual source.

**Two defensible options:**

*Option A — Cite Kahneman accurately (lower scholarly weight):*
> "a phenomenon consistent with what Kahneman (2011) discusses under availability bias — the tendency to reason from salient, surviving cases while remaining blind to absent, non-surviving ones"

*Option B — Replace with stronger academic citation (recommended):*
> "a function of survivorship bias (Elton, Gruber & Blake, 1996), wherein only the experiences of successful candidates enter professional discourse, systematically suppressing evidence of procedural failure"

**Citation for Option B:**
> Elton, E. J., Gruber, M. J., & Blake, C. R. (1996). Survivorship bias and mutual fund performance. *The Review of Financial Studies, 9*(4), 1097–1120. https://doi.org/10.1093/rfs/9.4.1097

## PART 3: STRUCTURAL RESTRUCTURING FOR PEER REVIEW

The paper's current architecture creates three vulnerabilities that experienced peer reviewers will exploit. This section identifies each vulnerability and provides a restructuring strategy.

---

### VULNERABILITY A — Scope Mismatch with JEFL

**The problem:**
JEFL's stated scope is "teaching and learning English as a foreign language, with specific focus on developing and non-English speaking countries." Your case involves France (developed, non-English-speaking) operating under UK regulatory frameworks — a dual jurisdiction that is genuinely interesting but sits awkwardly within JEFL's geographic focus.

**The solution — reframe the contribution:**
The paper's most transferable insights concern: (1) how intensive certification models affect non-native speaker candidates globally, (2) how disability accommodation frameworks intersect with EFL teacher training in non-English-speaking contexts, and (3) how CELTA-as-global-product operates across jurisdictions where local labor law (France's Code du travail) intersects with UK qualification frameworks.

**Revised framing for Abstract and Introduction:**
Begin by foregrounding the global reach of CELTA in non-English-speaking, developing contexts — the vast majority of CELTA candidates globally are non-native English speakers in precisely the countries JEFL covers. Position France not as the focus but as the *site* of a case that illuminates dynamics relevant to any non-English-speaking country where CELTA operates.

Suggested opening reframe:
> "With CELTA operating in over 70 countries, the majority of candidates globally undertake this qualification in non-English-speaking contexts where local disability law, labor rights, and assessment cultures intersect with British regulatory frameworks. This study examines one such case..."

---

### VULNERABILITY B — Single Case Supporting Sweeping Claims

**The problem:**
A single-case forensic autoethnography is methodologically rigorous for documenting what *happened* in one case. It is not methodologically sufficient to support claims such as "CELTA is following the Nokia/Kodak trajectory" or "CELTA's resistance to structural reform constitutes an existential threat." Reviewers will ask: how does one candidate's experience support a claim about an entire global certification system?

**The solution — two-tier argument structure:**

Restructure the paper into two clearly separated analytical tiers:

**Tier 1 — What This Case Demonstrates (evidential claims):**
These are claims your documentary record directly supports:
- That specific procedural obligations under the Equality Act 2010 and the Candidate Agreement were triggered and not documented
- That the warning letter imposed criteria exceeding Cambridge Syllabus p.18's holistic assessment standard
- That the summative record was altered post-course without annotation, potentially violating Ofqual G8
- That contradictions exist between documented performance and the fail recommendation

Frame these as *demonstrated findings* with full evidentiary grounding.

**Tier 2 — What This Case Raises (contextual questions):**
These are the broader structural critiques (Nokia/Kodak trajectory, cognitive load concerns, neurodiversity gaps) that the case *illustrates* but does not *prove*. Frame these explicitly as questions the case motivates rather than conclusions it establishes.

**Practical restructuring suggestion:**
Move the Nokia/Kodak analysis, the Thornbury critique, and the "fitness-for-purpose" argument from the Literature Review into a clearly labeled Discussion subsection titled something like: "2.12 Contextual Questions Raised by This Case." This signals to reviewers that you understand the methodological boundary between what your data proves and what it illuminates.

---

### VULNERABILITY C — Conflation of Legal, Ethical, and Educational Claims

**The problem:**
The paper simultaneously argues: (1) specific legal violations occurred, (2) assessment was ethically invalid, and (3) CELTA's entire pedagogical model is educationally obsolete. These are three different types of claims requiring three different evidentiary standards. Conflating them allows a reviewer to dismiss the structural critique by attacking the legal argument, or vice versa.

**The solution — three-lane architecture:**

Restructure your findings and discussion around three clearly separated analytical lanes:

**Lane 1 — Procedural/Legal Analysis:**
Focus: What specific obligations were triggered? What does the documentary record show? What regulatory standards apply?
Evidence standard: Documentary, contractual, and statutory — your strongest lane.

**Lane 2 — Assessment Validity Analysis:**
Focus: Does the documented performance trajectory support the fail recommendation under Cambridge's own holistic assessment standard?
Evidence standard: Messick's validity framework applied to the candidate's performance record.

**Lane 3 — Structural/Pedagogical Critique:**
Focus: What broader questions does this case raise about the fitness-for-purpose of intensive certification models for neurodivergent or health-disclosing candidates?
Evidence standard: Literature synthesis — explicitly framed as raising research questions, not drawing conclusions from one case.
