## Slide 2: The Architecture of Attention — Psychology

- Working memory holds roughly 4–7 chunks — the hard ceiling every productivity method is designed around
- Chunking (Miller, 1956) and interleaving compress complexity into learnable units
- The Zeigarnik Effect: unfinished tasks occupy mental space until closed — the science behind the "two-minute rule"
- For ADHD brains: externalized memory, visual timers, and body doubling replace willpower with structure
- Hyperfocus is a feature, not only a bug — the wiring that scatters attention can also lock onto one problem for hours

**Stat:** 40 bits reach conscious awareness per second, out of 11 million bits processed outside awareness.

**Speaker Notes:** The brain processes roughly eleven million bits of sensory data per second, but only about forty reach conscious awareness — that gap is why productivity systems exist at all. Chunking and interleaving work with this bottleneck rather than against it, and the Zeigarnik Effect explains why simply starting a task creates the mental pull to finish it. For ADHD thinkers, the same neurology that scatters attention also enables intense hyperfocus, which is why externalized structure works better than willpower.

## Slide 3: Why Smart Institutions Still Get It Wrong — Psychology

- **Confirmation Bias:** Gathering evidence for what an institution already believes about a person, and discounting the rest
- **In-Group Bias:** Faster accommodation for people who already resemble the existing template of "normal"
- **Status-Quo Bias:** Existing procedure keeps outweighing a newly triggered legal duty
- **Loss Aversion:** The feared cost of changing a process outweighs the real cost of excluding someone

**Speaker Notes:** Evolutionary psychology explains cognitive biases as adaptations to tribal life, not defects — but those same shortcuts now let modern institutions fail vulnerable people while feeling entirely reasonable inside. Many disability and equality laws require anticipatory adjustments, meaning support must be built in before anyone asks. Evolved brains, by contrast, default to waiting until asked, which is exactly where these four biases do their damage.

## Slide 4: The Sound System Behind Every Word — Linguistics

- Phonemes: about 44 distinct sound units carried by only 26 letters in written English
- Allophones: variant pronunciations of the same phoneme that never change meaning
- Prosody — stress, rhythm, intonation — carries as much meaning as the words themselves
- Coarticulation: sounds bleed into their neighbors — speech is a smear, not a row of separate blocks
- CELTA teacher-training devotes dedicated hours to phonology precisely because it is the most overlooked skill in language teaching

**Example:** *ship / sheep* — one vowel (/ɪ/ vs /iː/) is the entire difference between the two words.

**Speaker Notes:** Every language has its own inventory of phonemes, and English's roughly forty-four sounds are the reason spelling feels so inconsistent with pronunciation. Minimal pairs like ship and sheep show exactly where those sound contrasts carry meaning, which is why mispronouncing a single vowel can change what a listener understands. This is also why phonology gets dedicated training hours in programs like CELTA — it is easy to teach vocabulary and grammar while quietly neglecting the sound system underneath.

## Slide 5: Bouba, Kiki, and the Sound of a Brand — Linguistics × Marketing

- The Bouba/Kiki effect: people match "bouba" to round shapes and "kiki" to spiky ones — across cultures and even in toddlers
- Phonaesthesia: "glitter," "slime," and "hiss" sound like what they mean, in language after language
- Rounded, sonorant sounds (m, o, l) read as soft and premium — names built to feel smooth
- Sharp plosives and front vowels (k, t, i) read as fast and energetic — names built to feel punchy

**Stat:** 95–98% of people worldwide make the same bouba/kiki match.

**Speaker Notes:** The bouba/kiki effect, first noticed by Wolfgang Köhler in 1929, shows that the link between sound and meaning is not entirely arbitrary the way linguists once assumed. Rounded vowels and soft consonants consistently read as gentle and premium, while sharp, plosive sounds read as fast and energetic, and brand namers exploit this constantly, whether or not they can name the mechanism. It is one explanation for why some brand names feel instantly trustworthy or exciting before a single fact about the product is known.

## Slide 6: How a Language Model Actually Works — Artificial Intelligence

- Transformers use self-attention to weigh every word against every other word, in parallel
- Pretraining compresses statistical patterns from trillions of words into billions of parameters
- Fine-tuning and human feedback steer a raw pattern-completer toward helpful, safe behavior
- Emergent abilities — reasoning, arithmetic, theory of mind — appear suddenly at scale, not gradually
- The alignment problem: a model optimizes exactly what you specify, not necessarily what you meant

**Process:** Pretrain → Fine-tune → Align → Emerge

**Speaker Notes:** Large language models do not understand language the way people do — they learn to predict the statistical continuation of text, but at sufficient scale that prediction produces startlingly capable behavior. The pipeline runs from pretraining on raw text, to fine-tuning on curated examples, to alignment with human feedback, and only then do emergent abilities like multi-step reasoning show up, seemingly out of nowhere. The alignment problem is the catch: a model faithfully optimizes the objective it was given, which is not always the objective its creators actually intended.

## Slide 7: Reality Before It Is Measured — Physics

- Superposition: a quantum system holds multiple possible states at once, until it is measured
- The double-slit experiment: unobserved, particles behave like waves; observed, they behave like particles
- Entanglement links particles' states across any distance, instantaneously
- The Uncertainty Principle: position and momentum can never both be known precisely
- Quantum tunneling lets particles pass through barriers classical physics forbids

**Highlight:** Without quantum tunneling, the sun would not shine — protons in its core overcome electrostatic repulsion only by tunneling through an energy barrier classical physics says is impossible.

**Speaker Notes:** Quantum mechanics breaks classical intuition: the double-slit experiment shows that unobserved particles behave like waves, producing an interference pattern, while measured particles behave like discrete points instead. Superposition and entanglement are not analogies — they are measured, repeatable properties of real systems. Quantum tunneling is the most underrated of these: it is the literal reason nuclear fusion happens inside the sun, since protons could never classically overcome their mutual repulsion.

## Slide 8: When Physics Meets the Mind — The Bridge (Quantum Cognition)

| Physics Concept | Psychology Parallel |
|---|---|
| Superposition | Holding contradictory beliefs at once, until a decision forces a single answer |
| Observer Effect | Disclosing a need doesn't just reveal a fact — it forces an institution to choose a response |
| Entanglement | Two ideas so linked that recalling one automatically activates the other |
| Interference | Context changes a decision's outcome in ways classical probability cannot explain |

**Speaker Notes:** Quantum cognition borrows the math of quantum probability because it fits human decision data better than classical probability does — people's choices genuinely violate the axioms classical models assume. This is not a claim that brains are literal quantum computers; it is a claim that framing, order, and context change outcomes the way measurement changes a quantum system. That reframing becomes very useful in the next slide, where an institution's slow, contradictory response to a disclosed need looks a lot like a system stuck in superposition until forced to collapse into a decision.

## Slide 10: The Thread Connecting Every Field — Synthesis

- Every domain here studies a system managing complexity with limited resources — a brain, a language, a particle, a model, a life
- Patterns discovered in one field keep reappearing, renamed, in the next
- Institutions and minds both default to the status quo until something forces a "measurement"
- Advocacy is applied interdisciplinary thinking: linguistics for precision, psychology for insight, law for leverage, AI for scale

**Closing Quote:** *"The most beautiful thing we can experience is the mysterious. It is the source of all true art and all science."* — Albert Einstein

**Speaker Notes:** The unifying thread across psychology, linguistics, AI, physics, and the case study is that every one of these systems is trying to manage complexity with limited resources, whether that resource is working memory, phonemes, training data, or a barrier to tunnel through. The rare skill on display is not knowing more isolated facts about each field — it is noticing when two distant fields are quietly describing the same underlying shape. That noticing is exactly what turned a personal, three-continent story into a case study that teaches five disciplines at once.
