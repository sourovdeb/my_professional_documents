## SLIDE 2 · PSYCHOLOGY

### Productivity Protocols: Working With the Brain, Not Against It

- Ultradian rhythms: the brain focuses in ~90-minute waves — schedule sprints, not marathons
- Implementation intentions: "When X happens, I will do Y" triples follow-through (Gollwitzer)
- The Zeigarnik effect: unfinished tasks haunt working memory — write them down to silence them
- Attention residue: every task-switch leaves a cognitive 'stain' that lasts up to 20 minutes
- Protocol beats willpower: pre-decided rules outperform in-the-moment motivation

**Speaker notes:** Most productivity advice fails because it fights biology. These findings are rarely taught together: your brain cycles through high-focus and low-focus phases roughly every 90 minutes, so the trick is surfing the wave, not forcing it. Peter Gollwitzer's implementation intentions research shows that pre-deciding 'when situation X arises, I will respond with Y' roughly triples the odds of actually doing it — a protocol, not a wish. And the Zeigarnik effect explains why writing a to-do list feels like relief: the brain stops rehearsing open loops once they're safely stored outside the head.

## SLIDE 3 · PSYCHOLOGY

### Evolutionary Bias: The Smoke Detector in Your Skull

- Mismatch theory: brains built for the savannah now run modern offices — errors are the default
- Error management (the 'smoke detector principle'): evolution prefers cheap false alarms over one fatal miss
- Loss aversion: losses hurt ~2× more than equal gains feel good (Kahneman & Tversky)
- Status and tribe: workplace conflict triggers the same circuitry as ancestral exile
- You can't delete a bias — but you can design around it

**Speaker notes:** Here's an idea rarely explained well: many biases aren't flaws, they're insurance policies. The smoke detector principle, from error management theory, says evolution tunes us to over-react — a hundred false alarms are cheaper than one missed predator. That's why a curt email from a boss can feel like a survival threat: to the ancient brain, losing standing in the tribe once meant death. Keep this slide in mind — in our case study we'll watch a real workplace conflict where recognizing this circuitry made the difference between panic and strategy.

## SLIDE 4 · LINGUISTICS

### Pronunciation & Phonology: The Secret Physics of Speech

- Phonemes are categories, not sounds: /r/ vs /l/ exists in English but not in Japanese ears
- The schwa /ə/ — English's most common vowel — is the one learners are never taught
- Connected speech: native speakers say "gonna", "wanna", "nex(t) day" — deletion is the rule, not laziness
- Prosody carries meaning: the melody of a sentence can reverse its words
- A trilingual life = three sound systems: Bangla (no /v/–/bh/ split like English), French (no stress accent!), English (stress-timed)

**Speaker notes:** Phonology is the physics of speech: invisible rules governing what your mouth does. Two rare gems here. First, French is one of the few major languages with no lexical stress — every syllable is roughly equal — which is why French speakers 'sound French' in English: they flatten the stress mountains. Second, the schwa, that lazy 'uh' sound, makes up over 10% of all spoken English vowels, yet textbooks barely mention it. Our case-study subject teaches English for a living and lives in French, with Bangla as his mother tongue — he is a walking experiment in three colliding sound systems.

## SLIDE 5 · ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE

### LLM Fundamentals: A Machine That Learned Language by Predicting It

- One training objective: guess the next token (word-piece) — everything else is emergent
- Embeddings: words become points in space, where 'king − man + woman ≈ queen'
- Attention: every word looks at every other word and asks 'how relevant are you to me?'
- Temperature: a dial between boring-but-safe and creative-but-risky output
- Echo of Slide 2: your brain is also a next-moment prediction engine — LLMs industrialized the trick

**Speaker notes:** Strip away the hype and a large language model does one thing: given text so far, predict the next fragment. The astonishing part is what emerged from that humble task — translation, reasoning, code. Explain embeddings with the famous arithmetic: the direction from 'man' to 'king' is the same as from 'woman' to 'queen' — meaning became geometry. And here's the bridge to earlier slides: predictive processing theory says human brains minimize surprise the same way. When you finish someone's sentence, you're doing next-token prediction with neurons.

## SLIDE 6 · PHYSICS

### Quantum Basics: Reality Is a Probability Until You Ask

- Superposition: a quantum system holds many possibilities at once — until measured
- Entanglement: two particles share one fate, even light-years apart ('spooky action')
- The measurement problem: asking the question changes the answer
- Rare gem — the Quantum Zeno effect: observe a system constantly and it freezes, unable to change
- Quantum tunneling powers the sun — particles pass through walls they 'shouldn't'

**Speaker notes:** Keep this gentle: quantum mechanics says the world at bottom is probabilistic, not clockwork. The crowd-pleaser here is the Quantum Zeno effect — genuinely rare in intro talks: if you measure a quantum system rapidly enough, it never evolves; the watched pot literally never boils. There's a life metaphor hiding in it — constantly checking on a process can prevent it from changing — but be honest with the audience that the metaphor is poetic, not physics. Tunneling is the other wow: without particles sneaking through energy barriers, hydrogen fusion in the sun wouldn't happen and we wouldn't exist.

## SLIDE 7 · PSYCHOLOGY × ECONOMICS

### Why Brands Win: The Science of Being Chosen

- Processing fluency: the brain mistakes 'easy to recognize' for 'good and true'
- Costly signaling: expensive ads work because they're expensive — a flag that says 'we'll still be here tomorrow'
- The availability heuristic: what comes to mind first feels like the best option
- Brands are risk insurance: a known logo compresses a thousand unknowns into one feeling
- Same law everywhere: a brand, an accent, a certification (like Cambridge CELTA) — all are trust shortcuts

**Speaker notes:** Why do people pay double for a logo? Not stupidity — compression. A brand converts an unknowable question ('is this coffee safe, good, consistent?') into a single cached feeling. The rare idea here is costly signaling, borrowed from evolutionary biology: like a peacock's tail, a lavish ad campaign is credible precisely because a fly-by-night company couldn't afford it. Connect it to careers: a Cambridge CELTA certificate works exactly like a brand — it lets a stranger trust 18 years of unverifiable experience in a single line on a CV.

## SLIDE 8 · CASE STUDY, PART I

### The Fight: Illness, a Hostile Boss, and Protocol Over Panic

- The setting: a specialty-coffee boutique on Réunion Island; illness leads to sick leave, then a work-medicine (médecin du travail) verdict of inaptitude
- The conflict: the employer withholds end-of-contract documents — a David-vs-Goliath moment
- The response: no shouting — a formal mise en demeure email, CC'ed to the labour authority (DEETS)
- Every exchange archived and labelled 'POUR ARCHIVE ET LEGAL' — evidence as a habit
- Slides 2 & 3 in action: smoke-detector panic converted into implementation intentions

**Speaker notes:** Now the theory gets a face. In 2025 our subject, working at a café boutique, fell ill; the occupational physician declared him unfit to return, which under French law triggers a protected dismissal process. When the employer dragged its feet on the legally required documents, the ancestral brain screamed 'tribe exile!' — but instead of reacting, he executed protocol: a courteous formal-notice email citing the exact legal obligations, copied to the labour inspectorate, and a personal archive of every exchange. That is Slide 2's lesson — pre-decided rules beat in-the-moment emotion — deployed against a real Goliath.

## SLIDE 9 · CASE STUDY, PART II

### The Reinvention: Three Countries, One Certificate, a New Nationality

- Bangladesh → Sydney: 18 years in Australian luxury hospitality — English absorbed by full immersion
- Sydney → Réunion: marriage, and a naturalization file navigated line by line with the préfecture
- During the legal fight, not after it: enrolled in Cambridge CELTA — turning experience into a 'brand' (Slide 7)
- February 2026: French nationality acquired; 2026: CELTA in hand, applications from Madrid to Bangkok
- The pattern: when one door closes, credential + language = portable capital

**Speaker notes:** Here's the arc that makes the science human. Three sound systems (Slide 4), three cultures, one strategy. The remarkable detail: he signed his CELTA training contract in October 2025 — in the middle of the legal battle, not after it. That's the Zeigarnik effect managed deliberately: open one meaningful loop to shrink the others. By February 2026 he walked out of the préfecture a French citizen, and by summer he was applying to language schools from Madrid to Bangkok. The certificate did what brands do — compressed 18 unverifiable years into one trusted line.
