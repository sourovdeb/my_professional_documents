## Bipolar Type I — What It Actually Means

**Official source:** HAS (Haute Autorité de Santé) — *Recommandations de bonne pratique: Trouble bipolaire* (2015, updated guidance 2021)

Bipolar Type I means you have had at least one full manic episode. Manic episodes can be dangerous (impulsivity, no sleep needed, grandiosity, risky decisions). Depressive episodes are the more frequent end.

**What this means for you day-to-day:**
- Sleep is your most important medication. One bad night can destabilise your mood for days.
- Routine is protective. Not because you lack creativity — because your brain responds to predictability as safety.
- The gap between "doing well" and "episode" is narrower than it looks from outside.
- Alcohol is contraindicated. It disrupts sleep architecture and interacts badly with mood stabilisers.
- Stress — even positive stress — is a trigger. Exciting news can shift you just as much as bad news.

**Red flags to watch for:**
- Needing less than 5 hours sleep and not feeling tired
- Racing thoughts you cannot slow down
- Spending money you don't have
- Starting many projects, finishing none
- Feeling invincible

If you notice three or more of these for two consecutive days, contact Dr. Padovani — don't wait.

## ADHD — How It Overlaps with Bipolar

**Official source:** NICE (UK) Clinical Guideline CG72 — *Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder* | HAS — *Trouble déficit de l'attention avec ou sans hyperactivité (TDAH)*

ADHD and Bipolar coexist in roughly 20% of people with Bipolar diagnoses. They are often confused for each other in adults.

**ADHD symptoms that look like hypomania:**
- Impulsivity
- Racing thoughts
- Starting multiple projects
- Difficulty finishing

**The key difference:** ADHD is chronic and consistent. Hypomania comes in waves, often with reduced sleep.

**What helps you:**
- External structure (timers, alarms, clear task lists) substitutes for the internal structure your executive function doesn't provide naturally.
- Body-doubling: working alongside another person (or a YouTube "study with me" video) can dramatically increase focus.
- Short writing sprints (25 minutes) beat long unfocused sessions.
- One task visible at a time. Not a list of ten.

## Complex PTSD — The Body Keeps the Score

**Official source:** ICD-11 (WHO) — *6B41 Complex post-traumatic stress disorder* | ISTSS (International Society for Traumatic Stress Studies) guidelines

Complex PTSD (C-PTSD) differs from single-incident PTSD. It develops from prolonged, repeated trauma — especially in childhood when you cannot escape. Your brain learned that the world is permanently threatening.

**What you'll notice:**
- Emotional flashbacks: sudden overwhelm, shame, or rage that seems disproportionate to what just happened. The feeling is real; the trigger may be from 30 years ago.
- Hypervigilance: scanning rooms for threats, difficulty relaxing, over-preparing for conflict.
- Dissociation: feeling detached from yourself, the "unscrewing the head" you described. A protection response.
- Difficulty trusting good things: waiting for something to go wrong.

**What helps:**
- Psychoeducation (which you are doing — understanding the mechanisms reduces their power).
- Titrated trauma therapy — not diving straight into the worst memories, but building safety and resources first.
- Regular physical movement, ideally outdoors. The nervous system needs discharge.
- Naming what's happening: "This is a flashback. I am safe now. This feeling is from the past." Out loud, if possible.

## Writing and Your Brain

Writing is therapeutic when it is structured. Unstructured writing about trauma can retraumatise.

**Safe writing practice:**
- Write from a place of distance, not raw immersion. "I remember that I felt..." not diving straight into the feeling.
- Set a time limit (30 minutes) and stop when the timer goes, even mid-sentence.
- Have a grounding ritual after writing about hard material: a hot drink, a walk, music you associate with safety.
- The six writing pillars in this repo are designed to alternate between difficult and lighter material. Do not write about trauma every day.

## Energy Management — The Practical Map

| State | Signs | What to do |
|-------|-------|------------|
| **Stable** | Sleeping 7–8h, motivated, consistent mood | Write the hard pieces. Job hunt. Reach out to contacts. |
| **Elevated** | Sleeping less, many ideas, fast speech | Write shorter pieces. Avoid big decisions. Sleep more deliberately. |
| **Depressed** | Sleeping too much or too little, no motivation, negative self-talk | Write one paragraph. Do not judge it. Tell Dr. Padovani. |
| **Triggered / Dissociating** | Detached, confused, emotional flashback | Stop screen time. Physical anchor (cold water, feet on floor). Call your wife. |
