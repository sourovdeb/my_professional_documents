# Investigative Research Template

## 1. Claim Identification
State the claim or comparison as a neutral question, not a conclusion.

Example:
- What hidden education opportunities exist for EU citizens that are not visible through standard channels?
- How do recent migration law changes affect specific groups of migrants?

## 2. Source Gathering
Follow the Source Hierarchy (prefer higher tiers):

1. Primary: Official documents, government records, court judgments, primary research data
2. Secondary: Peer-reviewed journals, institutional reports, reputable media
3. Tertiary: NGO reports, expert commentary, social media

For each source, document: Title, Author/Organization, Date, URL, Access date, Relevance.

## 3. Conflict of Interest Checklist
For every source used:
- Is the source funded by an organization that benefits from promoting certain outcomes?
- Does the source have political affiliations that might bias the information?
- Are there commercial relationships between the source and entities mentioned?
- Does the author have personal financial or professional stakes in the outcome?
- Has the source been involved in controversies or retractions?

If any answer is YES, disclose and assess impact on credibility.

## 4. Draft the Piece
Use the Micro-blog Template for short-form investigative content:

HEADLINE: Clear, specific, and accurate

LEAD: One-sentence summary that answers So what?

FINDINGS:
- Fact 1 [Source: Author, Year, Publication]
- Fact 2 [Source: Author, Year, Publication]
- Fact 3 [Source: Author, Year, Publication]

FUNDING AND CONFLICT OF INTEREST:
Disclose all funding sources, affiliations, or potential conflicts.

ALTERNATIVE INTERPRETATION:
Present the strongest counter-argument with its evidence.

SOURCES:
1. Full citation with author, year, title, publication
2. Full citation
3. Full citation

## 5. Disclose Verification Limits
At the top of the draft, explicitly state:
- Whether live web search was used
- Which claims rest on pre-cutoff training knowledge vs. verifiable primary records
- Any unverifiable claims (clearly labeled as such)

Example: Live web search was used to verify all sources on July 5, 2026. Claims about historical data are based on official records. No unverifiable claims are presented as confirmed.

## 6. Balance Check
Before finalizing, ensure:
- The piece includes the strongest counter-argument to the main claim
- All sides are represented proportionally to their evidence
- No side is given preferential treatment without justification
- The conclusion follows from the evidence, not the other way around

A piece ending with only one interpretation is incomplete.

## Style Constraints
- Complete grammatically precise sentences
- No contractions
- No inline bold or italic for emphasis
- No informal language
- No emojis
- Formatting reserved for headings and structured data only

## Citation Format
Every factual claim must include: Author/Organization, Year, Publication/Source, Direct link (if verifiable)

Example: Source: European Commission, 2024, Erasmus+ Annual Report

## Final Checklist
- Claim stated as neutral question
- Sources from highest available tier
- COI checklist completed for all sources
- Micro-blog template followed
- Verification limits disclosed
- Alternative interpretations included
- All factual claims cited
- Balance check passed
- Style constraints applied

## Post-Publication
- Monitor for corrections or updates
- Document any challenges or disputes
- Maintain source archive for audit trail
