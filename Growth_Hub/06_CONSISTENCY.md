# 06 — Consistency

**Goal: keep publishing over months — through crash days, hypomania, and insomnia — without relying on willpower.**

This is the part that decides everything. Talent gets you one good piece. Consistency gets you an audience, a reputation, and a job. And consistency is exactly what bipolar + ADHD + insomnia attack hardest.

So the core principle from your Daily Living Strategy carries straight over:

> **The system carries the day the person can't.** Build for the worst version of you, not the best. If a step needs motivation, it is designed wrong.

## Redefine consistency (this is the key move)

Consistency does **not** mean "publish every single day no matter what." That definition guarantees failure on a crash day and then a shame spiral. Redefine it:

> **Consistency = the system runs, even when output is zero.**

A crash day where you do nothing but keep the *rhythm* (no panic, no abandoning the plan) is a successful consistency day. You are protecting the engine, not feeding it.

## The cadence: pick a floor you can hit on a bad week

Not "500 words daily." Start with a promise you can keep at your *worst*, then let good days overflow it:

- **Publish 1 piece per week.** That is the public promise. 52 pieces a year builds a serious body of work.
- **Write most days** when you can, but bank the extras — don't publish them all at once.
- **The larder principle:** in a hyperfocus / elevated window, draft 3–5 pieces and **schedule** them. Those drafts feed the weeks when you produce nothing. This is the single highest-leverage consistency move you have.

## Routed by state (match the task to the day)

| State | Writing instruction |
|-------|---------------------|
| **Elevated / hypomanic** | Draft in bulk, schedule it, then **hard stop** to protect sleep. Stock the larder. Do not impulsively publish raw. |
| **Stable / level** | The full 500-word session. Edit yesterday's drafts. Send outreach. This is when you build the buffer. |
| **Flat / low** | Minimum viable: one paragraph, or just publish a piece already scheduled. A scheduled post going out counts as a publishing day. |
| **Crash / shutdown** | Nothing. The scheduled buffer publishes for you. Anchors only. This is the plan working as designed, not failure. |

## The scaffolds that make it automatic

- **One writing block, same window, timer-run.** Add a `systemd --user` timer or phone alarm for it, like your meds prompt. The alarm starts the session; you don't decide to.
- **Schedule, don't publish live.** Use LinkedIn's and Substack's native schedulers. Write when the energy is there; let the machine release it on time.
- **The buffer is your insurance.** Aim to always have 2–3 finished pieces scheduled ahead. A full buffer means a crash week never breaks your public streak.
- **One log, visible.** `Growth_Hub/PUBLISHED_LOG.md`: date, title, links. Seeing the list grow is the only "streak" that matters — and it survives gaps.

## When you miss (you will — that's built in)

- No "catch-up" binge. You don't owe the missed days anything. Resume at the next scheduled slot.
- No public apology for going quiet. Just publish the next piece. Readers don't track your gaps; they notice your good work.
- A miss is data, not a verdict. If you keep missing the same slot, the slot is wrong — move it, don't blame yourself.

## The one number to watch

Not followers. Not likes. **Pieces published, lifetime.** It only goes up, it ignores bad weeks, and it is the number that actually compounds into exposure, contacts, and work.
