# 02 — Exposure

**Goal: the right people find your work. Not the most people — the right ones (students, schools, recruiters, peers).**

Exposure is not luck. It is *publish where your readers already are* + *make it easy to find* + *show up again*. You control all three.

## Where to publish (official platforms, what each is for)

Pick **two homes**, not seven. More platforms means more upkeep and faster burnout. Based on your goals — credibility, a teaching audience, and jobs — start here:

| Platform | What it is | Best for you | Note |
|----------|-----------|--------------|------|
| **LinkedIn** | Professional network; posts + Articles | Your #1. Recruiters, schools, and teachers live here. Thought leadership turns into job offers and contacts. | Free. Post natively (paste the text in, don't just link out). |
| **Substack** | Newsletter you own; readers subscribe by email | Your home base. You keep the email list — no algorithm can take your audience away. Free; takes 10% only if you ever charge. | substack.com |
| **Medium** | Open blog platform, large built-in readership | Reach beyond your network; good for the long essays. | medium.com — free |
| **Your GitHub repo** | This repository | Already a portfolio. A clean README that links your best pieces *is* exposure to technical and education employers. | Free, you own it. |

**Recommended pair to start:** LinkedIn (reach + jobs) + Substack (an audience you own). Add Medium only when posting feels effortless.

## How people actually find it

1. **Post where they are, in the format they expect.** A LinkedIn post that lives *inside* LinkedIn beats a link to your blog — every algorithm punishes "leave my app" links. Re-share the long version's link in the comments instead.
2. **Title and first line do 80% of the work.** People decide in two seconds. "CELTA: The Gold Standard That's Starting to Rust" works because it is specific and a little provocative. Spend real time here.
3. **Use the words people search.** On Medium/Substack, put the obvious search terms in your title and subtitle ("CELTA", "IELTS", "teaching English in France", "ADHD and learning"). This is honest SEO — describe the thing plainly.
4. **Tags and topics.** Add 3–5 relevant tags on Medium; pick the matching LinkedIn topics. They route your piece to people who follow those subjects.
5. **Engage the first hour.** Reply to every early comment. Platforms read early engagement as "this is good" and show it to more people. This also fits a hyperfocus window — ride it when it comes.

## Your distribution ritual (per published piece)

```
1. Publish the long version on Substack/Medium.
2. Post the native short version on LinkedIn. Link the long one in a comment.
3. Reply to comments for the first hour (or whenever the focus is there).
4. Pin or save the link in the repo README "Writing" section.
5. Log it in /Growth_Hub/PUBLISHED_LOG.md (date, title, links). Done.
```

## What earns exposure honestly (and what to avoid)

- **Earns it:** being genuinely useful, specific, and first-hand. A real teacher's honest take on CELTA cuts through because almost no one else will say it.
- **Earns it:** consistency. The fifth piece outperforms the first because people now recognise you.
- **Avoid:** engagement bait, fake outrage, buying followers, "comment 'YES' for the guide" spam. It draws the wrong crowd and no recruiter respects it. Quality over quantity applies here too.

## Turning exposure into the goal

Exposure is the means; the goal is **jobs and useful contacts**. Every piece should make it obvious who you are and how to reach you:

- LinkedIn headline and Substack "About" page say clearly: *Certified English Trainer (CELTA, Cambridge) — IELTS / TOEIC / Business English*, plus how to book or contact you.
- End relevant pieces with a soft line: *"I train English for [exams/business]. If your team or school needs that, here's how to reach me."*
- Keep the repo README's contact section current.
