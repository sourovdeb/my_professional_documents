# Daily Stability Guide
## Adapted for Bipolar I / ADHD / C-PTSD — Sourov Deb

*Based on the Stability-First Functioning Program. Personalised from lived experience and clinical recommendations.*

---

## The One Rule That Overrides All Others

**Protect the rhythm before the output.**

When the rhythm is stable, output follows. When output comes at the cost of rhythm, both collapse.

---

## Morning (Fixed Time — Same Every Day)

**Wake at the same time regardless of how you slept.** This is the single most evidence-based intervention for bipolar mood stabilisation. Not the most comfortable — the most effective.

1. **Do not check phone for 20 minutes.** The phone activates the stress response before the nervous system is ready. Drink water first.

2. **Light exposure within 30 minutes of waking.** Open the window or step outside. Five minutes of daylight resets the circadian rhythm more reliably than any supplement.

3. **Fixed breakfast.** Same food, same time. Decision fatigue is real. Remove the decision.

4. **One sentence in a log.** How did you sleep (1-10)? How does your mood feel (1-10)? This takes 30 seconds and gives you the data to spot a cycle before it peaks.

5. **Writing window: 30-60 minutes.** Before email. Before admin. The creative brain has its sharpest hour in the first two hours after waking. Protect it.

---

## Mood Monitoring (The Traffic Light System)

**Green** — Baseline mood. Steady energy. Proceed with normal schedule.

**Yellow** — Energy slightly elevated OR slightly flat. Stick rigidly to the routine. Do not extend work hours during elevated energy — this is the danger zone where you feel productive but are actually entering a cycle.

**Red** — Significantly elevated (racing thoughts, little need for sleep, fast speech) OR significantly low (cannot get out of bed, no motivation). Contact your psychiatrist. Do not white-knuckle through a red day alone.

## ADHD Strategies (What Actually Works)

**Body doubling** — Work in a café, a library, or on a video call with another person working. The presence of another person regulates the ADHD nervous system. This is not a preference; it is a neurological reality.

**Implementation intentions** — Do not say "I will write today." Say "At 9am, I will open Obsidian and write 200 words before making coffee." The specificity matters.

**Externalise everything** — Your brain is a processing engine, not a storage unit. Write every task, every appointment, every thought down immediately. Do not trust working memory for anything important.

**Time timers** — Use a visual timer (Time Timer app or physical clock) rather than a countdown. Seeing time passing is more regulating than a buzzer at the end.

**End the task before finishing** — Stop mid-sentence if needed. This makes it easier to re-enter the next day. A finished paragraph is a closed door; an open one invites re-entry.

## Writing Routine (500 Words/Day)

The goal is not inspiration. The goal is showing up.

1. Open Obsidian or your writing app.
2. Set a 25-minute timer (Forest app to lock the phone).
3. Start with a prompt if stuck: *"What do I actually think about ____?"* Fill in anything.
4. Do not edit while writing. Close the editing brain. Editing is for after.
5. 200 words is a good day. 500 is a great day. Zero is still a day you showed up to the desk.

---

## Evening Wind-Down (Fixed Time — 90 Minutes Before Sleep)

1. **Screens off** (or blue-light filter on). The melatonin suppression from screens is real and matters more for bipolar-affected sleep architecture than for neurotypical sleep.

2. **Same wind-down sequence.** Tea, shower, or reading — pick one and make it a ritual. The brain learns from repetition that this sequence means sleep is coming.

3. **Tomorrow's single priority.** Write one sentence: "Tomorrow the most important thing I will do is ____." This offloads the planning anxiety before bed.

4. **Sleep at the same time.** Even if you are not tired. Lie in the dark. The body clock trains from the pattern, not from the feeling.

---

## What to Do When You Break the Routine

You will break the routine. This is not failure. This is bipolar disorder.

The rule is: **return to the routine at the next available anchor point.** Not Monday. Not after the mood passes. At the next meal time, the next morning, the next hour.

The routine is not a standard you failed. It is a boat you can climb back into.
