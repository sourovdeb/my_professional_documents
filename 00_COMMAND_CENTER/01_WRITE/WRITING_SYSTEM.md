# The Writing System — a daily 500-word habit you can actually keep

*Quality over quantity. One short, finished thing beats ten brilliant fragments.*

You don't have a writing problem. You have a *finishing* problem — the autoethnography names it: avoidance of finishing, fear that a capable reader will see the weakness. So this system is built to defeat the one enemy: the unfinished draft. The rule is brutal and kind at the same time — **500 words, then stop.** Small enough to finish on a bad day. Done is the whole point.

---

## The daily 25-minute loop

1. **Open the template.** Copy `drafts/_TEMPLATE.md` to `drafts/YYYY-MM-DD_slug.md`. (Or run `new_draft.sh` in `03_TOOLS/`.)
2. **Pick one topic** from `TOPIC_BANK.md`. Don't choose — take the top one that pulls you. Choosing is procrastination.
3. **Set a 25-minute timer.** The timer is the boss, not your sense of time.
4. **Write 500 words. Do not edit while writing.** Editing and drafting are different brains; never run them at once. If you stall, write *"what I mean is…"* and keep going.
5. **Stop at the timer even mid-sentence.** Stopping mid-thought makes tomorrow's start easy (Hemingway's trick — it works).
6. **Save. Done.** Editing happens on a *different* day. Capture the next thought in `06_IDEAS/`.

**Crash-day version:** write three honest sentences about how today feels, save it, close the laptop. Streak intact. The habit is the asset; the words are the interest.

---

## What "500 words, quality" actually looks like

A good 500-word essay does **one** job: it takes the reader from a concrete moment to a single idea they didn't have before. Structure that never fails:

1. **The hook (1–2 sentences).** A specific image or line. *"The tutor told me to get off my high horse. I held the tears until the room emptied."* Concrete beats abstract every time.
2. **The turn (the middle).** What it meant. The thinking. This is where your forensic mind earns its keep — name the pattern, not just the wound.
3. **The landing (last 2–3 sentences).** One idea the reader keeps. Earn it; don't decorate it.

Cut the throat-clearing. Delete "I think that," "in my opinion," "it is important to note." Start at the second paragraph — your real first line is usually buried there.

---

## Your voice — the rules (active, human, never monotone)

- **Active voice.** *"The centre changed the grade,"* not *"the grade was changed."* Subjects do things.
- **Short sentences carry weight. Long ones carry music. Alternate them** — that's how you avoid monotone.
- **Concrete nouns, strong verbs, almost no adverbs.** "He broke promises rather than bones" needs no adverb.
- **One idea per paragraph.** White space is a feature, not a gap.
- **Write like a person, not a brand.** Contractions are fine. A blunt line is fine. Your power is that you've lived this, not that you sound polished.
- **End on something earned.** No fake uplift. Readers trust a writer who doesn't lie to make them feel better.

## Five essay shapes you can reuse forever

Rotate these so the blog never feels samey:

1. **The scene → the lesson.** One memory, one insight. (Your default, and your strongest.)
2. **The reframe.** "Everyone thinks X about [ADHD / failure / institutions]. Here's what it's actually like."
3. **The how-to from the inside.** "How I built a day that survives a crash." Lived expertise, given away.
4. **The short argument.** One claim, three reasons, one consequence. (Use for the CELTA/rights pieces.)
5. **The letter.** "To my 17-year-old self." Direct address. Devastating when honest.

---

## The editing pass (separate day, separate brain)

When you return to a draft, do three quick passes, in order — never all at once:

1. **Cut 10%.** Every draft is 10% too long. Kill the weakest sentence in each paragraph.
2. **Read it aloud.** If you stumble, the sentence is wrong. Your ear is a better editor than your eye.
3. **Run LanguageTool** (free, in `03_TOOLS/`). Fix grammar last, never first.

Then mark it `STATUS: ready` and move it to the WordPress workflow. Three ready drafts at a time is plenty — publish on a schedule, not in a binge.
