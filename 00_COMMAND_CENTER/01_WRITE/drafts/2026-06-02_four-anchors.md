# The Four Anchors: How I Run a Day That Survives a Crash

Bipolar crashes are not a mystery once you name them. You wake heavy. Every task looks impossible. The only rational thing is to stop moving. And if you believe that feeling, you will.

I used to believe it. I'd crawl back to bed, skip the meds, skip the meal, tell myself I'd try again tomorrow. Tomorrow came the same way. The crash would hold for a week, two weeks — and by the time it released, the unread emails had piled into something I didn't know how to face.

Until my psychiatrist asked: what if the day isn't supposed to feel good? What if the day isn't supposed to feel ready? What if the day is just supposed to happen?

That changed everything.

Now I have four things that happen, every single day, no matter how heavy the morning is. I call them anchors. They're so small they feel pointless on a good day — almost insultingly easy. On a crash day, they are the only thing that keeps the day from sinking entirely.

**The fixed wake time** — same time every day, 7 days a week, alarm out of reach. Not because I need eight hours; not because motivation is strong. Because your brain's circadian rhythm is the single best regulator of bipolar mood, and the rhythm needs a fixed point. Missing wake time by two hours can trigger or worsen a crash. No exception, ever. The alarm lives in the kitchen, not the bedroom.

**Meds, water, daylight** — on waking. Medication first, before any decision is required. A full glass of water, because dehydration is a hidden mood destabiliser. And light on the eyes — open the blinds, stand outside if you can — because light timing controls the circadian system that controls your mood. This takes five minutes. It costs almost nothing. It works.

**One real meal at a roughly fixed time** — not a snack, a meal. Skipping a meal doesn't mean you lose calories; it means you lose a rhythm cue, and for a bipolar brain, rhythm is medication. On a crash day when nothing tastes like anything, I don't care what the meal is — a scrambled egg, a tin of beans, pasta with olive oil. The point is the hour, the rhythm, the sitting down.

**One line of capture** — *"Today I am ___. Today the one thing is ___."* Not a to-do list; that's for a different brain state. One sentence. Written, not thought. It names the state so I can route it — on a crash day, it might be *"Today I am heavy. Today the one thing is to shower and eat lunch."* On a high day, it might be *"Today I feel fast. Today the one thing is to write, not to spend money."* The routine tells me what this particular brain is capable of, and I match the task to that truth instead of forcing the same tasks on every weather.

If those four things happen, the day counts. Completely. Nothing else is required. I did not solve bipolar. I did not have a good day. I just survived the day while bipolar was happening — and that is exactly enough.

The worst days still come. But now I know: the day was never supposed to be good. It was just supposed to happen. And these four small anchors are what make the happening possible.
