# The Forensic Auditor

A tutor once admitted, in writing, that she had changed one of my assessment comments after the fact. Then she called it a typo.

For most people that's an annoyance. For me it was the oldest wound in the world, wearing a lanyard. I grew up in a house where authority rewrote reality at will — where what happened to you on Monday could be denied by Friday, and the denial always won, because the people doing the denying were the people in charge. A child has no record. A child only has his word, and his word is the first thing they take.

So when the institution changed the comment and shrugged, something in me did not collapse the way it would have at seventeen. It went cold and clear instead. I thought: *welcome to 2026, when information is the democracy.* I have the emails. I have the dates. I have the policy documents and the timestamps. For the first time in my life, the person being told he imagined it had the receipts.

I gave the role a name. The Forensic Auditor.

Here is what I want to be precise about, because it's the part people misunderstand. The goal was never to make them admit they were wrong. I'm not waiting for an apology. An apology is something they hand down, and I am done receiving my reality from people above me. The goal was to build a record so complete, so dated and sourced and undeniable, that I could close the file and walk away knowing one thing for certain: *they did not get to rewrite it this time.*

That's the whole move. Not revenge. Not even justice, necessarily — the appeal may win or it may not. The function is older than the outcome. I am proving to my younger self that his reality can never be erased again. Every document I file, I file for the boy who carried water up three flights and was told, afterward, that it never cost him anything.

You can do this too, and you don't need a tribunal. You need a habit. Write down what happened, with the date, while it's fresh. Keep the email; don't just read it. Save the version before they edited it. When someone tells you that you're imagining the thing you watched happen, you don't have to argue — you have a file. The argument was never winnable anyway. The record is.

Documentation is how the powerless stop being gaslit. It is the one weapon that doesn't require permission, doesn't require you to be believed, and doesn't run out.

They can refuse to apologise. They cannot refuse to have been recorded.

So I keep the file. And I fire them, quietly, as the judges of my worth.
