# ADHD and the Last-Minute Submission: A Love Story

I have submitted exactly one assignment early in my entire life. A high-school essay I wrote at four in the morning, not out of diligence, but because I'd accidentally woken up and my brain was already running hot. I submitted it at 6 a.m. for a noon deadline. It got a B.

I've never recovered from that disappointment.

For forty years, the narrative has been: *"If you had just started earlier, you would get an A."* Teachers said it. Parents said it. I said it to myself. The only problem with that narrative is that it is completely, totally wrong.

Here is what ADHD actually is: it is not laziness or deficiency of character. It is a fundamental difference in how attention works. I don't struggle with the *work*. I struggle with the *initiation* — walking to the door, opening the laptop, writing the first sentence. Once the thing has started, I am often brilliant. My CELTA applications were a mess because I sent them last-minute, but the actual teaching, the improvisation, the attention to a student in the room — that I did well.

The neuroscientist William Dodson calls it **interest-based attention**. My attention doesn't respond to deadlines, importance, or good intentions. It responds to novelty, urgency, and genuine interest. A problem becomes interesting *only when it becomes urgent.* Before the deadline turns the alarm on, the work is not in focus — not because I don't care, but because the attention system cannot *fire* before the pressure fires it. It's not a choice. It's wiring.

So I've spent forty years being told I would be brilliant if I would start earlier. And I've spent forty years starting on the last day, proving I'm brilliant while the instruction — *START EARLIER* — goes in one ear and bounces off the solid wall of neurology it cannot move.

Here's what changed: I stopped listening to the instruction and started listening to the wiring.

On the CELTA, when I knew the assignment was due, I didn't try to force early starts. Instead, I accepted the physics: the work would begin 48–72 hours before the deadline, when my attention would fire. I moved *all other obligations out of the way* so that, when the pressure came, I could actually sit down. I didn't fight the wiring; I built a life around it.

Did I get perfect scores? No. Did I get *finished*, with actual thoughts in the thing rather than a panic-written mess? Yes. And for the first time, the late submission wasn't a failure — it was the only way the attention system I actually have could produce coherent work.

If I ever have a child with ADHD, I will not spend their childhood telling them to start earlier. I will tell them: *"Your attention works differently. That is not wrong. Here is how to build a life around how it actually works."* And I will mean it.

The A I'm chasing will never come from starting earlier. It will come from understanding that the last-minute submission is not a flaw in my productivity — it's a feature of how my brain is built. And once I accept that, the shame goes away, and something else becomes possible: doing the work at the speed that the work requires.
