# If I'm Being Funny, It's Because I'm Stressed

A tutor once told me I needed to "get off my high horse" during a live teaching observation. My teaching, she said, was vibrant and likeable but lacked "educational value." A colleague said I "had no idea what I was talking about." In the moment, in front of students, I held back tears and said nothing.

Afterward, I cried hard for a long time.

In therapy, I mentioned this in passing: *"I must have been arrogant in there. I was being so funny and confident."* My therapist stopped me cold. "That wasn't arrogance," she said. "That was masking. When you're stressed, your nervous system reaches for humour because it's the only survival tool that worked when you were a child."

I'd never thought of it that way. The vibrant teaching wasn't a flaw — it was a reflex. A self-protection I'd learned so young it felt like personality.

Here's the pattern: a child in a house where conflict is constant and authority is unpredictable learns that invisibility is dangerous. Being quiet makes you a target. Being small makes you obvious. But if you make people laugh, if you take the energy in the room and redirect it toward something light, you might — *might* — avoid the blow. So you become the funny one. You deflect with humour. You disarm the room with charm.

By the time you're grown, it's so automatic you can't turn it off. Someone asks a hard question → a joke fires. You're scared → you smile. The pressure rises → you become funnier. And the people around you read it as confidence. They read it as arrogance. They don't read it as *fear being expressed in the only language you learned.*

I spent thirty years being told I was charming and quick-witted and not realizing I was in constant terror, translated into jokes.

The morning of that observation, I woke anxious. All day, the nervous system was lit. When the tutor began the feedback, the threat-detection system I've had since childhood screamed a single message: *protect yourself.* And the only protection tool I had was charm. So I made jokes. I smiled. I lightened the room.

And everyone, including me, mistook survival for arrogance.

The reframe matters not because it gets me off the hook — I could have handled the feedback better, prepared more. The reframe matters because it explains why my defence system reached for humour instead of listening. It wasn't a choice. It was a child's wisdom, woven so deep into the nervous system it came out as personality.

Now, when I notice I'm being funny, I pause. *What am I protecting?* Usually it's fear. Fear of not being good enough. Fear of being exposed. Fear of the authority figure finding out I'm not what they think.

The funny is still there — that's not going away, and I don't want it to. But now I can notice when it's *me* and when it's the frightened eight-year-old borrowing my mouth to survive another room full of judgment.

That distinction changes everything.
