3. Conclusion
As Sourov stated in his exchange with the centre (document 7 listed above) his appeal ‘is not to assign blame 
but to understand the decision’ and ensure that every aspect of his performance on the course has been 
considered fairly. His appeal centres around three main questions:
1. Why was his final recommended grade recorded as ‘fail’ when his last lesson (TP 8) was graded as 
‘to standard’? 
2. What was the process that led to his final recommended grade?
3. Who was involved in the discussions that led to this decision?
Having taught three ‘to standard’ lessons (TPs 1, 3 and 4) and one ‘below standard’ lesson (TP 2) in the first 
half of the course, Sourov was graded, as ‘to standard for this stage of the course’ by his tutor at the Stage 
Two Progress Record. However, both his TP 5 and TP 6 were graded as ‘below standard’. At this point (27th
January 2026), Sourov had a Stage Three Progress Record where he was graded as ‘not to standard for this 
stage and needs more work in order to pass the course’. The following 13 criteria were identified as ‘not to 
standard’:
Planning – 4e/4i/4j/4k/4l
Teaching – 2a/2e/2g/3a/5c/5d/5f/5h
As part of ‘the summary of tutorial and action points’ box the tutor wrote ‘In your final two lessons, all the above 
criteria in this box need to be to standard’. Sourov’s TP 7 was graded as ‘below standard’. Of the 13 criteria 
listed as ‘not to standard’ in the Stage Three Progress Record, only one (4i) was recorded on the TP 7 feedback 
sheet as ‘now to standard’. Given that TP 7 was a receptive skills lesson, criterion 2g was referenced as ‘not 
covered in this lesson’. The other 11 criteria were all noted as ‘not met’. For criteria 5f and 5g the tutor noted 
‘some progress’ indicating that Sourov’s effectiveness in these areas had moved forward since TPs 5 and 6, 
but, overall, improvement was still needed to meet the required standard.
On 31st January 2026 the tutors issued Sourov with a warning letter to inform him that after TP 7 he was not 
at the standard required to pass the course. The letter listed 12 criteria which were not to standard at the Stage 
Three Progress Record tutorial and, following TP 7, remained not to standard. The omission of criterion 4i from 
the warning letter reflected that this criterion had been recorded as ‘now to standard’ in the tutor’s written 
feedback to TP 7. The warning letter also clarified that ‘to have a possibility of passing the course, you will 
need to pass your final lesson, TP 8. In addition, all the above criteria need to be to standard’. 
In the written feedback sheet for TP 8, Sourov’s final assessed lesson, the 4 planning criteria noted as ‘not 
met’ in TP 7 (4e/4j/4k/4l) were all highlighted as met. The teaching criteria 2a, 2e, 5d and 5h, which were 
highlighted as ‘not met’ in TP 7 and criterion 2g (which was not applicable in that lesson) were also recorded 
as ‘met’. Noting the ‘significant progress on key areas’, the tutor graded this lesson as ‘to standard’. However, 
in the written feedback to TP 8, three criteria (3a, 5c and 5f) were recorded as ‘not met. Again, the tutor 
recognised progress compared to previous lessons but not of a sufficient level to mark these criteria as ‘met’. 
Therefore, three of the criteria highlighted as ‘not to standard’ in the Stage Three Progress Record (3a, 5c and 
5f) on 27th January 2026 and then included in the warning letter issued on 31st January 2026 were ‘not met’ in 
either TP 7 or TP 8 (it’s worth noting that criterion 5f was also graded as ‘not to standard’ by the tutor at the 
Stage Two Progress Record). The tutors, along with the course assessor, discussed the final recommended 
grade. Having reviewed all the assessed components of the course, they concluded that whilst Sourov had 
passed all four written assignments and made some progress in his final lesson, this was not sufficient to 
achieve an overall Pass grade.
