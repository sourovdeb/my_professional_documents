## Slide 2: The Psychology of Productivity - Flow States and Deep Work
**Title:** The Neuroscience of Peak Performance

**Bullet Points:**
- Flow state: Optimal experience where skills match challenge (Csikszentmihalyi)
- Deep Work: 4-hour blocks of uninterrupted, focused cognitive effort (Cal Newport)
- The 90-Minute Ultradian Rhythm: Natural attention cycles in human biology
- Dopamine's role: Reward prediction error drives motivation and learning
- ADHD and productivity: The paradox of hyperfocus in neurodivergent minds

**Speaker Notes:**
The human brain operates in rhythms. Research shows we have natural 90-minute cycles of peak focus. Flow states occur when challenge slightly exceeds skill—creating that 'in the zone' feeling. For those with ADHD, like our case study subject, the brain can achieve hyperfocus on stimulating tasks, but struggles with sustained attention on mundane ones. Understanding these psychological patterns helps us design better work protocols.

## Slide 3: Pronunciation and Phonology - The Hidden Architecture of Language
**Title:** The Physics of Speech: How We Shape Sound

**Bullet Points:**
- Phonemes: The smallest units of sound that distinguish meaning (/p/ vs /b/ in "pat" vs "bat")
- Formants: Resonant frequencies that define vowel qualities (F1, F2, F3)
- The Motor Theory of Speech Perception: We perceive speech by mentally simulating articulation
- Prosody: The melody of language—stress, rhythm, intonation that convey emotion
- Accent and identity: How pronunciation shapes social perception and belonging

**Speaker Notes:**
Language is physics made audible. When we speak, we're sculpting airflow into distinct sound patterns. The formants—resonant frequencies in our vocal tract—are what make an /i/ sound different from an /a/. Interestingly, our brains don't just hear speech; they mentally reproduce the movements needed to make those sounds. This motor theory explains why we can understand accented speech, and why learning pronunciation involves physical as much as auditory training.

## Slide 4: Evolutionary Bias - Why We Believe What We Believe
**Title:** Cognitive Biases: The Brain's Shortcuts and Their Consequences

**Bullet Points:**
- Confirmation bias: Seeking information that confirms pre-existing beliefs
- Negativity bias: Evolutionary advantage of paying more attention to threats
- The Dunning-Kruger effect: Why incompetence masks itself
- In-group bias: The tribal origins of modern polarization
- The "Just World" fallacy: Believing the world is fair to reduce cognitive dissonance
- System justification: Why oppressed groups sometimes defend their oppressors

**Speaker Notes:**
Our brains evolved to make quick decisions in dangerous environments. The negativity bias kept our ancestors alive—missing a threat could be fatal, but missing an opportunity was just a missed meal. Today, this means we remember negative news more vividly. Confirmation bias served our tribal ancestors by reinforcing group cohesion. But in the modern world, these evolutionary shortcuts can lead to polarization, poor decisions, and systemic injustices—like the discrimination faced by our case study subject.

## Slide 5: LLM Fundamentals - How AI Learns Language
**Title:** From Statistics to Understanding: The LLM Revolution

**Bullet Points:**
- Transformer architecture: Self-attention mechanisms that weigh word relationships
- Tokenization: Breaking language into ~50,000 piece fragments (subword units)
- The next-word prediction task: Autocomplete as a path to general knowledge
- Emergent abilities: How scale (parameters, data) creates unexpected capabilities
- The "Stochastic Parrot" debate: Do LLMs understand or just mimic human language?
- Alignment: Fine-tuning to make AI helpful, honest, and harmless

**Speaker Notes:**
Large Language Models don't understand language the way humans do. They predict the next word in a sequence based on statistical patterns learned from vast amounts of text. Yet, at scale, something remarkable emerges—abilities like translation, reasoning, and even simple coding that weren't explicitly programmed. The debate continues: are these models truly understanding, or are they just extremely sophisticated pattern matchers? Either way, they're transforming how we interact with information.

## Slide 6: Quantum Physics Basics - Reality at Its Most Fundamental
**Title:** The Quantum World: Where Certainty Dissolves

**Bullet Points:**
- Wave-particle duality: Light and matter exist as both particle and wave (double-slit experiment)
- Superposition: Quantum systems exist in multiple states until measured (Schrödinger's cat)
- Entanglement: "Spooky action at a distance"—particles instantaneously influence each other
- The measurement problem: Observation collapses the wave function
- Quantum tunneling: Particles passing through barriers—explains nuclear fusion in stars
- Many-worlds interpretation: Every quantum possibility becomes a separate universe

**Speaker Notes:**
At the smallest scales, reality behaves nothing like our everyday experience. Particles don't have definite positions until measured. They can be in multiple places at once—a state called superposition. And when particles become entangled, measuring one instantly determines the state of another, no matter the distance. This isn't just philosophy; it's the foundation of modern electronics, cryptography, and may one day enable quantum computing that solves problems impossible for classical computers.

## Slide 7: Case Study - Sourov's Journey: From Bangladesh to France
**Title:** One Man's Fight for Recognition in a Complex World

**Bullet Points:**
- Born in Bangladesh, educated in Australia (Accounting diploma from Sydney)
- 18 years in anglophone environments, fluent in English and French
- Settled in Reunion Island, building a new life with family
- CELTA certification journey: Exposed systemic discrimination
- ADHD and depression: Recognized disabilities (ALD status in France)
- Legal battle: Fighting for accommodations and justice against institutional bias
- Naturalization process: Navigating French bureaucracy with spouse and daughter

**Speaker Notes:**
Sourov Deb's story is a microcosm of our interconnected world. Born in Bangladesh, educated in Australia's anglophone environment, he now lives in Reunion Island—a French territory in the Indian Ocean. His journey to become a CELTA-certified English teacher revealed systemic issues: despite disclosing his ADHD and depression (medically recognized disabilities), the training institution failed to provide required accommodations. His fight for justice exposes how evolutionary biases, institutional structures, and cognitive patterns can create barriers for neurodivergent individuals.

**Speaker Notes:**
