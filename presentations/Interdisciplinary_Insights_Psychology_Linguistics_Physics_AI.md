- **Risk aversion vs. human cost**: The economics of visa processing
- **Institutional memory**: Why bureaucracies resist change

# Slide 5: LLM Fundamentals Through the Lens of Justice
## How AI Systems Mirror Human Bias

- **Training data reflection**: LLMs inherit biases from their source material
- **The "reasonable accommodation" problem**: Can AI systems be forced to adapt?
- **Adversarial prompts**: How users with disabilities must "hack" AI tools
- **The transparency paradox**: We demand AI explainability but accept human opacity

**Speaker Notes**: 
Large Language Models face the same accommodation challenges as human institutions. Just as The ELT Hub refused to modify its assessment methods for Sourov's ADHD, early AI systems often couldn't adapt their responses for users with different cognitive styles. The difference: we can legally compel human institutions to change (under laws like France's Loi 2005-102 and UK's Equality Act 2010). Can we do the same for AI? The visa system's opacity mirrors black-box AI: both make life-altering decisions with little explanation or appeal process.

- **Observer effect**: How filing a complaint changes the system's behavior
- **Decoherence**: When legal systems collapse into arbitrary outcomes

**Speaker Notes**: 
Sourov's legal battles demonstrate quantum principles in action. He existed in a superposition: a highly qualified professional (18 years experience, CELTA-certified) AND a person with disabilities requiring accommodations. His complaints created entanglement: French labor law (Code du travail L.5213-6), UK Equality Act 2010, Cambridge Assessment English regulations, and Qualiopi certification standards all became linked. The observer effect was clear - once he filed complaints, The ELT Hub, Qualitia Certification, and even the French Embassy changed their behavior. # Slide 10: Synthesis & Rarely-Discussed Connections
## What This Case Study Reveals About Human Systems

- **The Accommodation Paradox**: Systems that teach inclusion often practice exclusion
- **Bias as Feature, Not Bug**: Evolutionary advantages of risk-averse systems
- **Quantum Justice**: Legal outcomes exist in superposition until observed
- **The AI Mirror**: Our biases become visible when reflected in machine behavior
- **The Advocate's Burden**: Systemic change requires individual sacrifice

**Speaker Notes**: 
This case study reveals five rarely-discussed truths about human systems. First, the accommodation paradox: institutions that teach inclusion (like ELT teacher training programs) often practice exclusion when it comes to their own students with disabilities. Second, bias is often a feature, not a bug - risk-averse systems like visa processing evolve to exclude, and this has evolutionary advantages for the system, if not for individuals. Third, quantum justice: legal outcomes exist in superposition until a judge or authority observes them. Fourth, AI mirrors our biases back to us, making visible what we accept in human systems. Finally, the advocate's burden: real systemic change requires individuals to sacrifice their health, time, and resources. Sourov's story is one of resilience, but it's also a cautionary tale about the cost of fighting for justice.
