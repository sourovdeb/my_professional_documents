# Productivity Protocols in Psychology 

- **Flow State Theory**: Optimal performance when challenge matches skill
- **Eisenhower Matrix**: Urgent vs. Important task prioritization
- **Cognitive Load Theory**: Working memory has limited capacity (Sweller, 1988)
- **Pomodoro Technique**: 25-minute focused work intervals
- **Deep Work**: Cal Newport's framework for distraction-free concentration

**Speaker Notes**: These protocols share a common principle: managing attention and cognitive resources. Flow states occur when tasks are 4% beyond current ability. The Eisenhower Matrix helps avoid the "urgency trap" where important but non-urgent work gets neglected. Cognitive load theory explains why multimedia learning works best when visual and auditory information complement rather than duplicate each other.

# Pronunciation and Phonology: The Science of Speech 

- **Articulatory Phonetics**: How speech organs produce sounds (lips, tongue, vocal cords)
- **Acoustic Phonetics**: Physical properties of sound waves (frequency, amplitude, duration)
- **Phonology**: Sound patterns and systems in language
- **IPA (International Phonetic Alphabet)**: Universal transcription system
- **Cross-linguistic Variations**: Why "th" sounds don't exist in many languages

**Speaker Notes**: The production of the /θ/ sound in "think" requires the tongue tip to protrude between the teeth - a position that doesn't occur in many languages, making it difficult for non-native speakers. Phonology explains why English speakers struggle with French nasal vowels, while French speakers find English word stress patterns challenging. The IPA provides a precise way to transcribe any sound in any language.

# Evolutionary Bias in Human Behavior 

- **Confirmation Bias**: Preference for information that confirms existing beliefs
- **Loss Aversion**: Fear of losses is psychologically twice as powerful as potential gains (Kahneman & Tversky)
- **Tribality**: Evolutionary tendency to form and favor in-groups
- **Negativity Bias**: Negative experiences have greater impact than positive ones
- **Dunbar's Number**: Cognitive limit to ~150 stable social relationships

**Speaker Notes**: These biases evolved as survival mechanisms. Confirmation bias helped our ancestors quickly identify threats in their environment. Loss aversion made sense when resources were scarce - losing your food supply could be fatal. The negativity bias ensured we remembered dangerous situations. However, in modern contexts, these same biases can lead to poor decision-making, from investment choices to workplace conflicts.

# Large Language Models: Fundamentals 

- **Transformer Architecture**: Self-attention mechanisms process sequential data in parallel
- **Tokenization**: Text broken into subword units (average: 4 characters per token)
- **Context Window**: Limited memory of previous tokens (typically 4K-128K tokens)
- **Training Phases**: Pre-training on vast datasets, then fine-tuning for specific tasks
- **Emergent Abilities**: Unexpected capabilities arise at scale (200B+ parameters)

**Speaker Notes**: The transformer architecture, introduced in 2017, revolutionized NLP by allowing models to weigh the importance of different words when processing each word. Unlike humans who read sequentially, transformers can look at all words in a sentence simultaneously. Tokenization explains why LLMs sometimes struggle with rare words - they're broken into multiple tokens. The context window limitation is why LLMs "forget" information from earlier in long conversations.

# Quantum Physics: Counterintuitive Reality 

- **Wave-Particle Duality**: Light and matter exhibit both wave and particle properties
- **Superposition**: Quantum systems exist in multiple states simultaneously until measured
- **Entanglement**: Particles remain connected regardless of distance (Einstein's "spooky action")
- **Uncertainty Principle**: Cannot simultaneously know position and momentum with precision
- **Quantum Tunneling**: Particles can pass through energy barriers

**Speaker Notes**: Quantum mechanics challenges our classical intuition. In superposition, an electron in an atom doesn't have a definite position until we measure it - it exists as a probability cloud. Entanglement means measuring one particle instantly determines the state of its partner, even light-years away. The uncertainty principle isn't about measurement limitations but a fundamental property of nature. Quantum tunneling explains nuclear fusion in stars and is the basis for flash memory technology.

# Health, Disability, and Systemic Barriers 

- **ALD Recognition**: Long-term condition status provides medical coverage and protections
- **Documentation as Power**: Medical records, prescriptions, and official letters create evidence trails
- **The Invisibility Problem**: Mental health disabilities often lack visible markers
- **Accommodation Types**: Extended time, alternative formats, additional support, flexible deadlines
- **Systemic Failure**: Even with legal requirements, compliance often requires enforcement

**Speaker Notes**: The user's experience reveals a common pattern: disabilities that aren't visibly apparent face greater skepticism. ADHD and depression, while recognized as ALD in France, don't come with obvious physical markers. This leads to situations where accommodations that would be automatically granted for a wheelchair user require extensive justification and advocacy for mental health conditions.

# Why Brands Become Popular: The Psychology of Preference 

- **Mere Exposure Effect**: Repeated exposure increases liking (Zajonc, 1968)
- **Social Proof**: We prefer what others prefer (Cialdini's influence principle)
- **Scarcity Principle**: Limited availability increases perceived value
- **Storytelling**: Brands with compelling narratives create emotional connections
- **Consistency Bias**: We favor brands that align with our self-image

**Speaker Notes**: These psychological principles explain viral phenomena. The mere exposure effect is why advertising works - we don't need to pay attention to ads for them to influence us. Social proof drives trends: when we see others adopting something, we assume it's good. Scarcity creates urgency, which is why limited editions sell out quickly. The most successful brands don't sell products; they sell identities and stories we want to be part of.
