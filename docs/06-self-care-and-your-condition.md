# Self-Care & Learning About Your Condition

This is the foundation everything else rests on. Output is not the point; a life
you can live is. The whole system in this repo bends around your energy on
purpose.

> **Important:** This guide is general energy-management information, not medical
> advice and not a diagnosis. Personalize it with your own clinician, who knows
> your specific condition. Where this guide and your body disagree, your body wins.

## Pacing: the one skill worth learning first

Pacing means matching activity to the energy you actually have — not pushing on
good days until you crash, then losing the days after. It is the most evidence-
backed self-management approach for fatigue and many chronic conditions.

The core moves, drawn from reputable clinical sources:

1. **Plan, prioritize, and pace.** Decide what matters before energy is spent.
2. **Break tasks into smaller pieces** with rest *between* them — rest *before*
   you're exhausted, not after.
3. **Alternate task types** (sitting/standing, screen/no-screen, hard/easy).
4. **Stop while you still have something left.** The urge to "finish while I feel
   good" is the trap that causes the crash.
5. **Notice your early warning signs** and treat them as a full stop, not a
   suggestion.

### Official, free sources to learn pacing properly
- **Cleveland Clinic — Energy Management & Activity Pacing:**
  https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/articles/energy-management-and-conservation-activity-pacing-for-chronic-pain-and-fatigue
- **ME Association — Pacing guide (PDF):**
  https://meassociation.org.uk/me-cfs-information/living-with-me-cfs/pacing-for-people-with-me/
- **National MS Society — Pacing to manage fatigue:**
  https://www.nationalmssociety.org/managing-ms/living-with-ms/diet-exercise-and-wellness/fatigue/pacing
- **Versus Arthritis — Spoon Theory explained:**
  https://www.versusarthritis.org/news/2023/march/what-is-the-spoon-theory-help-and-resources/

For anything specific to your diagnosis, start from an **official source for that
exact condition** (your national health service, a recognized patient charity, or
peer-reviewed guidance) rather than general blogs or forums.

## Spoon Theory, in one line

You start each day with a limited number of "spoons" (units of energy). Every
task spends some. You can't borrow from tomorrow without paying interest. Spend on
what matters; protect the rest.

## A health-first daily floor

- **Sleep is productivity.** Protect it before you protect output.
- **One thing a day is a complete day.** See the two-list method in
  [docs/04](04-consistency-and-productivity.md).
- **Rest is part of the work, not a failure of it.** Schedule it like a task.
- **Warm screen at night** (f.lux), **dictation on low days**, **timer for
  pacing.** Tools are in [docs/02](02-tools-write-and-design.md).

## When to get human help

If symptoms change, worsen, or frighten you — or if low mood becomes persistent —
contact your clinician. A repo can organize a life; it can't replace care. Reaching
out is a strength move, not a weakness.

## How this connects to the writing

Your condition is not separate from your work — it's part of your story, and that
story is exactly what makes your writing yours. Written honestly (on your terms,
only what you choose to share), it's the thing no one else can copy. Pace it like
everything else.
