# I Failed My Teaching Exam. Then They Rewrote the Evidence.

In January 2026 a teacher told me to get off my high horse.

She said it in front of my peers. In front of the students I was teaching. I held back tears, said nothing, and cried alone for a long time afterward. She was my Cambridge CELTA tutor. I was fifty days into a course I had chosen because I wanted to change my life.

I had disclosed my ADHD in writing. I had completed every assignment. I had turned up to every one of the 120 required hours.

What happened next is not, actually, about her.

Within 72 hours of that feedback session, the assessment record changed. A comment she had written — one that supported my progress — became a “typo,” retroactively removed and replaced with the opposite. I have the original. I have the timestamp. I have the email where she called it a typo. Both documents exist. They say different things.

I am not asking you to believe me. I am asking you to read the record.

Here is what I know about myself: I am the child who never told. Every time something was done to me — in school, at home, in the spaces children are supposed to be safe — I calculated that speaking would add more problems to the pile. I learned to absorb. I learned to minimise. When I disclosed my ADHD to Cambridge, I added the words: “this is not to ask for any special consideration.” I wrote that. I genuinely believed it was the right thing to say.

It was not a waiver. It was the trauma speaking — decades of conditioning telling me to make myself smaller so people would not find me inconvenient.

The law does not care about that disclaimer. The institution’s duty triggered the moment they received the disclosure, not the moment I made the “correct” request.

I failed the course. I filed an appeal. Three procedural errors were confirmed at Stage One. I filed a complaint with Ofqual. Reference: SJ3XP35D.

But here is what I need you to understand: this is not a victim’s story.

I call it a forensic audit. I stopped asking the institution to confirm my reality. I started building a record so solid that reality needs no outside confirmation. Data. Screenshots. Timestamps. Regulatory citations. A blog that exists on a server I control, indexed by search engines I do not control, readable by anyone who types the right words.

Welcome to 2026. Information is the democracy.

I am not writing this because I need Cambridge to apologise. I am writing this because I spent forty years being taught — by parents, by abusers, by institutions — that my perception of events was always the unreliable one. That the authority in the room got to decide what actually happened.

Not anymore.

I want my younger self to know: your reality was never wrong. They were just louder.

If you have been where I have been — different story, same shape — this is for you.
