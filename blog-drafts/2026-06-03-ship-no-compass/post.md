# The Ship With No Compass

I spent forty years sailing brilliantly in bad weather because that was all I ever learned.

No destination of my own. No course I chose. Just the skill — and I built real skill — to keep moving through storms someone else created. Eleven years managing luxury hotel teams in Sydney. Four languages acquired almost by accident. A CELTA from Cambridge, earned in the worst mental health year of my life.

Always someone else's structure holding me up.

I am bipolar I. ADHD. Complex PTSD. The diagnoses came when I was nearly forty, living in La Réunion, after my body gave out before my brain admitted anything was wrong. Back pain. Knee pain. Pain everywhere. My GP looked at me and said, straight and kind: "My friend, I think you are depressed. Like, big time."

He was right. And he was also only half right.

The diagnoses did not devastate me. They explained me. ADHD for the seven-year unfinished degree, the last-minute everything, the books I devoured at 1am on subjects no one asked me to study. Bipolar for the years I spent in Sydney riding waves of invincibility — and the crashes that followed, the rent money spent, the lies I told to stay afloat. C-PTSD for the hypervigilance, the dissociation, the way authority figures make my nervous system brace as if expecting a blow.

"Looking at my diagnostic today, I know exactly why."

What I did not know, for most of my life, was that survival is not the same as living.

I was good at surviving. I am a high-functioning survivor. I walk into a room of VIP clients, read every face, smooth every friction, perform under pressure that would crack most people. I learned this before I was ten years old. I became the family mediator at thirteen — the silent sponge who absorbed every conflict so the household would not fly apart.

My psychologist's image for me is a ship with a broken compass. It sails brilliantly. It handles weather no one should handle. It just has no idea where it is going, because it was built for someone else's journey.

Here is what I know now: the compass is not broken. It was never given a chance to point anywhere.

I am building that chance. Not in one dramatic gesture. In small, unglamorous daily acts: a fixed wake time, a walk, a 500-word page. My psychiatrist and I protect the rhythm before the output, because for bipolar I, riding a rising episode feels like genius and costs you three weeks of your life afterward.

I write because writing is the first thing I have ever done that belongs entirely to me.

This blog is not a recovery story. Recovery implies you get back to where you started. I never had a before worth returning to.

This is a navigation story. And for the first time, I am the one holding the compass.
