# What 11 Years Serving Billionaires Taught Me About Ordinary People

I spent eleven years in some of the most expensive rooms in Sydney.

Star Casino. The Merivale Group. I managed teams of up to fifteen people speaking five or six languages between them. I sourced wine from suppliers who knew my voice before my name. I read the room — every room — for a living.

The people with the most money were not the hardest to serve. They were often the easiest. Money at a certain level removes the anxiety of cost. What you are left with is just the person, stripped of the usual performance. Rich people in private are frequently tired, frequently lonely, and surprisingly grateful for any warmth that is not paid for.

The hardest people to serve were the ones performing.

The couple who talked too loudly about money they wanted you to hear about. The man who tested the sommelier not because he wanted good wine but because he wanted to win. The group who needed every staff member to understand, through the architecture of their requests, that they were important.

I am not judging any of them. I recognise the performance because I spent most of my life giving it.

What I learned in those rooms is that almost everyone manages something the room cannot see. The snapping guest is often frightened. The demanding client is often ashamed. The impossible table is, in most cases, someone who has been made to feel small somewhere else and chose this room to get it back.

You cannot help a person you have decided to be annoyed by. You can help almost anyone you choose to be curious about.

This is the skill I now translate into English teaching.

I work with adults — professionals, aviation staff, hospitality workers, medical teams — who need functional English and who arrive already embarrassed about what they do not know. The embarrassment is the obstacle, not the vocabulary. I learned in luxury hotels that the first job is never the stated job. The stated job is: “I need my glass refilled.” The real job is: make this person feel seen without making them feel watched.

The same dynamic runs through every classroom.

Language learning is vulnerable. You put your thoughts out in a code you are not yet fluent in and wait for someone to confirm you made sense. Every adult who opens their mouth in a language that is not theirs does something genuinely brave.

I carry this from the hotel floor: do not let competence become performance. Do not make the person in front of you feel audited.

Eleven years taught me that the most expensive thing you can give someone is the feeling that they belong in the room.

That cost nothing. That was always within reach.

I try to give it to every student I teach.
