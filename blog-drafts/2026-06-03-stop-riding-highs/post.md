# Why I Stopped Riding My Highs

Last year I had a system for managing my bipolar disorder. It matched my work to my mood states. Hypomanic? Deep focus, complex writing, new projects. Low? Admin, walks, gentle tasks. The logic was elegant. It made me feel clever.

My psychiatrist explained why it was wrong.

When you ride a rising episode — even a productive, creative, brilliant one — you extend it. Extended episodes cost sleep. Lost sleep costs stability. And for bipolar I, instability is not an inconvenience. It is a medical risk.

I was using my highs to produce. My highs were using me to burn.

The new principle we built together is simple: protect the rhythm before the output.

Fixed wake time, regardless of how I feel. Fixed meals. Fixed wind-down. Behavioural activation on low days — not because I feel motivated, but because movement creates mood, not the other way around. CBT-I protocol for the insomnia that got dramatically worse when therapy reopened old memories and my sleep fell apart before it got better.

None of this is exciting. That is the point.

Bipolar I has a specific cruelty: the upswing feels like your best self. You feel invincible, fast, witty, productive. Every idea connects to every other idea. You sleep four hours and feel better for it. You think: this is who I actually am, and the medication and routine are suppressing me.

I know this feeling. I also know what comes after it.

The crash is not symmetric. You spend four days running on dopamine and three weeks paying the bill in a currency you cannot afford.

So now I do something that feels counterintuitive: I stop working when the episode is rising.

Not because the work is bad. Sometimes it is good. But the episode is not a productivity tool. It is a medical event. Treating it like a resource to exploit is like treating a fever as free energy.

The ADHD layer adds its own problem. My brain, left unstructured, generates fifteen ideas, starts all of them, finishes none, and feels genuinely proud of the chaos. The fix is externalising — writing every task outside my head, committing to the next physical action only, keeping the environment as the reminder system instead of my memory.

Both conditions share a deep need for external structure when internal structure fails. I do not trust my own sense of “feeling productive.” I trust my calendar, my fixed times, my doctor.

This is not weakness. This is engineering.

The version of me that rode every high and apologised for every low was performing a version of health for an audience that did not exist. The version of me now has a fixed wake time, a morning page, and a 500-word daily target I almost always hit.

I am slower. I am more consistent. I am still here.

That is enough.
