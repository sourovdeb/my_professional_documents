# What We Don't Talk About: Your Hunter-Gatherer Brain Running the Digital Empire's Game

## Hook

In 2004, a Facebook engineer was A/B testing the Like button. In 2016, a former senior engineer at the company, Aza Raskin, went public with what they had found: **the infinite scroll alone was generating 200,000 extra hours of daily usage across the platform.** He invented it. He was, publicly, horrified.

He shouldn't have been surprised. He was just the most recent person to discover what the Roman Empire had known two thousand years earlier: **if you give humans a mechanism that triggers the reward pathway at variable intervals, they will not stop.** The Romans called it *panem et circenses* — bread and circuses. The neuroscientists call it *variable ratio reinforcement*. B.F. Skinner discovered it in rats in 1957. Every slot machine, every social media feed, every algorithmic recommendation engine runs on the same ancient biological machinery.

The machinery was not designed by the Romans. It was not designed by Silicon Valley. It was built by 200,000 years of evolution in conditions where finding food, status, and mates was genuinely hard — and any burst of reward deserved *pursuit*.

We are those same animals. We are running that same code. And the world has been architectured to exploit it.

## The Hidden Driver

The neurochemical at the centre of this story is **dopamine** — and the first thing to understand is that almost everything you have read about dopamine is wrong, or at least incomplete. Dopamine is not a pleasure chemical. It is an *anticipation* chemical. It is released not when you get the reward, but when you *expect* you might.

This is the mechanism that powered the hunter-gatherer brain. When a human ancestor detected signs of game — tracks, disturbed vegetation, distant sound — dopamine flooded the brain. This created a state of focused, energised pursuit. The kill might happen. It might not. The variability was not a bug. It was the entire point. Variable reward schedules create the most persistent, compulsive pursuit patterns in all of mammalian behaviour.

Now map this onto **bipolar disorder, ADHD, and clinical depression** — conditions documented in serious clinical detail (NIMH, NHS, Haute Autorité de Santé) and directly relevant to a substantial portion of the population. ADHD is fundamentally a **dopamine regulation disorder**: the brain struggles to generate baseline motivation for routine tasks, but can hyperfocus with extraordinary intensity when the dopamine environment is right — when the task is novel, high-stakes, immediately rewarding, or socially resonant. This is not a deficit. In ancestral terms, this was a *specialised survival mode*: the person who could hyperfocus during a crisis, who could hold twenty sensory threads simultaneously, who got bored of routine because routine was genuinely dangerous in an unpredictable environment.

Bipolar disorder maps similarly. The hypomanic state — elevated mood, reduced sleep need, accelerated thinking, grandiosity, impulsivity — is, in evolutionary terms, a **high-resource mobilisation mode**. Everything goes into the current objective. Sleep is not required. Danger is not registered. This is the mode that built civilisations during their expansion phases. It is also the mode that destroys individuals in a world that has scheduled bus routes, mortgage payments, and workplace conduct policies.

**Freud** saw the death drive — *Thanatos* — as the organism's pull toward dissolution, rest, the return to an undifferentiated state. Contemporary neuroscience has complicated but not refuted this: the brain under chronic stress, chronic overstimulation, or chronic pain develops strong inhibitory drives. The addiction to depressants — alcohol, opioids, benzodiazepines — is the nervous system seeking regulatory relief from a dopamine environment it cannot downregulate by itself.

## Historical Patterns

**The British Opium Trade in China** (1839–1860) is the cleanest historical example of a political economy deliberately exploiting the human reward pathway for extraction. The East India Company grew opium in Bengal, shipped it to China, and created — knowingly, with documented internal awareness — mass addiction across the Chinese population. When China attempted to stop the trade, Britain went to war. Twice.

The internal logic, documented in parliamentary debates, was framed as *free trade*. The mechanism was *systematic dopamine hijacking at civilisational scale*. An estimated two million Chinese were addicted by the 1830s. The Qing Dynasty's social cohesion was genuinely destabilised. The extraction of silver — the trade balance that British merchants demanded — flowed back to fund the Industrial Revolution.

This is not ancient history. The contemporary pharmaceutical opioid crisis in the United States follows an almost identical template: Purdue Pharma's internal documents (released in litigation) show explicit knowledge that OxyContin was highly addictive, combined with systematic marketing to physicians framing it as low-risk. The *dopamine hijack* was industrialised. The **Sackler family accumulated approximately $14 billion in family wealth** during the period when the crisis killed an estimated 500,000 Americans. The evolutionary machinery — variable reward, craving, loss aversion — was the product.

**Policymakers** have long understood that controlling the reward pathway is controlling the population. The CIA's MKUltra programme (1953–1973), declassified in 1977, was explicitly attempting to map and manipulate the human reward and compliance systems — using LSD, hypnosis, and torture — to create controllable states. The programme was shut down not because policymakers decided it was wrong, but because a congressional committee forced disclosure. The underlying logic — that human neurochemistry is a lever — never left intelligence doctrine.

**Social media platforms** are the current iteration. Former executives from Facebook, Instagram, Twitter, and TikTok have given sworn congressional testimony describing internal metrics that prioritised *engagement time* over user wellbeing — with full knowledge that certain content patterns (outrage, anxiety, social comparison) generated more engagement than neutral content. The economic model required maximising dopamine-mediated use. This is not conspiracy theory. It is documented product design.

The difference between the opium trader and the engagement engineer is primarily one of physical proximity to the harm. The mechanism is identical.

## Modern Echoes

The **skill half-life crisis** (documented in Lightcast and WEF Future of Jobs data, IBM estimates skill obsolescence at 2.5–5 years) is a structural anxiety generator. When workers believe that their current capabilities will be worthless within five years, they enter a chronic low-grade threat state. This activates *fast life history strategies* — the evolutionary mode characterised by short-termism, risk-taking, and reduced investment in long-term relationships. Ironically, chronic anxiety about the future produces the *impulsivity* that makes long-term planning harder.

This is exactly the neurological state that makes people most susceptible to the next course, the next certification, the next pivot — and least likely to build the slow, durable forms of expertise that actually create resilience. The attention economy and the skills economy converge on the same exploitation: manufactured urgency that drives consumption.

In the micro-enterprise world, the same pattern appears in the *productivity performance loop*: the solo operator who consumes endless productivity content, tools, and systems while producing less. The dopamine is in the consumption — the new system, the fresh template, the optimised workflow. The actual work is neurologically dull by comparison.

## Implications & Self-Reflection

Here is the synthesis that nobody in the wellness industry is paid to say: **the conditions that make you most vulnerable to exploitation — anxiety, dopamine dysregulation, social isolation, financial precarity — are the same conditions that the most profitable industries in the world are structured to maintain.**

This is not an accident. It is an emergent property of systems that are rewarded for engagement, not wellbeing. The evolutionary machinery that was calibrated for genuinely scarce environments has been placed inside an abundance machine that has learned to simulate scarcity.

What does this mean for you, practically?

It means that *managing your own neurochemistry* is not a self-care luxury. It is a political act. Every deliberate boundary around your attention — the fixed wake time, the scheduled offline window, the decision to read a book instead of scroll — is a refusal to have your dopamine system operated by someone else's revenue model.

It also means recognising that the darkness is not yours alone. The bipolar cycle, the ADHD hyperfocus, the addiction pull — these are ancient mechanisms running in a hostile modern environment. They are not your character defects. They are the cost of being human in the current configuration of the world.

That is worth knowing. That is worth sitting with.
