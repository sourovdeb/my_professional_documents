# The Primal Logic of Empire: How Evolutionary Bias Makes Systems Harm the Innocent

## Hook: "A Visa Is Not a Right. It Is a Privilege."

A French embassy official in Dhaka said this to a family member of a French tax-paying resident. The family member had been waiting for a visa to attend a family event. Embassy agents had yelled at her during her appointment, hung up on her brother when he called to clarify, and introduced a new requirement—physical return to the embassy—after she had already completed her application from France. When the resident raised the matter formally, the embassy's response was documented and precise: many Bangladeshi nationals abuse the visa system, making extreme measures necessary. The family were collateral damage. This was stated without apology.

There was no individual villain in that interaction. The official was executing institutional policy. The policy was rationally designed to reduce a measurable problem (visa abuse) at the cost of an unmeasured one (harm to innocent applicants). The unmeasured cost didn't appear in any metric. The institution optimised for what it could count.

This is how empires have always worked.

## The Hidden Driver: Evolutionary Risk Aversion and the Out-Group Calculus

Evolutionary psychology identifies a cluster of cognitive patterns that were essential for hunter-gatherer survival: **in-group/out-group distinction** (us vs. them), **risk aversion** (prefer certain small loss over possible larger gain), and **availability heuristic** (weight recent or vivid examples disproportionately).

In a band of 150 people—the Dunbar number, the approximate cognitive limit of personal relationships—these biases were adaptive. You needed to trust your band completely and treat outsiders with calibrated suspicion. You needed to over-weight threats: the predator you imagined in the bush that wasn't there cost you nothing; the predator you didn't imagine that was there cost you everything. Paranoia was fitness.

The problem is that these heuristics are now running inside institutions that process millions of people. The visa official in Dhaka isn't operating from personal prejudice—they are running a scaled-up, bureaucratically encoded version of the ancestral threat-detection system. A pattern has been identified: nationals of certain countries abuse visas at higher rates. The institution encodes that pattern as policy. Every individual from that country then absorbs the statistical behaviour of the worst-performing members of their national group.

The individual's humanity is irrelevant to the system. The system is doing exactly what it was evolved—and then institutionally designed—to do.

## Historical Patterns: The Mechanics of Collective Punishment

**The British Raj and the Criminal Tribes Act (1871).** The British colonial administration in India identified certain nomadic communities as having an "inherited criminal tendency." The Criminal Tribes Act of 1871 required members of these communities to register with local police, obtain passes to travel, and could be arrested without warrant simply for belonging to the designated group. Approximately 2.3 million people were subjected to this regime at its peak. The Act was not abolished until 1952—five years after independence. The communities designated as criminal were so because the colonial administration's risk-avoidance mechanism tagged them as threats. Their individual behaviour was irrelevant.

**The Japanese American internment (1942).** Following Pearl Harbor, Executive Order 9066 authorised the forced relocation of approximately 120,000 Japanese Americans from the West Coast. The military rationale was risk aversion: the impossibility of distinguishing loyal from disloyal individuals meant the entire group was treated as a threat. A government report later described this as "race prejudice, war hysteria, and a failure of political leadership." But those three factors are not aberrations—they are precisely what the evolutionary bias architecture produces under conditions of acute threat. The Out-Group becomes the threat category. Individual variation ceases to matter.

**The modern surveillance state and pre-crime logic.** The post-9/11 no-fly list in the United States at its peak contained over one million entries, including infants and sitting US senators. The Transportation Security Administration's logic was structurally identical to the Criminal Tribes Act: identify a pattern, create a category, apply it to everyone who resembles the pattern. The cost to the wrongly included was irrelevant to the architecture of the system. Systems optimise for their measurable output (intercepted threats) and ignore their unmeasured cost (harm to innocent individuals).

Marketers and policy architects are entirely aware of this mechanism. Political campaigns use it explicitly: identify an out-group, attribute responsibility for the in-group's suffering to that out-group, and offer protection through exclusion. The dehumanisation doesn't require explicit racism or explicit malice. It requires only the normal operation of the threat-detection system at scale.

The CIA's influence operations—documented in declassified COINTELPRO and MKUltra files—operated on the same logic. When the FBI under J. Edgar Hoover systematically surveilled, harassed, and disrupted Black civil rights organisations in the 1960s, the institutional logic was risk aversion: these organisations were classified as threats. Martin Luther King Jr. was classified as "the most dangerous Negro in America." The classification wasn't personal. It was the out-group calculus operating at institutional scale.

## Modern Echoes: Algorithmic Discrimination and the Bias Pipeline

The bias has now been encoded into algorithms that make consequential decisions faster than any human court could review. Predictive policing software (like PredPol, now rebranded Geolitica) uses historical arrest data to predict future crime locations. Because historical arrest data reflects over-policing of Black and Latino neighbourhoods, the algorithm directs police to those same neighbourhoods, generates new arrests, feeds that data back into the model, and reproduces the original bias at scale and speed. The system isn't racist in any intentional sense. It is running the evolutionary in-group/out-group heuristic through a feedback loop.

The visa system case that opens this article is the analogue in immigration. Credit scoring systems are the analogue in finance. Hiring algorithms are the analogue in employment. In each case, a pattern identified in a subset of a group is applied to the entire group. The individual's actual behaviour is filtered through a category that was never designed with them in mind.

The important institutional detail: none of these systems have a mechanism for absorbing the cost they impose. The French embassy official doesn't receive feedback on the harm caused to the wrongly refused. The predictive policing software doesn't receive a signal when it generates a false positive. The hiring algorithm doesn't know that the qualified applicant it filtered out would have been exceptional. The systems optimise for their own metrics and are blind to their own externalities—precisely as evolution designed their human architects to be.

## Implications and Self-Reflection

None of this is comfortable. The uncomfortable synthesis is that institutional harm to innocent people is not primarily the product of bad actors. It is the product of cognitive architecture running at the wrong scale. Your brain was designed for a band of 150. You are now embedded in systems processing millions. The biases that kept your ancestors alive are now running visa departments, credit agencies, hiring platforms, and border controls.

This matters because it changes what "fixing the system" requires. If institutional harm were primarily the product of bad individuals, we could fix it by replacing those individuals. But if it is the product of cognitive architecture, then every new individual who occupies the role will run the same biases under pressure. The fix must be structural: systems that force measurement of the harm they impose on innocent individuals, mechanisms for appeal and feedback, circuit-breakers that interrupt the out-group calculus before it becomes policy.

What we know about ourselves, we can begin to govern.

**Reflect on this:** Think of a system you participate in—a workplace policy, a community rule, a family norm—that punishes the innocent to manage the risk posed by others. Who absorbs the cost? Who decides whether that cost is acceptable?
