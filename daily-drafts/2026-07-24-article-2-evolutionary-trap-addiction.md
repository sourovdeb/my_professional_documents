# The Evolutionary Trap: What We Don't Talk About When We Talk About Addiction

## Hook

He arrived in Australia at nineteen with nothing except a desperate need to escape and a level of intelligence that had never found a structure willing to contain it. For the first few years it worked: he found work, paid rent, saved money. Then something shifted. He described it later with a precision that took thirty years to develop: *"I was like a kite without any string. I had no control over myself."*

Alcohol. Cocaine. Two years of it. Rent money spent on other things. Lies. The cascade of a person who had spent his entire childhood suppressing every signal from his own nervous system — and who, the moment the structure of parental control fell away, had no idea what he actually wanted, needed, or felt. The substances filled that void. Not because they were pleasurable, exactly, but because they were loud enough to drown out the silence where a self was supposed to be.

He is now abstinent. He has been for years. But the mechanism that produced the two years of chaos is not gone. It is the same mechanism that produced the procrastination, the last-minute submissions, the "productive avoidance" — cleaning the house rather than writing the assignment. It is, at its root, the same ancient circuit. Understanding it does not cure it. But not understanding it guarantees you will be run by it without even knowing it is happening.

## The Hidden Driver

The dopaminergic reward system evolved to do one thing: direct attention and energy toward behaviours that improve survival and reproduction. Food, sex, social status, the successful completion of a hunt — all of these trigger a dopamine release that signals to the organism: *do that again*. In the hunter-gatherer environment, this was a precision instrument. Calorie-dense food was rare; sex carried real reproductive risk and opportunity; high-status individuals had genuinely better survival outcomes. The reward signal was calibrated to a world of scarcity and real consequence.

Now introduce cocaine, alcohol, or any modern drug of abuse. These substances do not provide survival benefit. What they do is directly stimulate the dopaminergic circuitry — flooding the nucleus accumbens with dopamine at intensities the brain was never designed to receive from natural rewards. The brain responds as evolution designed it to: it downregulates its own dopamine receptors to compensate. Now you need more of the substance to get the same signal. Natural rewards — food, socialising, achievement — produce increasingly faint responses by comparison. The trap is complete. The ancient circuit, designed to direct you toward survival, is now directing you toward the substance because the substance has hacked the signal.

But here is the part that almost nobody talks about: **the vulnerability to this hack is not random, and it is not a character flaw**. It is, in large part, a consequence of the original environment. Children who grow up in chaotic, high-stress households — where the stress response is chronically activated and where self-regulation is never modelled or taught — develop neurological profiles that make them substantially more susceptible to addiction. The research is unequivocal on this (Adverse Childhood Experiences studies; Gabor Maté's *In the Realm of Hungry Ghosts*, 2008).

The mechanism is straightforward: chronic stress elevates cortisol, which depresses baseline dopamine tone. The child who grew up in a perpetually stressful environment arrives at adulthood with a dopamine system already running below baseline. Substances that spike dopamine are not just pleasurable to such a person — they produce, for the first time, a feeling of *normality*. The drug does not make them high, not exactly. It makes them feel what other people apparently feel all the time. This is why the person most at risk of addiction is not the hedonist seeking pleasure but the person who has never had access to ordinary wellbeing.

## Historical Patterns

The opium trade is perhaps the most stark historical illustration of this mechanism at imperial scale. Britain's East India Company grew opium in Bengal, smuggled it into China in exchange for silver, and when China's Qing government tried to suppress the trade, Britain fought two wars to keep it open — the First and Second Opium Wars (1839–1842, 1856–1860). The result was the forced addiction of an estimated 10–20% of the Chinese adult male population by the late 19th century.

This was not an accident. The Company's traders understood, at least empirically, that populations under stress — economically marginalized populations in particular — were highly susceptible to addictive substances, and that once addicted, demand was inelastic. The trade was, in economic terms, almost perfectly structured: the supply was colonial, the profit flowed to London, and the addiction itself destroyed the social fabric that might otherwise have organized resistance.

The same logic appears in the American opioid crisis. Purdue Pharma's internal documents, revealed in litigation, show that the company specifically targeted "high-decile prescribers" in regions with high rates of economic distress — Appalachia, the Rust Belt — where unemployment and the collapse of industrial identity had produced populations with high baseline stress and low access to other forms of relief. The CIA's documented history of looking the other way — or actively facilitating — drug trafficking in regions it sought to destabilize (documented in Alfred McCoy's *The Politics of Heroin*, 1972, and confirmed by later congressional investigations) follows the same pattern: addiction as a tool of social control, as reliable as any army.

Policy makers know this. The "War on Drugs" framing, adopted by Nixon's administration in 1971, was explicitly about control rather than health, as Nixon's domestic policy chief John Ehrlichman later admitted in a 1994 interview: the campaign targeted Black communities and anti-war protesters. Criminalization of addiction ensured that the populations most vulnerable — those with the highest ACE scores, the most economic stress, the least access to treatment — were also the most exposed to the carceral system.

## Modern Echoes

You do not need heroin to see this mechanism operating. Social media platforms are, from a neurological standpoint, variable ratio reinforcement schedules — the same mechanism that makes slot machines so difficult to stop using. The "like" notification is a dopamine spike. Its unpredictability (sometimes you get one, sometimes you don't) makes the behaviour more compulsive, not less. Instagram, TikTok, and their successors did not invent this; they built it deliberately, with full knowledge of the psychological literature on reinforcement schedules. Former executives at these companies have said as much on record.

The population most vulnerable to social media addiction is, predictably, adolescents with high anxiety and low self-esteem — that is, adolescents whose dopamine systems are running below baseline because of stress, which means adolescents from chaotic or emotionally neglectful households. The ancient trap has a new surface, but the same bottom.

In the micro-enterprise world — freelancers, remote workers, the gig economy — a version of this plays out in the relationship to productivity. The person who cannot stop checking email, cannot stop working, cannot resist picking up one more task: this is not ambition, in most cases. It is the same dopaminergic loop, substituting "completed task" for cocaine. The burnout that follows is structurally identical to the addict's crash: the system that was artificially flooded eventually goes silent.

## Implications and Self-Reflection

The most important thing to understand about addiction — to substances, to work, to social approval, to another person — is that it begins as a solution. It solved a real problem, probably for a long time. The problem was: the baseline felt intolerable. The addiction raised the baseline. Calling it a weakness, a choice, a moral failure is not only factually incorrect; it actively prevents recovery by directing shame at exactly the organ — the self — that is already wounded.

What does help is the question the addiction was answering: *What were you not feeling that it let you feel? What were you feeling that it let you not feel?* These are not rhetorical. They are diagnostic. The answer to those questions is the map of the wound.

Hunter-gatherer communities — the 200,000-year baseline — had naturally occurring social rituals that managed exactly this: trance states, communal ceremonies, seasonal rhythms that gave the nervous system legitimate periods of altered consciousness, integrated into a social fabric that also provided belonging, meaning, and accountability. We stripped all of that out and handed people a bottle and a slot machine and called them weak when they couldn't stop.

The kite without a string eventually has to find something to tie to. The question is what.
