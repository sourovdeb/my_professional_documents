# The Primal Empire: How Evolutionary Bias Became the Architecture of Exclusion

## Hook

In 2024, a French Embassy in Dhaka hung up on a man's brother. They yelled at his elderly mother. They added new bureaucratic requirements after she had already returned from France — requirements that didn't exist when she applied. When the man complained, the embassy's response was clinical: *"A visa is not a right. It is a privilege."*

The family was not undocumented. They were tax-paying French residents. The mother held a valid visa history. By every rational metric, the case was straightforward. But the embassy had made a calculation — not about this specific family, but about *the category* they appeared to belong to. Risk management. Evolutionary heuristics dressed in the language of bureaucratic protocol.

This is not an aberration. This is a pattern as old as organised human society — because it is older than organised human society. It is wired in.

## The Hidden Driver

When an institution — an embassy, a corporation, a university — consistently treats members of a group as high-risk based on the behaviour of a subset, it is not making an error. It is executing an ancient algorithm.

Evolutionary psychology identifies this as **coalition-based threat detection**: the brain evolved in environments where group membership (tribe, kin group, band) was the primary predictor of trustworthiness. To trust an unknown individual required costly information-gathering. The shortcut — *what group do they belong to, and what have that group's members done?* — saved metabolic energy and, in ancestral environments, sometimes saved lives.

The problem is that this shortcut was calibrated for bands of 150 people, not modern states of millions. Scaled up to institutions and governments, it becomes systematic exclusion. The mechanism doesn't know it's wrong. It is doing exactly what it evolved to do.

The *Interdisciplinary Insights* presentation (Sourov Deb, July 2026) documents this as **evolutionary bias in institutional behaviour**: systems optimise for risk avoidance even when it harms innocent individuals. The French Embassy's logic — *many Bangladeshis abuse the visa system, so extreme measures are necessary* — is structurally identical to every collective punishment mechanism in history. The specific group changes. The architecture does not.

Language becomes the instrument of formalisation. The embassy's phrase — *"a visa is not a right, it is a privilege"* — does specific cognitive work. It converts a systemic injustice into a philosophical position. It repositions the excluded family not as victims of institutional bias, but as supplicants who failed to earn inclusion. The linguistics of exclusion have always served the same function: to make the powerful comfortable with the harm they cause.

## Historical Patterns

**The Mongol Empire** (13th–14th century) operated on a clear binary: submit and be incorporated; resist and be annihilated. Entire cities were erased — Nishapur, Merv, Baghdad — not because their specific inhabitants had wronged Genghis Khan, but because *example* was the mechanism of control. The destruction of one city was a message to the next. This is coalition-based threat management at its most explicit: the individual is irrelevant; only the category matters.

What is rarely discussed is how **the Mongol administrators rationalised this**. The historical record shows consistent framing of annihilation as *justice* — cities that resisted had chosen death; those that submitted chose life. The framework converted mass murder into a *fair transaction*. The psychology is not alien. It is the same architecture the French Embassy used, at a radically different scale.

**The British Raj** (1858–1947) built an entire civilisational project on a variant of this logic. The 1905 partition of Bengal was described by Lord Curzon as administrative efficiency. The reality — documented by subsequent historians — was deliberate fracturing of nationalist solidarity along religious lines. The British did not need to believe they were doing harm. Their cognitive framework — *we are governing; they are being governed* — absorbed the contradiction entirely. The system of passes, permits, and racial classification in colonial India was not bureaucratic accident. It was evolutionary tribal logic institutionalised in law.

**Policymakers and the CIA** employed the same architecture in Cold War counterintelligence. COINTELPRO (FBI, 1956–1971) targeted not individuals with documented crimes but *categories* — Black leaders, socialist organisers, Native American activists. The internal justification was always threat prevention. The mechanism was always collective punishment. Declassified documents show operatives who genuinely believed they were protecting democracy while systematically destroying lives through infiltration, forged letters, and coordinated harassment.

**Marketers** weaponise the same evolutionary machinery in reverse: instead of exclusion, *manufactured inclusion*. Brand identity politics — the Apple user, the Nike runner, the Patagonia environmentalist — exploits the tribal brain's need for coalition membership. You don't just buy a product. You *join a tribe*. The advertising industry has studied evolutionary psychology not to help consumers but to make the tribal drive commercially useful.

## Modern Echoes

The French Embassy's logic lives in every credit scoring algorithm, every hiring ATS filter, every content moderation system. When a machine learning model is trained on historical data — data generated by humans operating with evolutionary biases — it learns to encode those biases as statistical fact.

The *observer effect* is real: the same institutional logic that caused a family to be mistreated becomes, when scrutinised and challenged, a vector for reform. Sourov Deb's complaints to multiple bodies — French labour authorities, Cambridge Assessment English, Qualitia Certification — created *entanglement*. Filing a complaint changes the system's behaviour. Not reliably. Not quickly. But the quantum of documented injustice alters the probability distribution of future institutional behaviour.

In workplace dynamics, the same exclusionary architecture operates through what organisational psychologists call *in-group favouritism*. Teams consistently overrate the contributions of members who look, sound, and culturally code like the dominant group. This is not conscious discrimination in most cases. It is the ancestral brain's coalition software running inside a modern office.

The modern micro-enterprise and gig economy has a particular vulnerability: workers who fall outside the expected profile — neurodivergent, foreign-born, disabled, unorthodox — face the evolutionary exclusion algorithm without institutional protection. The research from Sourov Deb's own legal case (documented in `presentations/Interdisciplinary_Insights_Psychology_Linguistics_Physics_AI.md`) shows that even when someone carries formal credentials — 18 years of professional experience, a Cambridge CELTA — the evolutionary shortcut of *category membership* can override individual assessment. The institution defaults to exclusion when confronted with what it cannot easily classify.

## Implications & Self-Reflection

The discomfort this should produce is precise: **you are not immune to this architecture**. Every person reading this runs the same tribal software. The question is not whether your brain makes categorical threat assessments — it does — but whether you have built any institutional or personal friction into that process.

The evolutionary answer to collective punishment is *costly individualisation*: taking the time, spending the attention, bearing the uncertainty of assessing individuals rather than categories. This is metabolically expensive. It goes against the ancestral grain. But it is also what distinguishes a functioning civil society from a bureaucracy executing evolutionary heuristics at scale.

The visa officer who yelled at a mother was not uniquely cruel. They were human. The algorithm they served was not designed by them. But they could have chosen, in that moment, to see a person rather than a category.

That choice is available to you, daily.
