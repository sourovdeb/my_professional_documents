# The Shadow of Power: How Institutions Evolved to Exclude

## Hook

On October 13, 2025, a man was fired from a specialty coffee shop in Réunion for "medical unfitness." He had diagnosed ADHD and depression — both classified as long-term disabilities under French law. He had disclosed this to his employer. He had documentation. The response was a termination letter.

He is not unusual. He is the pattern.

Every institution that teaches inclusion — schools that train teachers, embassies that process families, certification bodies that assess professionals — carries within it a structural drive toward exclusion. Not because the individuals running these systems are cruel, though some are. Because exclusion is, from the system's perspective, the path of least resistance. And evolution, at every scale, favors efficiency over justice.

---

## The Hidden Driver

The core concept here is what the *Interdisciplinary Insights* presentation calls **Evolutionary Bias in Institutional Behavior**: the tendency of systems to optimize for risk aversion even when doing so imposes catastrophic costs on individuals.

Consider how institutions respond to disability accommodation requests. The legally correct response is adjustment: extended time, alternative formats, modified environments. The evolutionarily efficient response is rejection: the system already runs, why change it for one person? The legal requirement to accommodate is, in evolutionary terms, an artificial override — an external pressure forcing a system to behave against its natural gradient.

This is Jung's Shadow at the organizational scale. Every institution that positions itself as inclusive has a Shadow: the policies, cultures, and decision-making patterns that systematically contradict that positioning. The Shadow is not hidden because people are hypocrites (though hypocrisy is abundant). It is hidden because the system generates genuine self-belief among its participants that they are doing right, even as they are doing harm.

The Jungian Shadow in individuals becomes deflected aggression, unacknowledged prejudice, suppressed desire. In institutions, it becomes: *a visa is not a right, it's a privilege. Your disability was not disclosed through the correct channel. The assessment criteria apply equally to all students.*

Each of these statements is technically defensible. Each is the Shadow speaking in the language of neutrality.

## Historical Patterns

**The British colonial education system** is the case study in the accommodation paradox operating at civilizational scale. The British Empire built schools, printed textbooks, trained local teachers — and simultaneously constructed a curriculum that pathologized indigenous knowledge, denigrated local languages, and measured intelligence by performance in the colonizer's language and cultural framework. The system taught. The system also excluded. It did both simultaneously, and those who ran it largely believed they were doing the former while doing the latter.

The accommodation paradox — institutions that teach inclusion while practicing exclusion — is not an anomaly. It is the evolutionary baseline. A system that grants accommodation undermines its standardization. Standardization is the mechanism by which institutions scale. Therefore, accommodation is structurally anti-institutional, and institutions will resist it unless externally compelled.

**Mongol administrative practices** offer the inverse illustration. The Mongol Empire, uniquely among the great empires of the medieval world, deliberately incorporated foreign administrators, scribes, tax collectors, and engineers from conquered territories. This was not altruism. It was efficiency: the Mongols had conquered populations with superior administrative capacity, and excluding them from institutional function would have reduced the extraction efficiency. Inclusion was adopted when exclusion became costly to the system.

The lesson: institutions include when inclusion is more profitable than exclusion. They exclude when exclusion is more profitable. Rights legislation and policy frameworks are attempts to shift that calculation — to make exclusion costly enough that inclusion becomes the efficient choice.

**How policy makers and marketers deploy this:** Political campaigns systematically exploit the in-group/out-group machinery of institutional exclusion. The message is never "exclude those people." It is "protect this community" — which activates the same evolutionary circuitry while maintaining deniability. Marketing research consistently shows that scarcity framing (this is limited, you might miss out) and threat framing (protect what you have) outperform opportunity framing in driving action. These are not manipulations imposed on an otherwise rational public. They are direct activations of hunter-gatherer threat-response systems that remain fully operational in the prefrontal-cortex era.

The CIA's COINTELPRO program — which systematically infiltrated and disrupted civil rights, feminist, and anti-war organizations from the 1950s through the 1970s — was, in institutional terms, an application of evolutionary exclusion logic: identify groups that threaten the existing power distribution, and deploy the institution's resources to render them dysfunctional.

## Modern Echoes

The certification industry illustrates this dynamic with particular clarity. A certification body exists to validate competence. The competence validation framework, however, is necessarily built around the majority case — the typical student, the standard learning style, the conventional demonstration of mastery. Students who demonstrate competence differently, or who require modified conditions to demonstrate what they know, fall outside the efficiency envelope.

The result: an industry dedicated to recognizing talent systematically fails to recognize talent that doesn't present in the approved format.

In workplace power dynamics, the micro-version plays out daily. The employee who requires flexibility for chronic illness, or who communicates through means other than confident verbal performance, or who needs written rather than verbal instructions — each of these represents an accommodation cost to the system. Systems that are not externally compelled to absorb that cost will not do so.

The modern HR apparatus — with its diversity statements, its unconscious bias training, its reasonable adjustment policies — is the legal override attempting to counteract the evolutionary gradient. The question is whether the override is strong enough to actually change the system's behavior, or merely its language.

Evidence suggests the latter is more common than the former.

## Implications & Self-Reflection

The advocate's burden is real and measurable. Fighting an institution takes the same psychological resources that the institution has the luxury of distributing across thousands of employees. One person versus a system is never a fair fight, and the system knows this, even when no individual within it consciously wills the unfairness.

What Jung would say: before you fight the institution, identify its Shadow in yourself. The same drive to categorize, to exclude the anomalous, to prefer the familiar, lives in you. It is how you navigate information overload. It is how you form in-groups. It is how you make fast decisions. The institution is not an alien force. It is you, at scale.

That does not mean stop fighting. It means fight with clear eyes — knowing you are fighting something that includes a version of yourself, and that the only durable victory changes the system's incentive structure, not just its vocabulary.
