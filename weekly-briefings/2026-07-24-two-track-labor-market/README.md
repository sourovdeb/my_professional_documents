# The Two-Track Labour Market
### Update: the AI-skills story just split in two — 2026 trend brief #2
*5-minute read · compiled 24 July 2026 · follow-up to the 19 July "Human-Skills Premium" brief · sources at the bottom*

---

## The one line
> Last brief's story was **repricing**: AI makes human judgment more expensive.
> Five days of fresh data sharpen it into something colder — **bifurcation.**
> The same forces are now visibly splitting the workforce into **two tracks**,
> and the dividing line is no longer "AI vs. human." It's **who gets to do the
> judgment** — and the rung it's quietly kicking away is *entry-level.*

## Force #1 — The premium isn't one number, it's a fan

PwC's 2026 Barometer, read closely, shows the 62% average is a mirage. The real
distribution:

```
  AI-SKILLS WAGE PREMIUM, BY SECTOR (PwC 2026)
  premium
   ▲
 118%│ ██████████████████████  consumer / customer-facing
     │ ██████████████████████
  62%│ ███████████            ← the "average" everyone quotes
     │ ███████████
  16%│ ███                     government / public sector
     └──────────────────────────────────────────────►
        where the AI meets a paying customer, the premium explodes
```

**Read this:** the premium is largest exactly where a human still has to *decide,
persuade, and be trusted in front of a customer.* It shrinks toward zero in
process-bound public roles. AI doesn't pay you for knowing the tool — it pays you
for the judgment you wrap around the tool, and that judgment is worth most at the
customer's edge.

**Real example.** Two people both "use AI at work." A public-sector caseworker
runs an AI form-checker — ~16% bump, because the process is fixed and the
judgment is the statute's, not hers. A B2B solutions consultant uses the *same*
underlying model to draft proposals, then reads the room, kills the wrong
recommendation, and closes — ~118%. Same tool. 7× the premium. The difference is
entirely the human layer on top.

## Force #2 — The market is professionalising *and* democratising at once

**democratiser** in others — and the two tracks are diverging in real time.

The uncomfortable part: **which track a role lands on is being decided now**, and
it tracks how much irreplaceable judgment the role carries. The strategy is the
same as last week's — but the stakes just got asymmetric.

## Force #3 — The new casualty: the bottom rung

This is the genuinely new signal since the last brief, and the one worth acting on.

- Entry-level roles in AI-exposed fields are **7× more likely to demand
  traditionally *senior*-level skills** than they were.
- "Seniorised" entry roles have grown **+35%** since 2019; **ordinary** entry
  roles have **shrunk −10%.**

```
  ENTRY-LEVEL JOBS, AI-EXPOSED FIELDS (since 2019)
  "seniorised" entry roles   ██████████████████  +35%
  ordinary entry roles       ░░░░░░░ ▼            −10%
```

Translation: the job market is deleting the "learn on the job while doing the
easy tasks" phase — because AI now does the easy tasks. The ladder still exists,
but someone sawed off the first two rungs. **For early-career people and career-
changers, the move is to arrive already carrying a durable skill and a validated
domain judgment — you can no longer count on the job to teach you the basics
slowly.**

## Force #4 — Durable skills, now with hard numbers

Last brief leaned on survey opinion (73% of TA leaders *say* critical thinking).
New Lightcast data (75M+ postings) turns opinion into demand:

- **76%** of *all* job postings request **at least one durable skill.**
- **47%** request **three or more** — up **13 points** since 2021.
- **8 of the top 10** most-requested skills overall are durable (communication,
  leadership, problem-solving…), not technical.
- McKinsey now ranks **critical thinking as the single highest-value *complement*
  to AI** — the skill whose value AI *raises* most.

Durable skills aren't the soft, nice-to-have layer anymore. On the actual
postings, they're the majority of what employers ask for by name.

## Force #5 — Sentiment: the fracture reaches the workers themselves

The mood didn't calm; it **fractured along the same two-track line.** A 2026
tech-workforce survey found the field "splitting in two":

- **63%** of designers feel *overwhelmed by the pace of change*; **61%** feel *tired.*
- Researchers are among the most likely to fear losing their job to AI (**36%**);
  **51%** are anxious about job security…
- …versus only **15%** of founders.

The same person who owns more judgment feels *safer*; the person nearer the
commoditised task feels the ground moving. Fear is now a **position indicator** —
it tells you which track you're standing on.

## The move (updated for bifurcation)

Last week's formula still holds — **AI × critical thinking × domain expertise** —
but the update adds a *direction*: aim yourself at the **professionalised,
customer-facing, judgment-heavy** side of every fork.

```
     ┌──────────┐     ┌───────────────────┐     ┌──────────────────┐
     │   AI     │  ×  │ CRITICAL THINKING │  ×  │ DOMAIN EXPERTISE │
```

## 🛠 A tool to try this week

**The Two-Track Sorter** (10 minutes, one page — builds on last week's Skill-Portfolio Canvas):

1. **Name your role's core task in one verb-phrase** (e.g. "write proposals",
   "check compliance forms", "triage tickets").
2. **Ask the fork question:** *Is AI making my task a "force multiplier for my
   judgment" (professionalising) or "something anyone can now do" (democratising)?*
   Be honest.
3. If **democratising** → find the judgment call *next to* your task that a
   customer would pay a human to get right. Move toward it. That's your escape
   rung.
4. If **professionalising** → double down: the 42%-faster raises are on this
   track. Grow the human skill that makes you the trusted decider, not just the
   tool operator.
5. **Entry-level / career-change check:** what senior-level judgment can you
   *demonstrate* (a portfolio, a case, a validated call) so you skip the deleted
   bottom rung?

**Pair it with a real tool:** keep using any AI assistant (Claude, Copilot,
Gemini) as a **sparring partner** — but this week give it a sharper job: paste
your role's core task and ask it *"which parts of this are you already better at
than me, and which parts still need my judgment?"* Its honest answer maps your
personal fork. (For structured idea generation on your escape rung, the
accompanying mind map runs a SCAMPER/Zwicky pass.)

## The takeaway
> The AI-skills premium isn't a rising tide — it's a **wedge.** It lifts the
> judgment-heavy, customer-facing, senior-skilled side and presses down on the
> commoditised, process-bound, entry-level side. You don't have to predict which
> tools win. You have to know **which side of every fork your work is on — and
> walk toward the judgment.**
