**The claim:** depression is caused by a serotonin "chemical imbalance," corrected by SSRIs. This line drove SSRI marketing through the 1990s–2000s and still shapes how many people understand their own diagnosis.

**The evidence:** Lacasse & Leo (2005, *PLoS Medicine*) compared SSRI ad copy against the research literature and found no support for the low-serotonin claim — the authors declared no funding and no competing interests. In 2022, Moncrieff et al. published an umbrella review in *Molecular Psychiatry* covering 17 systematic reviews, meta-analyses and large-cohort/genetic studies (serotonin/metabolite levels, receptor binding, transporter density, tryptophan depletion, SERT genetics). Conclusion: "no convincing evidence" depression is caused by or associated with lowered serotonin. No industry funding — but the lead authors co-run the Critical Psychiatry Network and a business helping people taper off antidepressants, an ideological interest worth naming.

**The pushback:** 36 researchers, several from King's College London, rebutted the review's methodology in *Molecular Psychiatry*. Two of the most quoted rebuttal authors have disclosed pharmaceutical-industry funding — Carmine Pariante (partial industry grants, publicly declared) and Allan Young (payments from multiple drug companies, per reporting in *New Humanist* and *Mad in America*, both advocacy-leaning outlets — read with that in mind). Notably, the rebuttal disputes the review's methods but doesn't present contrary data showing a serotonin-depression link.

**Verdict:** the marketing claim is not supported by the underlying research and wasn't even when it was popularized — mainstream psychiatry had largely dropped the simple chemical-imbalance framing by the early 2000s. The 2022 review's core empirical finding stands unrebutted by new evidence; the dispute is about framing and implications for prescribing, not the raw data.
