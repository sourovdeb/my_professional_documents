There is a version of my morning routine that fills three pages. I've written it. It has
habit stacks and review rituals and optimised blocks. It also collapses completely the
moment I wake up wrong — which, with a cycling mood, is not a rare event.

So I scrapped it. What I kept was four things. Not four pages. Four anchors.

An anchor is different from a habit. A habit is something you do when you're doing well.
An anchor is something that holds when you aren't. The test of an anchor is simple: can
I execute this on my worst day, half-awake, with no motivation and a flat mood? If yes,
it's an anchor. If no, it's a nice idea.

**Anchor one: fixed wake time.** Same time, seven days a week, alarm out of reach. This
one is non-negotiable because it is the strongest single regulator of a cycling mood —
more than diet, more than exercise, more than any supplement. The research on social
rhythm therapy is clear: the body clock stabilises the mood clock, and the body clock
runs off a consistent wake signal. Sleeping in on Sunday isn't rest. It's borrowing
against the week.

**Anchor two: meds, water, light.** On waking, before any decision. The Concerta goes
in, a full glass of water follows, and I get daylight on my eyes — five minutes by a
window or outside. This takes four minutes and anchors the day's chemistry before my
brain has any say in the matter. You can't negotiate with a disordered dopamine system.
You can preempt it.

**Anchor three: one real meal.** Not the best meal, not a tracked meal — a real one, at
a roughly fixed time. A meal time is a rhythm cue before it is nutrition. Skipping it
doesn't just mean I'm hungry. It means I removed a clock signal my nervous system was
counting on. The hunger I notice. The destabilisation I don't, until later.

**Anchor four: one line written.** "Today I am ___. Today the one thing is ___." Two
blanks. Thirty seconds. This names my actual state — not the state I want to have, my
real one — so I can route the day accordingly. It also commits to one thing, not ten.
One thing I will do, even on a crash day. Everything beyond that is bonus.

Four anchors. On a good day I build something on top of them. On a bad day they *are*
the day — and the day still counts. The streak doesn't break. The body clock holds. The
chemistry gets its head start.

The whole system you read about, the 500-word essays, the automation, the outreach — all
of it stands on this floor. Get the floor right, and the rest can be built when you're
ready. Neglect it, and nothing else holds for long.

Anchors first. Always.
