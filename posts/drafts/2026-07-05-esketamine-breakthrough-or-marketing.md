Esketamine (Spravato), Johnson & Johnson's nasal-spray antidepressant, is marketed as a
breakthrough for treatment-resistant depression. The claim rests on five Phase 3 trials,
every one designed, run, and paid for by Janssen: three short-term studies (TRANSFORM-1,
-2, -3), one relapse-prevention trial, one long-term safety study. Only one of the three
short-term trials — TRANSFORM-2 — actually hit its primary endpoint (a bigger drop in
depression scores than placebo at four weeks). TRANSFORM-1 missed significance outright;
TRANSFORM-3, in elderly patients, missed by a hair (p = 0.059).
([AJMC](https://www.ajmc.com/view/transform2-results-on-esketamine-published-along-with-caveats))

The funding matters: Janssen held fresh patents on esketamine right as generic ketamine —
already used off-label for depression — offered the company nothing to sell. That's a
direct commercial incentive to find in favor of the patented molecule, and most named
investigators on the trials had consulting or contract ties to Janssen.

The FDA's own advisory committee still voted 14–2 to approve in February 2019, despite six
deaths among trial participants, including three suicides, which the FDA called "difficult
to consider as drug related."
([STAT News](https://www.statnews.com/2019/02/12/depression-drug-related-to-ketamine-wins-endorsement-of-key-advisory-panel/))

Independent, non-industry reviewers have been less convinced. Psychiatrist Erick Turner
laid out "seven concerns about efficacy and FDA approval" in *Lancet Psychiatry*.
([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31680014/)) The UK's NICE, which has no
financial stake in the drug, reviewed the same trial data and declined to recommend
esketamine — citing missing head-to-head comparisons with existing options like ECT and
thin long-term evidence. ([NICE TA854](https://www.nice.org.uk/guidance/ta854))

Verdict: this isn't fraud, but it is a modest, industry-funded result stretched into
"breakthrough" marketing. Two reviewers with no financial stake in the outcome — an
academic psychiatrist and a national health-technology agency — looked at the same data
Janssen used to get approval and reached the same skeptical conclusion the trial design
itself almost failed to avoid.
