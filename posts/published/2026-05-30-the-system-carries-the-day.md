Most productivity advice is built for your best day. Wake at five, seize the morning,
stack your habits, win. It assumes the person showing up tomorrow is the same one who
made the plan today.

Mine isn't. Some days I arrive sharp and fast. Some days I arrive flat, and the simple
act of starting feels like pushing a car uphill. A system that needs motivation to run
will always fail on exactly the day I need it most — the low one.

So I stopped designing for my best day and started designing for my worst.

The rule I live by now: **if a step needs willpower to execute, it's designed wrong.**
Willpower is the most expensive and least reliable fuel I own. Anything important has to
run without it.

That changes everything about how I build a day. I don't trust my memory, so nothing
important lives in my head — it lives in one place I always check. I don't trust my
energy, so the day has a few fixed anchors that never move: a set wake time, one real
meal, one line written down naming how I actually am today. Those anchors cost almost
nothing and hold the day up even when I bring nothing to it.

And I don't force the wrong task on the wrong state. On a sharp day, I do the hard
thinking and I write *ahead* — banking work for the days I know are coming. On a flat
day, I don't fight myself for the hard task. I do the small one that still moves the
line forward, often by talking instead of typing. The work matched to the state always
beats the "right" work done through gritted teeth, because the gritted-teeth version
usually doesn't happen at all.

People hear this and call it lowering the bar. It's the opposite. Banking work on good
days is how the bar stays *up* on bad ones. The buffer is the bar. A streak that depends
on my best day isn't a streak — it's a coin flip. A streak that survives my worst day is
a system.

Here's the part that surprised me: this isn't only for people whose moods cycle. Everyone
has worst days. Everyone overestimates the person who'll show up tomorrow. The difference
is that an unreliable brain forces you to be honest about it early, and that honesty,
once you build around it, turns out to be a real advantage.

So I don't ask, "How do I get motivated?" anymore. I ask, "What would still get done if
I brought nothing today?" Then I build that, and let the system carry the rest.

The good days are a bonus. The system is the floor. And the floor is what you stand on
when you can't stand on yourself.
