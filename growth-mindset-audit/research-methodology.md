# Growth Mindset Audit — Investigative Research Methodology

## 1. Claim Identification
Does teaching students, employees, or clients a growth mindset, the belief that
intelligence and ability can be developed through effort, produce a meaningful
improvement in academic or professional performance, as claimed by the training,
publishing, and corporate consulting industry built around this idea.

## 2. Verification Disclosure
Live web search was used to verify all sources listed below on July 5, 2026.
Claims about study design, sample size, effect size, and funding rest on the
published methods and disclosure sections of the cited papers, or on the
organizations' own public statements. No statistic in this document is invented.
Where a figure could not be independently confirmed it is omitted rather than
estimated.

## 3. Source Hierarchy
1. Primary: peer reviewed meta-analyses and a nationally representative
   randomized controlled trial.
2. Secondary: peer reviewed and magazine-length journalism examining disclosure
   practices in psychology.
3. Tertiary: the commercial entity's own public "about us" page, used only to
   document its existence and business activity, not as evidence of efficacy.

## 4. Sources Reviewed

1. Sisk VF, Burgoyne AP, Sun J, Butler JL, Macnamara BN (2018) To What Extent
   and Under Which Circumstances Are Growth Mind-Sets Important to Academic
   Achievement? Two Meta-Analyses. Psychological Science, 29(4), 549 to 571.
   Meta-analysis of 273 studies and 365,915 participants. Found a small overall
   correlation between mindset and achievement and a small mean effect of
   growth mindset interventions on achievement, d equal to 0.08, with somewhat
   larger effects concentrated among academically at-risk or low
   socioeconomic-status students. Academically funded, no industry or
   foundation sponsorship declared.
   Source: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0956797617739704
   PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29505339/

2. Yeager DS, Hanselman P, Walton GM, et al., including Duckworth AL and Dweck
   CS (2019) A national experiment reveals where a growth mindset improves
   achievement. Nature, 573, 364 to 369. Randomized controlled trial embedded
   in a nationally representative sample of United States ninth grade public
   school students. A single, under one hour, online intervention modestly
   raised grades and increased enrollment in advanced mathematics among
   lower-achieving students, with no detected effect among higher-achieving
   students. Funded by National Science Foundation grant 1761179, Raikes
   Foundation grants 17-01177 and 13028, Bezos Family Foundation, Character
   Lab, Houston Endowment, the Eunice Kennedy Shriver National Institute of
   Child Health and Human Development and the National Institutes of Health,
   the Spencer Foundation, the William T. Grant Foundation, and Stanford
   University's Center for Advanced Study in the Behavioral Sciences.
   Source: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-019-1466-y
   PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31391586/

3. Mindset Works, About Us. Documents that Carol Dweck, originator of growth
   mindset theory and a co-author of source 2, co-founded Mindset Works with
   Lisa Sorich Blackwell, a for-profit company that sells growth-mindset
   training and assessment products, including the Brainology program, to
   schools. Used here only to establish the existence of a commercial interest,
   not as a research source.
   Source: https://www.mindsetworks.com/about-us/default

4. Chivers T (2019) Does Psychology Have a Conflict-of-Interest Problem?
   Scientific American. Reports that a review of sixty papers by prominent
   psychologists, naming Carol Dweck among researchers who earn paid speaking
   and consulting fees, found that almost all either declared no competing
   interest or omitted a disclosure statement altogether, and that psychology's
   disclosure norms treat such income differently than medical journals would.
   Source: https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/does-psychology-have-a-conflict-of-interest-problem/

## 5. Conflict of Interest Checklist
- Is the source funded by an organization that benefits from promoting certain
  outcomes. Source 1 is academically funded with no disclosed industry or
  foundation sponsorship. Source 2 is funded by government science and health
  agencies and by private education-reform philanthropies, including the
  Raikes Foundation and the Bezos Family Foundation, both of which have a
  programmatic and reputational interest in mindset-style interventions
  succeeding, even though neither profits commercially from the finding.
- Does the source have professional affiliations that might bias the
  information. Yes for source 2. Carol Dweck, a co-author, is also the
  originator of the theory under test and a co-founder of a company that
  monetizes it, a dual role not disclosed as a competing interest in the paper
  itself.
- Are there commercial relationships between the source and entities
  mentioned. Yes. Source 3 documents that Dweck co-founded Mindset Works,
  which sells growth-mindset training and assessment products to the same
  schools that are the intended audience for the research findings she
  co-authors.
- Has the source been involved in controversies or retractions. No retraction
  of either source 1 or source 2 was found. The National Study of Learning
  Mindsets design and its heterogeneous, subgroup-concentrated effect have
  drawn methodological debate in the wider literature, but no correction or
  retraction resulted.

## 6. Balance Check
The strongest case for growth mindset interventions: source 2 is the largest,
most rigorous, preregistered field experiment on this claim, run on a
nationally representative sample, and it found a genuine causal effect,
concentrated among lower-achieving students, delivered through a single
low-cost session.
The strongest counter-argument: source 1, pooling 273 studies and 365,915
participants, found the average intervention effect across the whole research
base is small enough that mindset accounts for roughly one percent of the
variance in achievement, meaning the version of the claim marketed to general
audiences, that changing mindset broadly transforms performance, overstates
what the accumulated evidence supports outside a narrow, disadvantaged
subgroup.
Both bodies of evidence are current, peer reviewed, and neither has been
retracted. The disagreement concerns how far a real, narrow, subgroup effect
can be generalized into the broader claims made by commercial growth mindset
training, not data integrity.

## 7. Micro-blog Post

HEADLINE: Growth Mindset Training Is a Multimillion-Dollar Industry. The
Average Effect Is Close to Zero.

LEAD: The largest randomized field experiment on growth mindset found a real
but narrow benefit for struggling students, while the largest meta-analysis of
the whole evidence base found the average effect across all students is close
to zero, a gap the training industry rarely mentions.

FINDINGS:
- A meta-analysis of 273 studies and 365,915 participants found growth
  mindset interventions raised achievement by only 0.08 standard deviations
  on average, with mindset explaining about one percent of the variance in
  achievement overall [Source: Sisk et al., 2018, Psychological Science]
- The largest randomized trial on the claim, in a nationally representative
  sample of United States ninth graders, found a single under one hour
  online intervention modestly raised grades and advanced course enrollment
  only among lower-achieving students, with no effect for higher-achieving
  students [Source: Yeager et al., 2019, Nature]
- That trial was funded by the National Science Foundation, the National
  Institutes of Health, and private foundations, not by a company selling
  mindset training [Source: Yeager et al., 2019, Nature]

FUNDING AND CONFLICT OF INTEREST:
Both studies carry public or philanthropic funding, not corporate sponsorship.
Carol Dweck, originator of growth mindset theory and a co-author of the 2019
trial, co-founded Mindset Works, which sells growth-mindset training to
schools, a financial stake in the theory's popularity not foregrounded in her
academic biography. A 2019 Scientific American review found psychologists'
paid speaking and consulting income, of which mindset trainers are one
example, is rarely disclosed as a competing interest, unlike in medicine.

ALTERNATIVE INTERPRETATION:
Mindset researchers argue that a real, replicated effect concentrated in the
students who need it most, delivered at near-zero cost, is a meaningful
public-health-style intervention even though its average size across all
students is small, since small averages are expected whenever a benefit is
concentrated in one subgroup rather than spread evenly.
