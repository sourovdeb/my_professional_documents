For years I called myself lazy. I'd plan a full day, get through one task, and
spend the rest of it ashamed. The word "lazy" felt true because it was the only
word anyone had given me.

It was the wrong word.

Lazy means you *could* but you *won't*. Depleted means you *want to* and the tank
is empty. They look identical from the outside — a person not doing the thing —
so the world reaches for the cheaper explanation. We learn to reach for it too,
and aim it at ourselves.

Here's how I finally told them apart. Lazy feels light; you'd rather do something
easier and more fun. Depleted feels heavy; even the fun thing is too far away.
When I'm lazy, a small push gets me going. When I'm depleted, the push just adds
guilt to exhaustion, and I sink lower.

The difference matters because the fix is opposite. For lazy, you raise the
stakes: a deadline, a friend waiting, a reward. For depleted, you *lower* them.
You eat something. You drink water. You walk ten minutes. You do the smallest
possible version of the task and call it a full day — because on an empty tank,
showing up at all is the whole achievement.

I built my life around that second case, because that's the one I actually live
in. My minimum day is one sentence. Not a page, not a chapter — one sentence. On
a good day I write five hundred words. On a hard day I write the first line and
close the laptop with my streak intact and my self-respect untouched.

That sounds like lowering the bar. It isn't. The standard for the writing stays
high; the *quantity* flexes with the energy I actually have. A streak I keep at
one sentence beats a brilliant plan I abandon by Thursday.

The cruel trick of depletion is that it disguises itself as a character flaw. So
you punish the tiredness, which makes it worse, which looks like more laziness,
which earns more punishment. Naming it correctly breaks that loop. Not lazy.
Depleted. One needs a push; the other needs fuel and rest.

If you've been calling yourself lazy, try the other word for a week. Ask, each
time you stall: *push, or fuel?* You'll be surprised how often the honest answer
is fuel — and how much kinder, and more effective, the right response turns out
to be.
