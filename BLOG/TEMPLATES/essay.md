# Essay Title Goes Here

## Opening Hook (1-2 sentences)
Start with something that makes them care. A story, a question, a surprising fact.

Example: "When my therapist asked me to describe depression, I said it's like writing with wet sand for a pen."

## Introduction (2-3 sentences)
Set up what you'll explore. What's the problem? Why does it matter?

---

## Main Body (2-5 sections)

### Section 1: Idea or Problem
Explain the first part of your argument. Use concrete examples.

**Key principle:** Active voice. "You can do this" not "This can be done."

### Section 2: Evidence or Example
Give a real example. From your life, from research, from others.

Include sources if you cite specific studies or stats:
- "According to [source], [claim]."
- "Research shows [finding] ([cite source])."

### Section 3: Deeper Insight
Go deeper. What's the underlying pattern or lesson?

### Section 4: Practical Takeaway
So what? How can your reader use this?

---

## Closing (2-3 sentences)
Return to the hook. Show how the reader's understanding has changed.

Example: "I used to think real writers worked despite depression. I learned they work *with* it."

---

## Tone Guide

### ✅ DO THIS:
- Active voice: "You can automate this" not "This can be automated"
- Real language: "I was terrified" not "Fear gripped my soul"
- Contractions: "I've learned" not "I have learned"
- Short sentences: Mix lengths. Short ones hit hard.
- Your voice: Write like you talk (but edited)

### ❌ AVOID:
- Passive voice: "It is known that..." (ugh)
- Jargon: Explain it if you use it
- Flowery language: "As the dawn broke o'er the hills..." (no)
- Hedging: "I think maybe possibly" (just say it)
- Second-person advice: "One should consider..." (no, YOU should)

# How Automation Saved My Sanity

I spent six months sending the same email to 50 companies. Every Monday, I'd open my inbox, copy-paste the template, change the company name, and send.

By week 24, I wanted to scream.

That's when I realized: If something is repetitive and draining, automate it.

## The Problem

With bipolar II, I have cycles. Some weeks I'm writing 5,000 words before breakfast. Other weeks, I can't stare at a screen.

My old system required me to show up consistently. Send emails Monday. Call contacts Thursday. Post on social media daily.

On depression weeks? Impossible.

I'd skip tasks, feel guilty, miss opportunities, spiral deeper.

## The Solution: Automation

I couldn't change my bipolar cycles. But I could change the system.

I started small: A Python script that scrapes job boards and emails me results. Now, job hunting is automated.

Then: Email templates + Gmail API for batch drafts. Now, outreach doesn't drain me.

Then: Google Sheets synced to my GitHub. Now, tracking is automatic.

**The magic:** Tasks that used to kill 10 hours per week now take 10 minutes.

And I only do them when I have energy.

## What This Actually Means

On high-energy weeks, I write essays and research. Automation handles busywork.

On low-energy weeks, I publish pre-written content. Automation sends the "hey, new post!" emails.

On baseline weeks, I review opportunities and reach out. Automation found the opportunities already.

## The Results

- Fewer missed deadlines (automation doesn't forget)
- Less guilt (depression doesn't stop the work)
- More energy for creative work (no busywork)
- Better opportunities (consistent outreach happens)
- Better mental health (sustainable pace)

## Start Small

You don't need advanced coding. Start with:
- Gmail filters to auto-sort emails
- Google Sheets formulas to auto-calculate
- IFTTT or Zapier to connect tools
- Cron jobs for recurring tasks

Automate one repetitive thing. See how it feels.

Then automate the next.

---

The best productivity hack isn't discipline. It's eliminating the tasks that don't matter so you have energy for the ones that do.

For me, that meant letting the robots send emails while I write essays.
