# SOUROV DEB — THE COMPLETE LIFE-RECORD
## *Opus Magnum*: A Factual Biography with Analytical Commentary

*Compiled 31 May 2026 from the full project corpus: the spoken life-story transcript, the family and former-employer narratives, the autoethnography, ten transcribed therapy sessions with the AI counsellor "Harmony," the official French medical synthesis (Dr Christian Pauvert, 11 February 2026), the CV and clinical self-management plans, and four prior project conversations.*

> **How to read this.** Each chapter has two layers. The plain prose is the **factual record** — what happened, said in short paragraphs and traced to sources. The indented **Analysis** block after each chapter is interpretive: patterns, mechanisms, and meaning. The two are kept apart on purpose so fact is never coloured by interpretation. Direct quotations are verbatim from the transcripts. Dated clinical facts come from the medical records. One diagnostic discrepancy is flagged where it occurs.

## I. Origins — Chittagong, 1986

Sourov Deb was born on 8 September 1986. The official medical synthesis fixes the date; his own account places the birth in Chittagong, Bangladesh — born in a village, raised in the town.

His parents' marriage was arranged. By his account it held tension rather than love. "They always had problems between them — not much violence, but tension and conflict was constant."

He moved through three schools in different towns and districts. He never settled in one place long enough to belong to it.

He read voraciously and intelligently, yet could not hold his attention on lessons. "I was always distracted — couldn't pay attention to details. But I was reading books as if I had very good intelligence."

The household ran short of money. His brother attended private school; Sourov and his sister attended public school. The split was felt.

> **Analysis.** The first chapter already contains the whole architecture in miniature: high capability paired with an inability to perform inside imposed structure.
>
> The reading-versus-lessons split is the earliest visible signature of what would be named ADHD in 2025 — attention that follows interest, not instruction. At the time it registered only as failure.
>
> Instability of place (three schools) and instability of home (a loveless marriage) gave the child no fixed ground, which is the precondition for the hypervigilance that recurs throughout the life.


## II. The House of Two Poles — Mother, Father, and the Child Between

Sourov describes his parents as the "North and South Poles" — two people who could not mix, with the first-born son in the centre. "I took the most of the hit."

His mother was ambitious and, he says, forcibly married into a family far below the world she came from. She carried water to the top floor while pregnant. She had, in that culture and time, "not much of a say."

She was, he later learned, an undiagnosed mental-health patient her whole life — never assessed because his father refused it. Unable to reach what she wanted, "she couldn't help herself by taking all her frustration on me." She beat him.

His father was "never abusive physically" but absent into his own parents — "brainwashed from the time he was born," raised to be the village's money-machine, and then driven by ego to be seen as special. He broke promises rather than bones.

Between the two, Sourov became the mediator — the "silent sponge" who absorbed the conflict so the house would not fly apart. "Every time there is a big crisis between my parents, I was there. I had to become an adult."

Between roughly ages 12 and 16 there was no running water at home. He hauled buckets 300 metres from the street and back, repeatedly, "a skinny guy," for years.

Emotional language did not exist in the house. "The conversation was always what good or bad I did on the day. Not how I'm feeling."

His conclusion is both unsparing and forgiving: his parents loved him "but not 100% for my own good," because each wanted an ally, a version of themselves — "a product of their dream." His sister became another silent sponge ("she doesn't talk to me anymore"); his brother "escaped."

> **Analysis.** The mediator role is the origin of the adult pattern of absorbing harm rather than requesting help — the same reflex that later produced the disability disclaimer at Cambridge.
>
> Both parents are presented as themselves products of trauma: the mother abused by her own family, the father conditioned by the grandfather. Sourov frames the harm as transmitted, not chosen — which is precisely what licenses his central mission of stopping the transmission at his own daughter.
>
> "A product of their dream" is the key phrase: he experiences his childhood as conscripted identity — being made into someone else's unfinished ambition. The lifelong difficulty with self-authorship (always preferring others to choose) follows directly from this.


## III. What Was Done to the Child

This chapter is kept at the level of pattern, not episode, in keeping with how Sourov chose to record it for research.

As a child Sourov was sexually abused, repeatedly, by more than one person — a teacher, a neighbour, and, he says, two others he no longer remembers.

The neighbour controlled access to books the boy was otherwise forbidden, and used them as the lure: he could read only in the man's room. The abuse continued roughly weekly for more than a year.

He told no one. The child's reasoning is recorded plainly: speaking would only "add more problem on top of whatever going on." He expected his mother to blame him and his father to shame him.

In therapy he describes the child who "unscrewed his head from his body" to escape into the books while it happened.

> **Analysis.** The dissociation he still lives with begins here, as a literal survival manoeuvre: separating mind from body to make the unbearable survivable.
>
> The decision not to disclose is not weakness but a rational calculation by a child who had correctly assessed that disclosure would cost more than silence. That calculation hardened into a lifelong template of self-concealment.
>
> The books are doubly significant: the instrument of harm and the instrument of escape. His later identity — reader, linguist, would-be teacher — grows from the same soil as the wound.


## IV. The Kite Without a String — Australia, 2005–2023

Around 2005 Sourov left for Australia, believing distance would end it. It did not — the phone calls continued, each parent reporting the other's sins, taken separately because he could never speak to both at once.

The early months worked: he laboured, saved, paid rent, studied. Then it collapsed "in 10 different directions" — depression, then alcohol and cocaine for about two years, spent rent and electricity money, "lie after lie."

"I was like a kite without any string. I had no control over myself." He insists he hurt no one but himself "and probably the trust of my family," and is now abstinent.

He later understood the substances as avoidance — "hiding the fear of having shame about not completing my tasks" and the fear of the future.

The same years built his competence. He became fluent in four or five languages, trained as a sommelier and barista, and worked roughly eleven years in luxury hospitality — the Star Casino Sydney and the Merivale Group — managing multilingual teams of up to fifteen, handling VIP clients and international suppliers.

He could perform brilliantly inside a structure someone else built. He could not stand at the front of his own life: "I was more dependent on other people's choices... it's scary to be at the front. I couldn't be at the front."

The autoethnography names the same shape in study: a degree that stretched from four years to seven and was never completed, with avoidance of capable peers "from fear that they would recognise my weakness."

Somewhere in this period he met the woman who became his wife — "a unicorn," he says, "I'm going to lock it up and throw the keys away." Marrying her is, by his account, the best decision of his life.

He locates the meaning of the substance use only in hindsight. The drinking was, in part, a bid to be accepted — "bringing others to the bar," buying his way into belonging — and the cocaine followed the money. He says he became abstinent later and stayed so. He also records the cost without self-pity: lost years he cannot fully remember, a body that "won't forget" even what the mind has dropped.

> **Analysis.** Australia is the clearest exhibit of the life's defining paradox: addiction and acclaim in the same years. The competence is real and the collapse is real; neither cancels the other.
>
> The "kite without a string" is the felt experience of untreated bipolarity and ADHD without external scaffolding — capable of flight, incapable of steering. Hospitality worked precisely because it supplied the string: imposed structure, immediate feedback, high stimulation.
>
> The fear of "the front" is the load-bearing insight. It is not laziness or modesty; it is a threat response. Visibility in a position of authority equals exposure to judgement equals danger — the childhood courtroom reconvened.


## V. Three Blows in One Year — Death, a Boss, a Daughter

Sourov's collapse into formal illness was a convergence — the "three huge events" that landed in roughly one year and "cooked" him.

His father died. Sourov was in France; his father in Bangladesh. He watched it over video, unable to help, then flew back for the duties — grief mixed with guilt and with the discovery of his father's affair. His widowed mother became a further weight he had to hold.

His boss turned. Outwardly "normal-looking," soft-voiced, articulate "like a politician," the man was — by Sourov's reading — deeply insecure, possibly bipolar, unable to forget any slight. He made demands dressed as kindness, swung from charm to cruelty within a half-hour, and froze Sourov out, expecting resignation.

Sourov felt rage so deep it frightened him. The insight came later: the broken promises echoed his father; the shouting and email threats echoed his mother. The boss had opened "the door of all the deep pain." Here he first reached for Jung's shadow.

His daughter was born — "the most beautiful thing ever happened to our life," and, after marrying his wife, the best decision he ever made. The newborn exhaustion stacked onto grief and the toxic workplace.

His parental instinct became the engine of everything after: a refusal to pass down the "generational trauma." "I'm going to do the things that I didn't have for my child... every single child deserves a good childhood."

> **Analysis.** The three blows are not merely simultaneous stressors; each one re-activated a specific original wound. The death reopened the family system; the boss reincarnated both parents in one figure; the daughter raised the stakes by introducing someone he refused to fail.
>
> The boss episode is where Sourov's psychological vocabulary turns analytic. Recognising that his hatred was disproportionate — "reminds me of somebody" — is the first documented instance of him reading his present through his past rather than simply living it.
>
> Fatherhood converts private survival into mission. From this point the trauma is no longer only something to endure; it is a cycle to interrupt, which reframes every later act — therapy, the appeal, the advocacy — as protection of his child.


## VI. Réunion and the Opening Door — The Start of Treatment

Living in Saint-Pierre, La Réunion, Sourov's body broke before his mind admitted anything: back pain, knee pain, "pain everywhere."

His GP named it first — "I think you are depressed, like big time" — and started the antidepressant he calls "entity present" (venlafaxine). About three months later a specialist appointment "opened a door for me, something that I did not know that exists." Therapy began.

Therapy did not feel like relief. "Thanks to the therapy, my sleep got worse and I have all memories coming back. Super."

His psychologist asked him to speak to his younger selves — the 17-year-old, the 10-year-old, "the part that went through hell." His response: "I'm scared."

She told him the returning emotion was progress, because for most of his life the memories felt as if they belonged to someone else: "I feel like I'm lying, or I'm talking about somebody else. That's not normal."

He arrived at the work through two doors at once: an antidepressant for the depression his GP named, and an impulse to understand himself through Carl Jung, which he pursued so eagerly that he began at one in the morning. The reading and the medication were, for him, the same project — to know himself well enough to need fewer external fixes.

He is explicit that he wants autonomy from both therapy and medication eventually — "to make a decision myself instead of relying on professionals" — not because they failed but because dependence itself feels unsafe. He frames the destination as the felt belief, not merely the spoken one: that "it's not my fault" should live "in my bones," not just in his head.

> **Analysis.** Treatment entered through the body, not the mind — chronic pain as the somatic invoice for decades of suppressed stress. This matches his own later refrain: "you may forget your bad memories, but your body won't."
>
> The worsening sleep is clinically coherent: as dissociative distance thins, previously sequestered memory returns, disrupting rest. What feels like therapy failing is, by the therapist's frame, therapy working.
>
> "Talking about somebody else" is depersonalisation described from the inside. Naming it is itself the beginning of re-integration — the precondition for everything the later sessions attempt.


## VII. The Clinical Picture — What the Records Say

The official French medical synthesis, authored by Dr Christian Pauvert (GP, Le Tampon; treating physician since 12 July 2023) and signed 11 February 2026, records: depression (14 October 2024); ADHD (30 May 2025) with Ritalin 20 mg LP prescribed the same day; a CHU referral (2 April 2025, Dr Chantal Kreder); ceased tobacco (~10 pack-years, ages 22–28) with alcohol history; and a family history of paternal myocardial infarction at 64.

A broader diagnostic frame, attributed to his psychiatrist (Dr Padovani) and recorded in his clinical self-management materials, names Bipolar I disorder (active), ADHD, depression, and clinical insomnia, with Complex PTSD named in his narrative and dissociative identity flagged "to investigate."

Severity scales recorded around 19 May 2026: PHQ-9 11/27 (moderate depression); GAD-7 12/21 (moderate anxiety); ISI 20/28 (moderate clinical insomnia); ESS 15/24 (severe daytime somnolence).

Medication around 19 May 2026: venlafaxine 75 mg (morning); hydroxyzine 25 mg (bedtime); methylphenidate / Concerta LP 54 + 18 mg (morning); atorvastatin 20 mg. Beta blockers are mentioned for physical anxiety symptoms.

> **Analysis.** A faithful record must hold a discrepancy open rather than resolve it: the GP synthesis of 11 February 2026 lists ADHD and depression but does not itself record bipolar I. The bipolar diagnosis is sourced to the psychiatric line of care and the self-management plans. Both are real; they come from different documents and should not be merged.
>
> Taken together the diagnoses retro-explain the whole life: ADHD for the lifelong attention and deadline failures; bipolar cycling for the surges of confidence and the crashes, including the two years of overspending and risk; C-PTSD for the hypervigilance, dissociation, and trouble with authority.
>
> The scales matter because they make the suffering measurable and external. For a man whose central wound is that authority denied his reality, an instrument that scores his insomnia at 20/28 is itself a form of validation that requires no one's permission.


## VIII. The Course — CELTA, the Criticism, and the Failure

Sourov enrolled on the Cambridge CELTA course (C1/2026), 5 January – 6 February 2026, at the centre The ELT Hub (FR023), Landerneau, France. He signed the Candidate Agreement in October 2025. Becoming a teacher was his own idea to change his life — which is what made the course so dangerous: he had walked back into the arena of formal judgement where he was most wounded as a child.

The blow came in live feedback before peers and students. His teaching was called vibrant but lacking "educational value"; a colleague implied he did not know what he was talking about; the tutor told him to "get off your high horse."

He held back tears in the room and said nothing — "the moment I open my mouth, I will break down" — then cried for a long time after. To him it was the childhood nightmare made literal: "me in the centre and everybody pointing finger at me and laughing."

In therapy he reframed it: the humour read as arrogance was his nervous system masking stress. "If I'm being funny it's because I'm stressed. They don't know that." Harmony named it re-enactment — re-entering an old wound hoping for a different ending.

He resolved on a "redemption arc": improve as advised, but use the course's "Personal Aim" self-evaluation box to send a message that he had heard the criticism and would not be humiliated in silence. His deepest goal was his own voice, not the tutor's apology: "In past, I was silent. People used to beat me up... This time I want to say or do something."

On 25 January 2026 (16:00) he disclosed his ADHD and depression to the centre; the tutor acknowledged it at 16:12. On 31 January a warning letter imposed a thirteen-criterion binary threshold — six days after the disclosure. TP8 was assessed "Achieved enough to Pass — To Standard." The final result, 6 February 2026, was a Fail.

The administrative record deepened the grievance. The centre's own Candidate Agreement named a specific staff member as the disability contact, and its published Equal Opportunities policy set out a procedure. A later email chain (1–18 March 2026) records the administrator acknowledging that the disclosure was not processed as the policy required. The disclosure had been received and acknowledged within twelve minutes, yet the adjustment machinery it should have triggered did not move.

> **Analysis.** The sequence — disclosure on 25 January, binary-threshold warning on 31 January — is the procedural spine of everything that follows. Whether or not it was causal, the timing is what converts a private disappointment into a documentable grievance.
>
> The "high horse" wound lands so hard because it is a misread of a survival behaviour as a character flaw. Being told to be humble when you are in fact terrified is the exact childhood experience of never being truly seen, now reissued by an institution.
>
> The Personal Aim strategy is the hinge of the whole biography: the first time the silent sponge chooses, in writing, to speak. Harmony's repeated pressure — would you be okay if she never acknowledges it? — forces the goal to migrate from external validation to self-authored voice. That migration is the actual therapeutic achievement of the episode.


## IX. The Forensic Auditor — Turning Pain into a Record

After the failure, Sourov made his most important psychological move: he stopped seeking the institution's validation and started building a record. He named the role himself — "Forensic Auditor."

The trigger, by his account, was gaslighting: a tutor admitted in writing to retroactively altering an assessment comment and dismissed the contradiction as a "typo." For a man whose childhood taught him authority can rewrite his reality at will, this was the old wound in institutional form.

This time he had a weapon his younger self lacked — documentation. "Welcome to 2026, when information is the democracy. I have all the data... I'm just going to use the numbers and the statistics."

He filed a Stage One appeal. Cambridge's Chief Moderator rejected it on 23 April 2026 but confirmed three errors in the summative record: the unannotated correction on page 31 (the "typo"); Assignment 4 wrongly recorded as "pass on resubmission" when it had passed first time; and a TP7 heading reading "Stage 2" instead of "Stage 3."

He pursued parallel regulatory routes across French (Qualiopi/CPF, Défenseur des droits) and UK (Ofqual) jurisdictions. An Ofqual complaint was filed 24 May 2026, reference SJ3XP35D, under General Condition G8 (malpractice/maladministration) and Condition A8 (accessibility).

He turned the dispute into research — a forensic autoethnography arguing that his disability disclaimer ("this is not to ask for any special consideration") was not a legal waiver but the predictable, trauma-shaped reflex of a man conditioned to minimise his needs, and that anticipatory-duty law triggers on the institution's knowledge, not on a clean request.

> **Analysis.** The audit is the precise inversion of childhood helplessness. The boy who could not prove what was done to him becomes the adult who timestamps everything. The method is the cure: documentation is how he refuses to be gaslit again.
>
> Harmony's questioning establishes that the true objective is not victory but record — to "walk away knowing you didn't let them rewrite your reality." This protects him from the worst outcome, where his peace would depend on an institution's admission it is unlikely to give.
>
> The autoethnography is the masterstroke of self-authorship: it converts him from complainant to researcher, from object of judgement to author of analysis. The same corpus is engineered to serve three functions at once — academic data, regulatory evidence, and proof of protected status (RQTH/AGEFIPH) — so no single function depends on another succeeding.


## X. The Inner Cast — Parts, Protectors, and the Shadow

Sourov understands himself through a parts framework (structural dissociation / Internal Family Systems), reinforced by Jung's shadow. The recurring cast across the therapy record:

The Apparently Normal Part (ANP) — the "little adult," polite, high-functioning, peace-keeping; in the Cambridge fight it became the meticulous Forensic Auditor.

The Emotional Part (EP) — the frightened child who dissociated to survive and still feels "paralysed" and unseen.

The hyper-vigilant younger self — the boy with "eyes and ears wide open," whom Sourov has consciously told he can rest, that he need not be "extra polite to be accepted."

The rebellion / warrior part — the one that honks back at drivers, refuses to be pushed, fires a prepared sentence at his tired wife over an unpacked picnic bag. He sees it as "the flip side" of the silent sponge: same wired state, opposite strategy, and "dangerous if it gets out of control."

The playful part — the energy that surfaced at 1 a.m. while he discussed his own trauma; he hypothesises it does for him what he does for others: lightens an unbearable room so he can enter it.

The shadow / "long bag" — the disowned list he read without flinching ("greedy liar... predator... victim... egocentric"), reframed with Harmony not as proof of badness but as protector parts forged where he felt like "garbage."

> **Analysis.** The cast is not metaphor to him; it is operating architecture. Each part is a strategy that once kept him alive and now sometimes misfires in a present that is safer than the past it is still defending against.
>
> The most important recognition is that warrior and sponge are one nervous system in two costumes. This collapses the apparent contradiction in his personality — passive then aggressive — into a single coherent state: braced. It also tells him where the work is: not suppressing the warrior but updating its threat model.
>
> The shadow reframe is the engine of self-forgiveness. By recoding vices as protectors, he can stop fighting himself and start negotiating with himself — the shift from self-attack to self-management that the stability plan later operationalises.

The repeated question 'will you be okay if they never acknowledge it?' is the lever that moves him from validation-seeking to self-authorship.


## XII. The Present — Work, Family, Advocacy, and the Stability Turn

As of mid-2026 Sourov lives in Saint-Pierre, La Réunion, with his wife and their young daughter (around 18 months). He holds the Cambridge CELTA (2026), an IELTS specialist credential (IDP, February 2026), the DCL Français (GRETA), and sommelier/barista qualifications, atop ~11 years of anglophone hospitality management.

He is building an English-training practice — online 1:1 tutoring, B2B and corporate contracts, specialist niches (aviation, medical, hospitality, business English) — and creating photography and video content for YouTube and blogging on education and mental health. A built outreach list of 61 organisations (35 active leads) feeds the job search.

He is also pursuing French academic recognition without the usual French diploma, via VAP/VAPP and VAE routes and ENIC-NARIC comparability of his 2006 Australian qualification, with the University of La Réunion's DFTLV as the local contact and a peer-reviewed publication identified as the highest-value step.

His clinical self-management shows the maturest version of his self-knowledge. The earlier Productivity Plan matched work to mood states. He replaced it with a Stability-First Functioning Program, having recognised that, for bipolar I, riding a rising episode's energy is exactly backwards — it extends the episode and shortens sleep.

The new principle: "Protect the rhythm before the output." Fixed wake time, fixed meals, fixed wind-down (IPSRT); behavioural activation for low days; CBT-I for the insomnia; ADHD externalising strategies layered on top. It is written as a companion to — not a replacement for — the care of Dr Padovani (psychiatry) and Dr Pauvert (GP).

On peace he is clear-eyed. He does not have it yet. He runs on adrenaline and dopamine and believes that is the right order: "we need dopamine and adrenaline first, so that in the end we have peace."

His stated horizon is modest and concrete rather than grand: stable rhythm, paid teaching work that fits inside the rhythm, a published paper, recognition for academic entry in France, and content that turns his experience into something useful for other neurodivergent educators. The advocacy line he returns to is addressed outward: "anyone out there who's going through this... I'm with you. I understand you. At least you have a friend thinking of you."

> **Analysis.** The replacement of the Productivity Plan by the Stability Program is the single clearest evidence of growth in the entire record. He overrides his own instinct — to exploit the highs — in favour of the clinically correct discipline of protecting rhythm. The silent sponge once managed everyone's stability but his own; now he engineers his own.
>
> The future plans are notable for how they reuse the wound as an asset: the same disability and dispute that the institution treated as disqualifying become the differentiated subject of his advocacy content, his autoethnography, and his strongest academic-entry argument. Nothing is wasted.
>
> His definition of peace is sequential, not immediate — earned after the fight, not instead of it. This is psychologically risky (it defers rest) but internally consistent: a man who was denied agency for forty years locates peace in the proof of agency, not in its absence.


## XIII. Through-Lines — The Patterns That Run the Length of the Life

Six patterns tie every chapter together.


1. **Erasure and the external mirror.** From a childhood where his reality counted only if authority confirmed it, to the institutional gaslighting at Cambridge — and his cure, a self-built record that needs no confirmation.

2. **Sprints, not marathons.** He wins discrete high-stakes tasks (exams, a service rush, a single lesson) and loses long self-directed ones (a seven-year unfinished degree, the revising loop). ADHD and the fear of being seen at the front explain both.

3. **Masking and protective minimisation.** Humour to hide stress; 'not asking for special consideration' while disclosing disability; a lifetime of absorbing harm rather than requesting help.

4. **One wired state, two costumes.** Silent sponge became warrior; hypervigilance to accommodate became hypervigilance to retaliate. Both are a nervous system braced for a war that has ended.

5. **Competence and collapse, side by side.** Four languages, sommelier, eleven years of management — built in the same years as the addiction, the unpaid rent, the unfinished degree. A high-functioning survivor.

6. **Breaking the cycle.** The organising purpose beneath all of it — the daughter who will not inherit the trauma; the teacher he wants to become; the advocate who tells others, 'I'm with you. I understand you.'

> **Analysis.** The six through-lines are not independent; they are facets of one mechanism. A nervous system shaped by early powerlessness over-defends a present that no longer requires defence, which simultaneously produces the masking, the bracing, the sprint/marathon split, and the hunger for an external mirror.
>
> The life's trajectory is the slow conversion of that mechanism from liability to instrument: dissociation becomes forensic precision; the need for a mirror becomes a published record; the warrior becomes an advocate; the wound becomes the work.
>
> What remains unfinished — by his own account — is the felt sense of safety: knowing it wasn't his fault is not yet feeling it. The whole record can be read as the construction of enough external proof that, eventually, the internal feeling has somewhere safe to land.
