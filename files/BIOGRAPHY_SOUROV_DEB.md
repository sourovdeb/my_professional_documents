## 1. Origins — Chittagong, 1986

Sourov Deb was born on **8 September 1986**. The official French medical synthesis of 11 February 2026 records the date and sex precisely; his own life-story transcript places the birth in **Chittagong, Bangladesh** — born in a village, then raised in the town. His parents' union was an **arranged marriage**, and by his account it was never a marriage of love. "They always had problems between them," he says — "not much violence, but tension and conflict was constant."

He moved through **three different schools** across different towns, never settling. He was, by his own later understanding, an intelligent child who could not attend to detail: "I was always distracted — couldn't pay attention to details. But I was reading books as if I had very good intelligence." Decades later a diagnosis would name what the boy could not: ADHD. At the time he was simply the child who failed at the things his parents most wanted him to win.

## 2. The House of Two Poles

Sourov describes his parents as the **"North and South Poles"** — two people who could not mix, with the first-born son caught in the centre. "I took the most of the hit," he says.

**His mother** was ambitious and, by his account, forcibly married into a family far below the social world she came from. She was, he later learned, effectively an unofficial mental-health patient her whole life — never formally diagnosed because his father would not permit it. She carried water to the top floor of the building while pregnant. She had no real say, in a culture that, as he puts it, gave wives "not much of a say" and then blamed the woman when things went wrong. She was herself traumatised by her own family. Unable to reach what she wanted, "she couldn't help herself by taking all her frustration on me." She beat him. He remembers it plainly.

**His father** was "never abusive physically," but absent into his own parents and his own family of origin — "brainwashed from the time he was born" by a grandfather Sourov remembers with contempt, raised to be a money-machine for the village. Having clawed his way to the power to feed that village, the father then needed the village to see he was someone special — "a huge stubborn ego. That's a bad mix." He broke promises rather than bones.

Between these two opposites the boy became the mediator — the **"silent sponge"** who absorbed the conflict so the household would not fly apart. "Every time there is a big crisis between my parents, I was there. I had to deal with everything. I had to become an adult." From around age 13, by his account, he was functionally an adult. There was no water in the home for long stretches of his youth; between roughly **ages 12 and 16** he hauled buckets the 300 metres from the street and back, again and again — "a skinny guy," doing it for years.

Emotional language did not exist in the house. "The conversation was always what good or bad I did on the day. Not how I'm feeling." His conclusion, reached only in adulthood, is unsparing and oddly forgiving at once: his parents loved him, "but not 100% for my own good," because what each wanted was not a son but an **ally** — a part of themselves, a friend who would say yes. He was, he says, "a product of their dream."

His **sister** was, he believes, another silent sponge — "I know it now because she doesn't talk to me anymore." His **brother** "kind of escaped." Of himself he says: "It took me 40 years almost to take an independent decision without any fear. 40 years."

## 3. What Was Done to the Child

This chapter is kept factual and brief, at the level of pattern rather than episode, in keeping with how Sourov himself chose to record it for research.

As a child Sourov was **sexually abused**, repeatedly. In the life-story transcript he names multiple perpetrators — a teacher, a neighbour, and two others he says he no longer remembers. The neighbour, who controlled access to books the boy was otherwise forbidden, abused him **roughly weekly for more than a year**, using the books as the lure: he could read them only in the man's room. Sourov's father considered buying books a waste of money, which left the boy dependent on exactly the person harming him.

He told no one. The reasoning of the child is recorded clearly, and it is the seed of a lifelong pattern: speaking would only "add more problem on top of whatever going on." He expected his mother to say it was his own fault and his father to say he should have protected himself. So he absorbed it silently — the same survival strategy he used for everything else. In therapy he describes the child who "unscrewed his head from his body" to escape into books while it happened: the beginning of the dissociation that would shadow his adult life.

## 4. The Kite Without a String — Australia

"What I decided is that I'm just going to get out of this country." Around **2005** Sourov left for **Australia**, believing distance would end it. It did not: the phone calls continued, each parent reporting the other's sins, "one hour or two hours of therapy from them" per call, taken separately because he could never speak to both at once.

The arc he describes is steep in both directions. At first he did well — working hard, saving, paying rent, studying. Then it collapsed in "10 different directions": depression, **alcohol and cocaine for about two years**, spent rent and electricity money, "lie after lie." "I was like a kite without any string. I had no control over myself." He is explicit that he hurt no one but himself "and probably the trust of my family," and that he is now **abstinent**. He later understood the substance use as avoidance — a way of "hiding the fear of having shame about not completing my tasks" and the fear of the future.

The contradiction that defines him is already visible here. While his academic and self-directed life fell apart, he was acquiring real, hard skills: he became **fluent in four or five languages**, trained as a **sommelier and barista**, and worked his way into **luxury hospitality** — by the CV's account around **11 years** with the **Star Casino Sydney and the Merivale Group**, managing multilingual teams of up to fifteen people, handling VIP clients and international suppliers. He could perform brilliantly inside a high-pressure structure someone else built. What he could not do was stand at the front of his own life: "I was more dependent on other people's choices. I was very happy about that because it's scary to be at the front. I couldn't be at the front." The autoethnography names the same shape in his studies — a degree that stretched **from four years to seven and was never completed**, with avoidance of capable peers "from fear that they would recognise my weakness."

Somewhere in this period he met the woman who would become his wife. He calls her, without irony, **"a unicorn"** — "I'm going to lock it up and throw the keys away." Marrying her, he says, is the best decision of his life.

## 5. Three Blows in One Year

Sourov's collapse into formal illness was not one event but a convergence — what he calls the **"three huge events"** that landed in roughly a single year and "cooked" him physically and mentally.

**His father died.** Sourov was in France; his father was in Bangladesh. He watched it unfold over video, unable to help, then flew back to discharge the family duties. Grief mixed with guilt, and with the discovery of his father's affair, which he also had to manage from a distance. His mother, now widowed, became another weight: she had lost the husband who had managed every family affair, and Sourov became the one holding the siblings and the mother together "so that your mother doesn't become alone."

**His boss turned.** The autobiographical note on the employer is one of the most detailed things Sourov wrote. The man was outwardly "normal-looking," soft-voiced, articulate "like a politician" — and, Sourov came to believe, deeply insecure, possibly bipolar, incapable of forgetting any slight. He made demands dressed as kindness, leveraged knowledge of employees' private lives, swung from "the coolest guy ever" to breaking people over a dry leaf on the floor within the same half-hour. Sourov was "convoked," frozen out, expected to resign. He felt a rage so deep it frightened him — "so deep that I" wanted to hurt him physically. The crucial insight came in hindsight: the boss's broken promises echoed his **father**; the shouting and email threats echoed his **mother**. "What my mom did to me was never personal — doesn't make it right though." The boss had opened "the door of all the deep pain." This is where he first reached for Jung's idea of the **shadow**.

**His daughter was born.** "She is the most beautiful thing ever happened to our life," and, after marrying his wife, the best decision he ever made. But the newborn fatigue and late nights stacked onto grief and the toxic workplace. His **parental instinct** became the engine of everything that followed: a refusal to pass the **"generational trauma"** down. "I'm going to do the things that I didn't have for my child... every single child deserves a good childhood."

## 6. Réunion and the Opening Door

Living in **Saint-Pierre, La Réunion**, Sourov's body broke before his mind admitted anything was wrong: back pain, knee pain, "pain everywhere." It was his GP who named it first — "Ah, my friend, I think you are depressed, like big time" — and started him on the antidepressant he refers to phonetically as "entity present" (**venlafaxine / Effexor**), about eight months before he recorded the family narrative. Roughly three months after that, an appointment opened "a door for me, something that I did not know that exists." Therapy began.

Therapy did not feel like relief. "Thanks to the therapy, my sleep got worse and I have all memories coming back. Super," he says, bitterly. His psychologist asked him to **speak to his younger selves** — the 17-year-old, the 10-year-old, the part that "went through hell." His response was two words: **"I'm scared."** But she also told him the returning emotion was a sign he was moving in the right direction, because for most of his life the memories had felt as if they belonged to **someone else** — "I feel like I'm lying, or I'm talking about somebody else. That's not normal."

## 7. The Clinical Picture — what the records say

The **official French medical synthesis** (Synthèse médicale), authored by **Dr Christian Pauvert** (GP, Le Tampon, La Réunion; treating physician since 12 July 2023) and signed **11 February 2026**, records the following:

- **Depression** — recorded 14 October 2024.
- **Attention-Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder** ("Hyperactivité avec déficit de l'attention") — recorded 30 May 2025, with **Ritalin 20 mg LP** prescribed the same day.
- **Hospital/specialist referral** — 2 April 2025, to Dr Chantal Kreder (CHU).
- **Tobacco** — ceased (≈10 pack-years, smoking from age 22 to 28), with alcohol history noted.
- **Family history** — father suffered a myocardial infarction at 64.

Sourov's own narrative and his clinical self-management plans add a further, broader diagnostic frame, attributed to his **psychiatrist, Dr Padovani**: **Bipolar I disorder, ADHD, depression, and clinical insomnia**, with **Complex PTSD** named in the life-story transcript. As of May 2026 his stated medication list is **venlafaxine, Concerta (methylphenidate), hydroxyzine, and atorvastatin**, with **beta blockers** mentioned for physical anxiety symptoms.

> **Discrepancy flagged.** The GP synthesis of 11 Feb 2026 lists ADHD and depression but does **not** itself record bipolar I disorder; the bipolar diagnosis appears in Sourov's narrative and in the productivity/stability plans as attributed to his psychiatrist. A faithful record notes both and does not collapse them: the GP document is authoritative for what it contains; the bipolar diagnosis is real to his treatment planning but is sourced separately. The two are not in conflict, but they come from different documents.

The diagnoses, taken together, retro-explain the whole life: ADHD for the lifelong inability to hold structured attention and the last-minute submissions; bipolar mood cycling for the surges of confidence followed by collapse and the two years of overspending and risk; C-PTSD for the hypervigilance, the dissociation, the trouble with authority. "Looking at my diagnostic today, I know exactly why."

## 8. The Course — CELTA, the Criticism, and the Failure

In late 2025 Sourov began the **Cambridge CELTA** teacher-training course (delivered through a centre referred to as The ELT Hub), with a signed Candidate Agreement dated **28 October 2025**. Becoming a teacher was, in his words, his own idea to change his life — which is exactly what made the course so dangerous to him. He had voluntarily walked back into the one arena, formal education under judgement, where he had been most wounded as a child.

The blow came in a **live feedback session in front of his peers and students**. His teaching was called vibrant and likeable but lacking "educational value"; a colleague said, in effect, that he had no idea what he was talking about; and the tutor told him to **"get off your high horse."** He held back tears in the room and said nothing — "the moment I open my mouth, I will break down." Then he cried for a long time afterward. To him it was the childhood nightmare made real: "me in the centre and everybody pointing finger at me and laughing."

In therapy he and Harmony reframed it: the humour and vibrancy the tutor read as arrogance were in fact his **nervous system masking stress** — "if I'm being funny it's because I'm stressed. They don't know that." The episode was **re-enactment** — unconsciously re-entering an old wound in the hope of a different ending. He resolved on a **"redemption arc"**: to do better as the tutor recommended, but also to use the course's **"Personal Aim"** self-evaluation box to send a message — that he had heard the criticism, would act on it, and would not be publicly humiliated in silence. His deepest stated goal was not her apology but his own voice: "In past, I was silent. People used to beat me up... This time I want to say or do something."

The course nonetheless ended in a **Fail**. By his account he completed **120/120 hours**, passed all four written assignments, and had teaching practice (TP8) graded "To Standard" — yet the final verdict was failure. The prior project index records the disputed mechanics: a feedback shift within roughly 72 hours, a retroactive amendment to a portfolio comment, and a centre-imposed pass threshold he says was not in the published syllabus.

## 9. The Forensic Auditor

The single most important psychological move of this whole period is the one Sourov made after the failure. He stopped seeking the institution's validation and started building a **record**. He named the role himself: **"Forensic Auditor."**

By his account the trigger was an act of **gaslighting** — a tutor, he says, admitted in writing to retroactively altering an assessment comment and dismissed the contradiction as a "typo." For a man whose entire childhood taught him that authority can rewrite his reality at will, this was the old wound in institutional form. But this time he had a weapon his younger self never had: **documentation**. "Welcome to 2026, when information is the democracy. I have all the data... I'm just going to use the numbers and the statistics."

He framed the goal precisely, and Harmony tested it hard. It was **not** to make Cambridge admit error. It was to **create an undeniable public record** so he could "walk away knowing you didn't let them rewrite your reality" — to **fire them as the judges of his worth.** He filed a Stage One appeal (the prior index dates it 6 March 2026; three procedural errors were later confirmed), pursued parallel regulatory routes across French (Qualiopi/CPF) and UK (Ofqual) jurisdictions, and turned the entire dispute into research — a **forensic autoethnography** in which his own life history becomes the data that explains why a disabled candidate, disclosing his condition, added the words "this is not to ask for any special consideration." His argument: that disclaimer was not a waiver of legal protection but the predictable, trauma-shaped reflex of a man conditioned since childhood to minimise his own needs — and the law's anticipatory duty triggers on the institution's *knowledge*, not on a clean request. (An Ofqual complaint was filed 24 May 2026, reference SJ3XP35D.)

Whether or not the appeal ever succeeds, the function of the audit is psychological as much as legal. "I want to prove to my younger self that his reality can never be erased again."

## 10. The Inner Cast — Parts, Protectors, and the Shadow

Sourov understands himself through a **parts** framework (structural dissociation / Internal Family Systems), reinforced by Jung's shadow. The recurring cast across the therapy record:

- **The Apparently Normal Part (ANP)** — the "little adult," polite, high-functioning, peace-keeping; in the Cambridge fight it became the meticulous Forensic Auditor.
- **The Emotional Part (EP)** — the frightened child who dissociated to survive, who still feels "paralysed" and unseen.
- **The hyper-vigilant younger self** — the boy with "eyes and ears wide open," whom Sourov has consciously told he can rest now, that he need not stay in fight-or-flight or be "extra polite to be accepted."
- **The rebellion / warrior part** — the one that honks back at drivers, refuses to be pushed, fires a prepared sentence at his tired wife over an unpacked picnic bag. Sourov sees clearly that this is "the flip side" of the silent sponge: same wired state, opposite strategy. He fears it "could be dangerous if it gets out of control."
- **The playful part** — the energy that showed up, strangely, at 1 a.m. while discussing his own trauma; he hypothesises it does for him what he does for others: lightens an unbearable room so he can enter it without shutting down.
- **The shadow / "long bag"** — the disowned material he listed without flinching ("greedy liar phony... predator... victim... egocentric"), which he and Harmony reframed not as proof of badness but as **protector parts**, survival strategies from a world where he felt like "garbage" or "a product of a dream."

The unifying image is his own: a **ship with a broken compass**, sailing brilliantly in bad weather because that is all it ever learned, never toward a destination of its own choosing — "still dressed for a war that ended years ago."

## 12. The Present — Work, Family, Advocacy, and Peace

As of mid-2026 Sourov lives in **Saint-Pierre, La Réunion** with his wife and their young daughter (around 18 months). He holds the **Cambridge CELTA** (2026), an **IELTS specialist** credential (IDP, Feb 2026), the **DCL Français** (GRETA), and sommelier/barista qualifications, on top of his ~11 years of anglophone hospitality management. He is building an English-training practice — online 1:1 tutoring, B2B and corporate contracts, specialist niches (aviation, medical, hospitality, business English) — and creating photography/video content with a view to **YouTube and blogging on education and mental health**. A built outreach list of 61 organisations (35 active leads) feeds the job search.

His own clinical self-management plans show the most mature version of his self-knowledge. The earlier **Productivity Plan** matched work to mood states. He then **replaced it** with a **Stability-First Functioning Program**, having recognised that, for bipolar I, riding a rising episode's energy to maximise output is exactly backwards — it extends the episode and shortens sleep. The new principle: **"Protect the rhythm before the output."** Fixed wake time, fixed meals, fixed wind-down; behavioural activation; CBT-I for the insomnia; ADHD externalising strategies layered on top. It is written explicitly as a companion to — not a replacement for — the care of **Dr Padovani (psychiatry)** and **Dr Pauvert (GP)**.

On peace, he is clear-eyed. He does not have it yet. He is running on adrenaline and dopamine, and he believes that is the correct order: "we need dopamine and adrenaline first, so that in the end we have peace." Peace, for him, is not the tutor's apology. It is the moment he has proven — to his daughter's future, and to his own younger self — that his reality can no longer be erased by anyone's authority. "At least you have a friend thinking of you," he says, to anyone who has been where he has been.

## 13. Through-Lines

Six patterns run the entire length of the life and tie every chapter together:

1. **Erasure and the need for an external mirror.** From a childhood where his reality didn't count unless an authority confirmed it, to the institutional gaslighting at Cambridge — and his cure, the self-built record that needs no external confirmation.
2. **Sprints, not marathons.** He wins discrete high-stakes tasks (exams, a service rush, a single lesson) that give an unassailable identity, and loses long self-directed ones (a seven-year unfinished degree, the revising loop). ADHD and the fear of being *seen* at the front explain both.
3. **Masking and protective minimisation.** Humour to hide stress; "not asking for special consideration" while disclosing disability; a lifetime of absorbing harm rather than requesting help.
4. **The same wired state, two costumes.** Silent sponge → warrior. Hypervigilance to accommodate became hypervigilance to retaliate. Both are the nervous system braced for a war that has ended.
5. **Competence and collapse, side by side.** Four languages, sommelier, 11 years of management — built in the same years as the addiction, the unpaid rent, the uncompleted degree. He is, in his phrase, a high-functioning survivor.
6. **Breaking the cycle.** The organising purpose beneath all of it — the daughter who will not inherit the generational trauma; the teacher he wants to become; the advocate who tells others, "I'm with you. I understand you."
