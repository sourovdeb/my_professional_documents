# When Cambridge Failed a Disabled Trainee — And What I Did Next

I am not writing this to win. I am writing this because no one told me I had rights, and someone else is sitting in the same room I was in, believing the same lie: that asking for what you are owed means causing trouble.

In October 2025 I enrolled in a Cambridge CELTA course — the gold-standard teaching qualification for English language educators. I disclosed my conditions: bipolar disorder, ADHD, complex PTSD. I then added a sentence I now understand as the most telling thing I have ever written: *"This is not to ask for any special consideration."*

A psychiatrist would call that trauma-adapted minimisation. A lawyer might call it a waiver. The truth is simpler: I had spent forty years learning that my needs were trouble, and the reflex was faster than the reasoning.

I completed all 120 teaching hours. I passed all four written assignments. My final teaching practice was graded "to standard" — the passing grade. Then, within 72 hours, the result changed. An official assessment document was retroactively amended. When I raised the contradiction in writing, I was told the amendment was a "typo."

That word — typo — did a great deal of work. It converted a substantive change to an assessed outcome into a clerical error too trivial to discuss.

I know this pattern. I grew up with this pattern. I know what it feels like when an authority tells you that what you experienced did not happen, and tells you this with complete calm and institutional weight behind them. The childhood name for it was survival. The adult name for it is gaslighting.

What I did next is what I should have done immediately: I built a record.

I filed a Stage One appeal. Three procedural errors were confirmed. I filed a complaint to Ofqual — the UK's education standards regulator — on 24 May 2026 (reference SJ3XP35D). I also filed under French consumer protection frameworks, since the course was sold and delivered in France. I am not a lawyer. I am a man with a folder and a timestamp.

The legal argument is narrow but solid: the duty of reasonable adjustment under the UK Equality Act 2010 and its French equivalent triggers on an institution's *knowledge* of a disability — not on a clean, formally worded request. My disclosure created that knowledge. The disclaimer I added did not extinguish my rights; it illustrated exactly why anticipatory adjustment is necessary for disabled candidates who have been conditioned to apologise for their own needs.

I do not know how this ends. I know I am not alone in this room.

If you are a disabled trainee in a certification programme and something has shifted in a way that makes no sense on paper: you have rights. They do not disappear because you said you were not asking for special consideration. They do not disappear because the institution is prestigious. Document everything. Keep every email. The timestamp is your witness.

That is the only thing my childhood did not prepare me for — and the only thing that, finally, I have.
