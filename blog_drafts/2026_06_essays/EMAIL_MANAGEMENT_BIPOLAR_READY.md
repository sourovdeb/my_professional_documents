# Email Management When Bipolar: Three Rules That Actually Work

**By Sourov Deb | June 2026**

---

## Hook

Depression makes email pile up for weeks. Mania makes you reply to everything at 3 AM and regret it by morning. I've lost jobs over both. I've also, over the last 18 months, discovered three rules that actually work—not willpower, not productivity hacks, but structural barriers that protect you when your mood can't.

---

## Context

Email is survival infrastructure. It's how employers, collaborators, and clients reach you. It's how you send a CV, negotiate a contract, ask for help. But bipolar disorder warps your relationship with it.

According to the Mayo Clinic, bipolar I disorder includes manic episodes where judgment is impaired—you oversleep, you hyperfocus, you miss urgent messages. During depressive episodes, initiating any communication feels impossible; the inbox becomes a weight. The SAMHSA (Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration) notes that untreated mood disorders significantly affect decision-making across all life domains, including communication—and the consequences are real. I've been fired. I've destroyed professional relationships in writing. I've also used email silence as avoidance when opportunities were sitting in my inbox waiting for a reply.

The problem isn't character. It's neurobiology. The solution isn't discipline. It's structure.

---

## The Three Rules

### Rule 1: The 24-Hour Delay

**The rule:** Never send an email when you feel rushed, emotional, or certain. Write it, save as draft, close the app. Wait 24 hours. If you still want to send it tomorrow at the same time, send it.

**Why it works for bipolar people:**
- During mania: You feel absolutely right; 24 hours later you see the recklessness.
- During depression: You feel hopeless; 24 hours later you see it was an overreaction.
- During mixed states: Rage + hopelessness; 24 hours clarifies which was real.

**Real example from my life:**
In January 2026, I was in a mixed episode during my CELTA course. A tutor gave me feedback I experienced as humiliation (she used the phrase "get off your high horse"). My first draft reply was 300 words of point-by-point rebuttal, sarcasm, and implied threat. I saved it as draft. The next morning—still angry, but clearheaded—I rewrote it: a single paragraph acknowledging her feedback, committing to the changes she requested, and stating (without accusation) that I had experienced the session as humiliating. That version was professional. The first version would have ended my teaching career before it started.

**How to use it:**
1. Write the email emotionally, completely, holding nothing back.
2. Read it aloud.
3. Save as draft. Do not send.
4. Set a phone reminder: "Review [recipient name] email tomorrow at [time]."
5. Tomorrow: Read it fresh. Edit if needed. Then send—or delete if you realise it was the mood talking.

**For urgent situations** (job application, time-sensitive reply):
If the deadline is within 24 hours, write the email, read it aloud three times, have someone you trust read it, *then* send. Same protection, faster.

---

### Rule 2: Folder Triage

**The rule:** Create a folder called "Requires Action." Everything else gets left in Inbox or archived. Once a week, spend 30 minutes on the Action folder. Everything else can wait, or doesn't need a reply.

**Why it works for bipolar people:**
- During depressive episodes: You can't face 200 emails. But you can face 8. Triage cuts the overwhelm by 95%.
- During manic episodes: You want to reply to everything immediately. Triage forces you to batch work and actually finish it.
- During normal periods: You see clearly what actually needs you, and what's just noise.

**How to set it up** (Gmail):
1. Create a label: "Action Required"
2. Create a filter: any email with words "offer," "apply," "urgent," "deadline," "decision needed" automatically gets the label
3. Flag emails manually that need your response
4. Archive everything else (it's searchable; you can find it)
5. Set a weekly 30-minute block (I use Sunday 6 PM): open the Action folder, work through it, clear it

**Real example:**
During my depressive episode in April 2026, I had 650 unread emails. I couldn't open the inbox. But I created the Action folder and labelled only the 12 that actually needed something from me: job applications with deadlines, a client waiting for tutoring confirmation, insurance matters. I worked through those 12 over two weeks. The other 638 were noise—newsletters, notifications, FYI messages that don't need me.

**What you actually do in the Action folder:**
- Replies to humans (clients, collaborators, employers)
- Applications with deadlines (not browsing; applying)
- Medical appointments (scheduling, confirmations)
- Family logistics
- Financial/legal documents that need action

**What you *don't* put there:**
- Marketing emails
- Notifications
- Newsletter digests
- "FYI" messages from colleagues
- Anything you're just "keeping for reference"

---

### Rule 3: Voice First, Writing Second

**The rule:** When you can't write (depression, fatigue, brain fog), voice-record your reply first. Listen to it. Then write it. This forces you to think through what you actually mean before committing to text.

**Why it works for bipolar people:**
- Writing is hard when depressed. Speaking is easier.
- You catch impulsive phrasing when you hear it aloud (you'd miss it reading).
- A 3-minute voice memo prevents a 30-minute email-writing spiral.
- You sound more human in the written version because you thought it through.

**How to do it** (using your phone):
1. Open Voice Memos (iPhone) or Google Recorder (Android)
2. Press record
3. Say out loud: "Hi [name], I'm replying to your email about [topic]. Here's what I want to say: [speak naturally, don't edit yourself]"
4. Stop recording
5. Listen back once
6. Now write the email in your own voice—shorter, cleaner, but with the same core message

**Real example:**
I used this during CELTA. I had to email the course administrator about the assessment dispute. I was dysphoric and angry—a terrible combination for writing. I voice-recorded myself explaining (verbally) what happened, what I wanted, and why. Listening to it, I heard myself being repetitive and slightly accusatory. I edited the written version to be concise and factual. The written email was 40% shorter, 100% more professional, and actually got action.

**The science:**
According to research in the *Journal of Affective Disorders*, people with bipolar disorder who externalise their thinking (say it aloud, write it down, record it) make better decisions because the external representation slows down impulsive cycles. You hear yourself. You think differently.

---

## Takeaway

Email is not character. It's infrastructure. If your infrastructure breaks, you don't become a bad person—you become someone whose email reflects a broken system. These three rules aren't about willpower. They're about making it structurally harder to send a regrettable email and easier to send a good one.

If you manage bipolar disorder: pick one rule this week. The 24-hour delay works for most people. Notice what changes. You'll likely discover that email isn't your weakness—it's just the first place where your mood becomes visible to others. Protecting that one system protects your whole professional life.
