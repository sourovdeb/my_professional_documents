## Abstract

This forensic autoethnography examines assessment irregularities in Cambridge CELTA certification through systematic analysis of one candidate's complete documentary record, including the Stage One Independent Reviewer's appellate report of 23 April 2026. Four intersecting procedural failures are identified: a written health disclosure on 25 January 2026 engaged the anticipatory duty under Equality Act 2010 sections 20–21 and the centre's signed Candidate Agreement, neither of which was discharged; a warning letter issued six days post-disclosure imposed a centre-defined threshold not derived from any Cambridge Syllabus provision; the summative assessment record was amended after formal candidate challenge without formal annotation, an irregularity acknowledged in the appellate report but not assessed against Ofqual General Condition G8; and the Stage One appellate process applied an asymmetric evidentiary threshold, accepting the centre's undocumented assertions at five identified points while declining to engage primary source documents submitted by the candidate. Cambridge's own data confirms a completion-based fail rate of approximately 0.4%, making the outcome a profound statistical outlier. Implications for candidate rights, anticipatory disability duty in training contexts, and regulatory oversight of dual-jurisdiction qualification appeals are discussed.

**Keywords:** appellate evidentiary asymmetry; assessment validity; CELTA; disability accommodation; forensic autoethnography; Ofqual G8; teacher certification

## Introduction

The English language teaching market is valued at approximately ninety-five billion United States dollars in 2026 and is projected to reach one hundred and twenty-two billion by 2030 (Research and Markets, 2026). Within this expanding ecosystem, the Cambridge Certificate in English Language Teaching to Adults (CELTA) functions as a dominant gatekeeper qualification recognised by employers across more than seventy countries (Cambridge English, 2025). The qualification is awarded under licence by Cambridge Assessment English, an Ofqual-regulated awarding organisation, through a global network of accredited centres. The vast majority of CELTA candidates undertake the qualification in non-English-speaking contexts, including those countries where ELT demand is growing most rapidly and where local disability law, labour rights, and assessment cultures intersect with British regulatory frameworks. Questions of procedural fairness and candidate accommodation therefore carry implications extending well beyond any individual case.

CELTA's 120-hour intensive assessment framework was formalised under the RSA/Cambridge CTEFLA in 1988, bearing a continuous institutional lineage to International House London's 1962 precursor certificate (IH World, 2024). This framework has undergone no substantive structural reform in approximately three decades. Hobbs (2013), in a peer-reviewed empirical study published in *ELT Journal*, demonstrates that the core nature of CELTA, perhaps more relevant in earlier periods of the discipline's development, is increasingly incongruous with the changing global ELT market and calls for significant structural changes. From a practitioner perspective, Thornbury (2012), who completed the original International House course in 1975 and subsequently became a CELTA trainer, questions whether the four-week model's monopoly on initial teacher training is justified and asks whether alternative pathways deserve greater professional credence.

Existing research on CELTA focuses primarily on aggregate success rates, comparative certification studies, and graduate employment outcomes. Very little research examines the operationalisation of anticipatory disability duty in intensive teacher training, post-course document alteration and its regulatory implications, centres imposing assessment criteria beyond the awarding body's published authority, the dual-jurisdiction complexity inherent in courses funded through France's Compte Personnel de Formation operating under a United Kingdom assessment framework, or the appellate process's own evidentiary integrity. Survivorship bias (Elton, Gruber, & Blake, 1996) compounds these gaps: candidates who experience procedural failure typically lack access to the institutional documents needed to mount a sustained challenge, with the consequence that cases involving complete forensic documentary records remain exceptionally rare in the literature.

A few researchers have addressed CELTA's structural limitations as a teacher education programme. There have been limited studies concerned with the procedural integrity of CELTA's appellate framework. There is no published research on the appellate evidentiary threshold's symmetry across the centre and the candidate as parties to the same appeal. Therefore, this research focuses on documenting one complete case in which the appellate process is itself analysable as a primary source, and examining what this single case reveals about the regulatory architecture of dual-jurisdiction professional training.

The objectives of this research are to address five research questions:

(1) **RQ1:** What contractual and statutory obligations were engaged by the candidate's health disclosure on 25 January 2026, and how does the documentary record show the centre responded?

(2) **RQ2:** How does the centre's warning letter of 31 January 2026 align with the Cambridge Syllabus's holistic assessment requirement, and on what published authority were its specific conditions imposed?

(3) **RQ3:** What contradictions exist between the candidate's documented performance trajectory and the Fail recommendation under the Cambridge holistic assessment framework?

(4) **RQ4:** What evidence exists of post-course document alteration, and what are its implications under Ofqual General Condition G8?

(5) **RQ5:** How did the Stage One appellate process apply the documentary evidence threshold to the centre and the candidate respectively, and is that application consistent with the impartiality requirement of Ofqual Condition I4?

The research contributes a methodologically rigorous three-lane forensic framework distinguishing procedural-legal, assessment-validity, and documentary-integrity analyses; the first published case study extending this analysis to the appellate process itself; an empirically grounded statistical contextualisation of CELTA Fail outcomes; and a systematic mapping of the dual-jurisdiction regulatory architecture governing CPF-funded CELTA delivery in France.

## Literature Review

### Assessment Validity and Construct-Irrelevant Variance

Messick (1989) established that validity is "an overall evaluative judgment of the degree to which empirical evidence and theoretical rationales support the adequacy and appropriateness of interpretations and actions based on test scores" (p. 13). Two major threats to construct validity are relevant to the present study: construct underrepresentation, where the assessment fails to capture important dimensions of the construct, and construct-irrelevant variance, where factors outside the construct systematically distort outcomes (Messick, 1989, p. 5). Sadler (1989) demonstrated that vague or inconsistently applied criteria allow assessment to function as gatekeeping rather than measurement, arguing that valid assessment requires criteria to be applied accurately and consistently across all candidates. The present study extends Messick's framework by documenting two mechanisms of construct-irrelevant variance: first, the imposition of additional centre-defined criteria following a health disclosure; and second, post-hoc record alteration following formal candidate challenge. A third mechanism, identified for the first time in this study, operates at the appellate level: the asymmetric application of the documentary evidence threshold to the two parties of the appeal.

### Anticipatory Disability Duty in Training Contexts

The Equality Act 2010 (United Kingdom), section 20, establishes the duty to make reasonable adjustments where a provision, criterion, or practice puts a disabled person at a substantial disadvantage. The duty is anticipatory: it is engaged by the institution's knowledge of disability and is not contingent upon a formal request from the disabled person (Equality Act 2010, Schedule 13). The Equality and Human Rights Commission Code of Practice (EHRC, 2015) clarifies at paragraph 127 that "employers and service providers cannot wait until a disabled person approaches them before they consider their duty." Schedule 13 paragraph 4 further provides that competence standards may be maintained without adjustment, but ancillary provisions — including planning support, documentation format, and time allocation — are not competence standards and remain subject to the duty.

Within French jurisdiction, Code du travail Article L.5213-6 imposes on training providers the obligation to take "mesures appropriées" (appropriate measures) to enable disabled trainees to access training. This obligation is also anticipatory in operation. Qualiopi Indicateur 26, governing all certified training providers in France, requires the provider to "mettre en œuvre les moyens nécessaires à l'accueil et à l'accompagnement des personnes en situation de handicap" — implement the necessary means for the reception and support of persons with disabilities. The convergence of these statutory and regulatory frameworks across the two jurisdictions, reinforced by contractual commitments in the centre's signed Candidate Agreement, creates an obligation framework with no opt-out contingent on candidate inaction.

### Cambridge Syllabus: Holistic Assessment and Progress Protection

The Cambridge Syllabus (2024), at page 10, establishes that the final grade is "a holistic assessment rather than an average of grades" and identifies six factors that the holistic assessment takes into account: development across the course, consistency across different lesson types, ability to manage the learning process, range of lesson types, different levels taught, and the amount of support received. The Syllabus also states that "weak lessons early in the course do not count against you if sufficient progress is achieved." The Performance Descriptors at Appendix 2 use explicitly permissive qualifiers — "generally," "some," "with a degree of success" — affirming that development areas are compatible with a Pass recommendation. Any centre-imposed threshold not derived from these provisions therefore operates outside the authority delegated to centres by the awarding body.

### Ofqual General Conditions and the Integrity of the Awarding Organisation

Ofqual General Condition G8 (Office of Qualifications and Examinations Regulation [Ofqual], 2025) requires regulated awarding organisations to take all reasonable steps to prevent the occurrence of malpractice or maladministration. Sub-condition G8.4 mandates a written policy for investigating concerns about malpractice. The Ofqual Handbook specifies that altering results documents falls within the definition of malpractice — a definition reproduced verbatim in Cambridge Assessment English's published Malpractice Procedure. Post-hoc alteration of summative records without annotation, particularly in temporal proximity to a candidate's formal challenge, renders the assessment record suspect under both the regulatory and the awarding body's own standards.

### Independence and Procedural Fairness in Awarding Organisation Appeals

Ofqual General Condition I4 requires that the appeals process operated by a regulated awarding organisation be independent and consider cases on their merits. The principle of procedural fairness in administrative law (Wade & Forsyth, 2014) requires that both parties to an appeal be held to a consistent evidentiary standard. The selective acceptance of undocumented assertions from one party, while requiring documentary proof from the other, constitutes a non-uniform application of the evidentiary burden which engages the impartiality requirement. The literature on educational appeals (Reid, 2017) has documented the structural tendency of internal review processes to validate rather than test the original decision, particularly where successive layers of review have access to previous conclusions before forming their own. The present study contributes the first detailed forensic analysis of this phenomenon at the appellate level in CELTA assessment.

### The 0.4% Completion-Based Fail Rate

Cambridge Assessment English's published data, reproduced in IH World (2024) citing Cambridge University English Assessment (2023), confirms that of candidates who complete the CELTA, 67.4% receive a Pass, 23.0% a Pass B, and 5.3% a Pass A — yielding a completion-based Fail rate of approximately 0.4%. This figure is more precise and more relevant than the commonly cited 5% Fail rate, which conflates candidate withdrawal (pre-completion) with Fail recommendations (post-completion). A Fail recommendation places a completing candidate in the bottom 0.4% of all completing candidates — approximately one in two hundred and fifty — an outcome of profound statistical rarity that requires evidentiary justification commensurate with its exceptionality. Survivorship bias (Elton et al., 1996) explains why such cases are systematically absent from both academic and professional literature.

### Critical Perspectives on CELTA's Fitness-for-Purpose

Hobbs (2013), in a peer-reviewed empirical study, demonstrates through analysis of trainee and experienced teacher data that CELTA's performance-based philosophy and short-course format are incongruous with modern ELT, particularly in its insufficient accommodation of different teaching contexts and its preference for native-speaker norms. Hobbs argues that more significant structural changes are needed, observing that accommodating these within a four-week course may be impossible and lending support to longer initial certification pathways. This peer-reviewed finding constitutes the scholarly basis for the structural critique applied in the present study.

These findings are contextualised by Thornbury (2012), a practitioner commentary published on Thornbury's own *An A–Z of ELT* blog. Writing on the fiftieth anniversary of the IH course from which CELTA descends, Thornbury reflects that it took him "many years to outgrow the four-week course" and asks whether the model's monopoly on initial ELT teacher training remains justified. While a blog post carries different evidentiary weight from peer-reviewed research, Thornbury's perspective is cited here as influential practitioner commentary from one of the field's most recognised voices, corroborating Hobbs's empirical conclusions from an insider perspective. Readers are invited to weigh it accordingly.

## Method

### Research Design: Forensic Autoethnography

This study employs forensic autoethnography (Denzin, 2014; Ellis, 2004; Sparkes, 2000), combining the insider access characteristic of autoethnographic research with the evidentiary rigour of forensic document analysis. Ellis (2004) defines autoethnography as "research, writing, story, and method that connect the autobiographical and personal to the cultural, social, and political" (p. xix). The forensic extension applies legal standards of evidence analysis to personal documents, treating them as exhibits subject to chain-of-custody verification and systematic contradiction mapping. The researcher served as participant-observer during a four-week intensive CELTA course at an accredited centre in France (January–February 2026) and as candidate-appellant in the subsequent Cambridge appeal proceedings. The analytical framework is structured in three lanes — procedural and legal, assessment validity, and documentary integrity — to maintain distinct evidentiary standards across different claim types. The Stage One appellate report (issued 23 April 2026) is treated as an additional primary source within Lane 3, allowing the appellate process itself to be subjected to forensic examination.

### Three-Lane Analytical Procedure

Documents were analysed using a three-lane forensic framework. Lane 1 (procedural and legal analysis) mapped documented events against contractual and statutory obligations, establishing which duties were engaged and what the documentary record shows was done in response. Lane 2 (assessment validity analysis) compared the candidate's documented performance trajectory against the Cambridge holistic assessment standard, applying Messick's (1989) construct-irrelevant variance framework. Lane 3 (documentary integrity analysis) identified record alterations, their chronological relationship to formal candidate challenge, and implications under Ofqual G8; this lane was extended to include the Stage One appellate report itself, examining the evidentiary threshold the report applied to each party. The three-lane architecture deliberately prevents conflation of legal, validity, regulatory, and appellate claim types.

### Ethical Considerations and Positionality

The researcher acknowledges dual positionality as both subject and analyst. All documents cited are primary records to which the researcher holds legal access as a party to the relevant contracts, correspondence, and proceedings. Institutional identifiers are retained where the centre's identity bears jurisdictional relevance to the analysis; individuals are referred to throughout by professional role rather than by name, reflecting both standard ethical practice in autoethnographic research involving non-consenting third parties and the heightened privacy considerations of French law (Code civil Art. 9). This study makes no findings about individual intention beyond what the documentary record directly supports. Concurrent regulatory proceedings before the Défenseur des droits, France Compétences, and the Commission Nationale de l'Informatique et des Libertés have been initiated; this research is independent of those proceedings and is presented for academic peer review.

## Findings

### Lane 1: Procedural and Legal Analysis

#### Health Disclosure and Engaged Obligations (RQ1)

On 25 January 2026, at approximately 16:00, the candidate disclosed in writing to the course tutor a history of hospitalisation, psychological trauma, and differences in cognitive processing (D8). The tutor responded within twelve minutes, acknowledging the disclosure. The candidate had been formally diagnosed with attention deficit hyperactivity disorder on 30 May 2025 — seven months prior to the disclosure — as an Affection de Longue Durée under Article L322-3 of the Code de la sécurité sociale (D10). Active medication included methylphenidate (Ritalin LP 20mg). Depression had been formally diagnosed on 14 October 2024 by the same physician; venlafaxine (37.5mg, escalated to 75mg) was active at the time of disclosure.

This single act of disclosure simultaneously engaged three overlapping obligations. First, the anticipatory duty to make reasonable adjustments under Equality Act 2010 sections 20–21, applicable by virtue of Cambridge Assessment English's status as a qualifications body within Schedule 13. Second, the parallel duty under Code du travail Article L.5213-6 and the Qualiopi Indicateur 26 quality framework, applicable by virtue of the centre's location in France and its Qualiopi certification. Third, the contractual duty in the signed Candidate Agreement (D3) to "discuss what can be put in place both before, during and after the course" through a named disability contact (the centre Director). All three are engaged by the institution's knowledge of a disability-relevant condition; their anticipatory character means no formal request from the candidate is required.

Documentary analysis of D8 and the subsequent record reveals no evidence that any of these obligations were discharged. The centre's written responses contain no reference to the accommodation provisions in the Candidate Agreement, no referral to the named disability contact, no request for further information about the candidate's needs, and no documentation of any adjustment considered or implemented. The Stage Three tutorial of 27 January 2026 — the first formal documented interaction between tutor and candidate following the disclosure, two days later — contains no reference to the disclosure in its written record (D5).

A post-course email from the centre Director confirmed that four categories of adjustment had been available throughout the course: document format, audio provision, additional tutor time, and assignment deadline extensions. None had been formally offered. On the same course, a colleague absent due to illness was accommodated with an additional lesson day. The Director's characterisation of CELTA as a "lockdown process" in which modifications cannot be made is therefore contradicted by the documentary record of the centre's own practice.

#### The Warning Letter: Centre-Defined Threshold Beyond Cambridge Syllabus Authority (RQ2)

Six days following the health disclosure, the centre issued a warning letter (D7) establishing a list of twelve specific criteria as conditions for a Pass recommendation. The Cambridge Syllabus (2024), at page 10, explicitly requires holistic assessment of the full performance trajectory and states that weak early lessons do not count against a candidate who demonstrates sufficient progress. The Performance Descriptors at Appendix 2 use permissive qualifiers throughout, explicitly accommodating development areas at Pass level. The warning letter cites no Cambridge Syllabus provision authorising a twelve-criteria binary threshold.

The CELTA Administration Handbook authorises the issuance of warning letters but does not prescribe their content. There is no published Cambridge provision requiring centres to issue warning letters listing individual criteria as binary pass conditions. The warning letter therefore represents a centre-imposed threshold not derived from Cambridge Syllabus authority. Whether this constitutes ultra vires action in the strict legal sense (Wade & Forsyth, 2014) is a question of administrative law beyond the scope of forensic documentary analysis; what the documentary record shows, with certainty, is that the threshold imposed cannot be located within Cambridge's published assessment framework.

The chronological relationship between the health disclosure (25 January 2026, D8) and the warning letter (31 January 2026, D7) is directly documented. The documentary record contains no centre communication between these dates addressing accommodation or adjustment. The warning letter therefore constitutes the centre's first substantive documented response to the disclosure — and that response imposed additional assessment criteria rather than considering any accommodation.

### Lane 2: Assessment Validity Analysis

#### Documented Performance Trajectory (RQ3)

The candidate's complete performance record (D4, D5, D6) shows: four of eight assessed teaching practices graded "to standard," including the opening lesson (TP1) and the final assessed lesson (TP8); all four written assignments at Pass level; "Some" support recorded throughout — the standard designation in the Cambridge framework, not an elevated intervention level; formal "to standard for this stage" assessment at the Stage Two tutorial; full attendance (120 of 120 hours); and the full range of lesson types completed at both required levels (A2 and B2). The TP8 feedback form (D4) records nine of twelve criteria as "met" (marked blue, the centre's coding for criterion met), with only three criteria remaining "not met" (3a, 5c, 5f).

The TP8 feedback form contains a closing tutor statement that includes the phrase: "I feel that you achieved enough to Pass, with significant progress on key areas." The CELTA 5 page 25 summary, written after TP8, qualifies this as a recommendation for Fail on holistic grounds. No contemporaneous document explicitly clarifies that the tutor's TP8 statement referred to the lesson alone rather than the course; the post-hoc interpretation was first articulated in the centre's response to the candidate's appeal in March 2026.

#### Internal Contradictions in the Fail Recommendation (RQ3)

Three independently documented facts stand in tension with the Fail recommendation. First, the final teaching practice was graded "to standard" with the explicit written tutor statement noted above. Second, the candidate's record on the four holistic factors specified at Cambridge Syllabus page 10 (excluding teaching practice and written assignments) is positive or neutral throughout: development is recorded as "great progress" at Stage Two and "significant progress" at TP8; support is at the standard "Some" level; range is the full set of lesson types; both required levels were taught. Third, the Chief Moderator's response to the appeal states there are "only two components of assessment on a CELTA course: teaching practice and written assignments" — a formulation that contradicts the Syllabus's six-factor framework and is not reconciled with that framework anywhere in the appellate record.

Under Messick's (1989) construct-irrelevant variance framework, these contradictions are consistent with the introduction of factors beyond demonstrated teaching competence into the final grade decision. The construct (teaching competence) was assessed positively at TP8 against nine of twelve criteria. The Fail recommendation's stated basis (the three remaining "not met" criteria) does not engage the holistic framework Cambridge itself prescribes.

### Lane 3: Documentary Integrity Analysis

#### Post-Course Record Alteration (RQ4)

Approximately five to six days following course completion, the candidate identified in writing an error on the summative assessment record (CELTA 5, page 31). The error was acknowledged by the tutor as typographical. The record was subsequently amended at the candidate's instigation. The Stage One appellate report (D11) confirms, on its own examination of the record, that "there is no evidence that the correction of the typing error on the summarising page (page 31) of the CELTA 5 booklet was formally annotated" and recommends that the centre consider formally annotating such corrections in future (D11, p. 9). Two further documented errors in the same primary summative record were also acknowledged by the appellate report: an Assignment 4 grade incorrectly recorded as "pass on resubmission" when it had passed on first submission, and a heading error in the TP7 feedback ("stage 2" rather than "stage 3").

Ofqual General Condition G8.1 requires awarding organisations to take all reasonable steps to prevent the occurrence of maladministration. The Cambridge Malpractice Procedure includes the alteration of results documents within its definition of maladministration. The absence of annotation means no independent party can verify whether the change was purely typographical or substantive in effect — precisely the evidentiary uncertainty G8 exists to prevent. The appellate report acknowledges the gap and treats it as advisory; it does not assess the acknowledged gap against G8 or against Cambridge's own Malpractice Procedure. The temporal relationship between the candidate's formal written challenge identifying the error and the subsequent amendment is also not addressed in the appellate report.

#### The Stage One Appellate Process and the Asymmetric Evidentiary Threshold (RQ5)

The Stage One appellate report (D11), issued by Cambridge Assessment English on 23 April 2026, constitutes a primary source document amenable to forensic analysis on its own terms. Examination of the report reveals a systematic pattern in the application of the documentary evidence threshold to the two parties of the appeal.

At five identified points, the centre's responses are accepted as determinative without citation of supporting documentary evidence:

1. *On Question 2 (criterion 4i at TP5):* The report states "the documented evidence supports the centre's initial response" (D11, p. 8). The centre's response acknowledges that the tutor "did not notice" the candidate's completed language analysis. The candidate's completed analysis (a primary source document) was not engaged with in the report's discussion.

2. *On Question 3 (post-final-lesson assessor moderation):* The report confirms a post-TP8 discussion occurred between tutors and assessor but identifies no document recording its format, participants, or the mechanism by which TP8's outcome was incorporated into the framework established at the prior assessor meeting of 30 January 2026. The assessor's reproduced report (D11, p. 18) contains observations for TP4 and TP7 only; no TP8 observation appears in the assessor's record.

3. *On Question 4 (holistic factors applied):* The centre's assertion that "all the mentioned elements were taken into account" is accepted without documentary support (D11, p. 6). The Chief Moderator's own formulation then introduces the "only two components of assessment" position that contradicts Cambridge Syllabus page 10.

4. *On Question 5 (criteria operating as absolute or holistic):* The report states "the documented evidence supports the centre's initial response" (D11, p. 8). The Cambridge Syllabus's qualifier language ("generally," "with a degree of success") is not engaged.

5. *On Question 6 (disability duty):* The candidate's own informal email disclaimer is cited as the operative basis for concluding the disability duty was not engaged. The centre is not required to produce any document demonstrating an accommodation review was conducted. The named disability contact in the Candidate Agreement is not addressed. The legal standard (EA 2010 s.20, EHRC Code of Practice 2015 ¶127) is not engaged.

At each of these five points, the candidate had submitted primary source documents that were either not engaged with or not addressed: Cambridge's own published CELTA5 (page 10, on holistic factors); the disclosure correspondence (D8); the completed TP5 language analysis (a primary source artefact); and the medical documentation (D10). The Independent Reviewer's conclusion that Cambridge "used procedures which were consistent with the regulatory authorities' code of practice; applied its procedures properly and fairly in arriving at judgements" (D11, p. 4) does not engage with the evidentiary asymmetry the report itself documents in its handling of the parties' respective submissions.

This pattern is consistent with the structural tendency of internal review processes documented by Reid (2017): successive layers of review have access to previous conclusions before forming their own, and the path of least resistance is endorsement. The pattern is not characterised in this study as evidence of intent. It is characterised as evidence that the asymmetric application of the evidentiary threshold operates as a third mechanism of construct-irrelevant variance — one that occurs at the appellate level and is therefore distinct from, and additional to, the variance mechanisms identified at the assessment level itself.

## Discussion

### Convergence of the Four Lines of Evidence

The four lines of evidence converge on a coherent pattern. A documented health disclosure on 25 January 2026 engaged statutory and contractual accommodation obligations that the documentary record shows were not discharged. Six days later, a warning letter imposed a centre-defined criteria threshold that cannot be located in Cambridge's published assessment framework. The final teaching practice was assessed "to standard" with explicit written confirmation of sufficient performance, and nine of twelve criteria were marked met; the candidate's record on the four holistic factors beyond teaching practice and written assignments is positive or neutral throughout. The summative assessment record was subsequently amended without annotation following formal challenge, an irregularity acknowledged but not assessed against the relevant regulatory standard. The Stage One appellate report applied the documentary evidence threshold asymmetrically across the two parties of the appeal.

Each finding is independently supported by primary documentary evidence; together they constitute a pattern in which the Fail recommendation is not adequately explained by the available record. The statistical context amplifies the significance considerably: a completion-based Fail rate of 0.4% means that a Fail recommendation is, by the awarding organisation's own data, an outcome of profound rarity. Such an outcome requires documentary justification commensurate with its statistical exceptionality. The candidate's documented performance — full attendance, full assignment pass, "to standard" final lesson, and progress affirmed across all six holistic factors — does not, on the available evidence, supply that justification.

### The Appellate Asymmetry as a Distinct Phenomenon

The asymmetric application of the documentary evidence threshold at the appellate level constitutes a distinct phenomenon worthy of theoretical attention. Existing literature on assessment validity (Messick, 1989; Sadler, 1989) is concerned primarily with the assessment moment itself: the construct, the rubric, the score. The present study suggests that construct-irrelevant variance can also be introduced by the appellate process — by the differential evidentiary threshold applied to the institution's account and the candidate's account of the same disputed facts. Where one party's undocumented assertions are accepted as determinative, while the other party's primary source documents are not engaged with, the impartiality requirement of Ofqual Condition I4 is engaged not in the abstract but as an empirically testable proposition about the report's own treatment of the record.

The implications for the regulatory architecture are significant. An appellate process whose evidentiary threshold is asymmetric does not merely fail individual candidates; it functions to insulate the assessment level from external scrutiny by validating decisions whose primary documentary basis cannot be confirmed. Where the appellate report itself becomes the regulatory mechanism by which Ofqual's General Conditions are deemed to have been complied with, an asymmetric appellate process undermines the very compliance it is meant to verify. This finding extends Messick's framework into the regulatory dimension and identifies a gap in the existing literature that warrants further empirical investigation.

### Alignment with Hobbs (2013) and Thornbury (2012)

The procedural findings of this study are contextually consistent with the structural critiques advanced by Hobbs (2013) and Thornbury (2012). Hobbs's empirical finding that CELTA's performance-based philosophy produces a "basic starter pack" mentality — favouring surface compliance over professional development — provides a theoretical basis for understanding why a multi-criteria checklist warning letter might emerge as institutional practice: when assessment is conceived in performance-based terms, reducing holistic judgement to countable criteria becomes a natural institutional response. Thornbury's (2012) practitioner observation about the limited methodological range of the four-week model is consistent with a centre culture that privileges compliance over contextualised professional formation.

Importantly, neither Hobbs (2013) nor Thornbury (2012) speaks directly to the procedural and documentary integrity failures documented in this study. The present study's evidential claims — disability obligations not discharged, centre-defined criteria not derived from Cambridge authority, record amended without annotation, appellate evidentiary threshold applied asymmetrically — stand independently on their documentary basis. The Hobbs and Thornbury sources contextualise the structural environment in which such failures may arise; they do not cause or prove them. This distinction between demonstrated findings and contextual framing is maintained throughout.

### Implications for Regulatory Oversight

The findings raise four implications for regulatory practice. First, Ofqual's G8 framework requires a mechanism for candidates to report and have investigated post-course document alterations independently of the centre under investigation — a structural conflict of interest that the current framework does not resolve. Second, the anticipatory nature of the Equality Act 2010 duty must be operationalised in centre training, accreditation auditing, and appellate review, not merely stated in candidate agreements; contractual acknowledgement of a duty does not constitute its discharge, and an appellate process that examines whether informal support was given does not substitute for examining whether the statutory duty was discharged. Third, the dual-jurisdiction complexity of CPF-funded courses operating simultaneously under French labour and quality-of-training law and United Kingdom qualification regulation creates a legal architecture in which neither jurisdiction's enforcement mechanism is straightforwardly applicable to the other's standards. Fourth, the evidentiary threshold applied within awarding organisation appeals processes warrants explicit articulation in published procedure: parties to an appeal should be on notice of the standard their submissions will be evaluated against, and that standard should be applied consistently to both parties.

### Limitations

This study is a single-case forensic autoethnography. Its demonstrated findings apply directly to the documented case; its broader contextual claims are explicitly framed as questions the case motivates, not as conclusions it establishes. The researcher's dual positionality demands interpretive discipline, and the three-lane framework is designed to enforce the distinction between what documents show and what the researcher experienced. The Stage Two appeal process and the concurrent French regulatory proceedings are ongoing at the time of submission; their outcomes are not incorporated into the findings presented here. Future research using systematic case sampling across multiple CELTA centres, jurisdictions, and candidate populations would be required to test whether the procedural patterns documented in this case are isolated or systemic. In particular, comparative analysis of multiple Stage One appellate reports — within the limits permitted by privacy and confidentiality — would allow empirical investigation of the evidentiary asymmetry hypothesis advanced in this study.

To conclude this discussion: the four converging lines of evidence, the statistical context of the 0.4% completion-based Fail rate, the structural critique provided by Hobbs and Thornbury, and the appellate evidentiary asymmetry identified for the first time in this study together constitute a case for expanded scrutiny of the procedural integrity of CELTA assessment in non-English-speaking jurisdictions and the appellate frameworks that govern it.

## Conclusion

This study has presented a forensic autoethnographic analysis of a CELTA assessment case across four converging lines of documentary evidence. Statutory and contractual accommodation obligations engaged by health disclosure were not discharged. A warning letter imposed a centre-defined criteria threshold not derived from any Cambridge Syllabus provision. The final teaching practice was assessed "to standard" with explicit confirmation of sufficient performance. The summative record was amended without annotation following formal challenge. The Stage One appellate process applied the documentary evidence threshold asymmetrically across the centre and the candidate. Cambridge's own data confirms a completion-based Fail rate of approximately 0.4%, making a Fail recommendation in these circumstances a profound statistical outlier requiring evidentiary justification the available documentary record does not provide.

The study makes four contributions. Methodologically, it demonstrates the utility of a three-lane forensic framework for separating procedural-legal, validity, and regulatory-integrity claim types in assessment autoethnography, and extends that framework to incorporate appellate analysis as a fourth dimension. Theoretically, it extends Messick's (1989) construct-irrelevant variance framework to encompass post-hoc documentary manipulation and asymmetric appellate evidentiary thresholds as distinct sources of systematic bias. Practically, it documents specific mechanisms by which duty-of-care obligations can fail in intensive certification contexts — a concern with direct relevance to the majority of CELTA candidates who undertake the qualification in non-English-speaking jurisdictions where dual-regulatory architectures govern provision. Empirically, it provides a single complete documented case against which broader structural patterns can be tested.

The contextual alignment with Hobbs's (2013) empirical findings and Thornbury's (2012) practitioner critique suggests that the procedural failures documented in this case may reflect broader structural tendencies in CELTA's performance-based, short-course model. Whether this alignment reflects a systemic pattern or a coincidence of single-case evidence requires the kind of multi-centre, longitudinal research that survivorship bias has historically made difficult to conduct. As the ELT market continues to grow in non-English-speaking contexts where dual-jurisdiction complexity is inherent to CELTA's operation, the adequacy of its procedural and appellate frameworks for protecting candidate rights becomes an increasingly consequential research and policy priority.

The most actionable implication of this study, for practitioners and regulators alike, is the need for explicit articulation and consistent application of the documentary evidence threshold within awarding organisation appeals. Where the threshold is applied asymmetrically — accepting one party's undocumented assertions while declining to engage the other party's primary source documents — the impartiality requirement central to Ofqual Condition I4 is compromised at precisely the regulatory layer where compliance is meant to be verified. Future iterations of awarding organisation procedure, in CELTA and in the broader regulated qualifications landscape, may benefit from explicit codification of evidentiary symmetry as a procedural standard.
