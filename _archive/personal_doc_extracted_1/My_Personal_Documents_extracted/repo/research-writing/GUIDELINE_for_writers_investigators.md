# AI Guideline for Reporters, Writers, and Investigators
## How to find, read, verify, structure, and break down a story — methods, tools, formats, logics, time, and the things that go wrong

**Derived from:** the two CELTA / Cambridge / The ELT Hub working sessions on 15 May 2026, plus the agent's own operational practice during them.
**Audience:** any writer whose work depends on finding, reading, and rendering nuanced material — beat reporters, investigative journalists, longform writers, policy analysts, academic case-writers, documentarians, regulatory writers, and AI agents serving any of the above.

---

## PART 1 — THE FIVE-PHASE SHAPE OF THE WORK

Almost every serious piece of investigative or analytical writing moves through five phases. They are not strictly sequential — phases recur — but they are useful as a checklist.

**Phase 1 — Find.** Locate the documents, sources, and public records that *could* matter. The error here is over-collection (drowning in material) or under-collection (missing the document that would have decided the piece). Both are failures of triage.

**Phase 2 — Read.** Read forensically, not for the argument the document is trying to make. The shift from reading-for-argument to reading-for-admission is the single most important professional habit. Most writers never make it.

**Phase 3 — Verify.** Every claim worth publishing crosses a verification threshold. The threshold rises with the damage the claim does to its subject. A claim that ruins someone's reputation needs more than a claim that names a building.

**Phase 4 — Structure.** Decide what shape the piece takes — news brief, feature, longform, sidebar, op-ed, Q&A, explainer. The structure is not aesthetic; it determines what evidence the piece can carry.

**Phase 5 — Write.** Render in a register the audience and the legal exposure permit. Administrative-neutral for regulatory writing. Narrative-controlled for feature work. Voice-forward for op-ed.

The CELTA dossier ran through all five repeatedly. The agent rebuilt context in Phase 1, did forensic reading in Phase 2, ran live web verification in Phase 3, built the eighteen-authority cartography in Phase 4, and drafted both the Jane Ryder communication and the academic write-up in Phase 5.

## PART 2 — METHODS, IN SMALL PARAGRAPHS

### 2.1 Read for admissions, not arguments

The opposing party's own correspondence is more useful than your own argument will ever be. Look for sentences that *explain* (these admit elements they were trying to defend), exhaustive lists (a list of options available is also a list of options not taken), and phrases assuming prior shared knowledge ("as you know," "as we discussed"). Jane Ryder's 1 March email defended the centre by listing four available accommodations — and in defending, admitted that none were offered. Source: legal-evidentiary tradition; transfers cleanly to journalism.

### 2.2 Timestamp to the minute when the document permits

A duty, a deadline, or a knowledge-trigger that runs on a clock should be cited as precisely as the source allows. "16:12" is more powerful than "the same afternoon." For news work this matters most in: regulatory failures, security incidents, financial trades, communications that contradict later denials. Always grab the original metadata. Screenshots without timestamps are weaker evidence than text with them.

### 2.3 The cross-reference matrix

Three columns: what the subject says, what the document shows, what the relevant standard (law, policy, claim) requires. Divergences are the story. This was how the CELTA case revealed its central asymmetry — the centre demanded perfect contemporaneous evidence from the candidate while accepting its own unannotated record amendment. The matrix is your single most useful pre-writing artefact. Build it before drafting any narrative.

### 2.4 Document tiering

Sort every source into four tiers before writing:

- **T1 — primary, in your possession.** Original documents, direct observation, on-record interviews with your subject.
- **T2 — adversarial / subject's own statements.** The subject's emails, public filings, prior interviews, social media. Most valuable for admissions.
- **T3 — contextual.** Background experts, policies, frameworks, prior reporting, scholarly literature.
- **T4 — reference.** Statutes, codes, datasets, dictionaries, glossaries.

Lead with T1, anchor in T2, support with T3, cite T4. Pieces that mix tiers without hierarchy read as muddled because they are.

### 2.5 The cliquet / ratchet logic for parallel work

When multiple bodies can rule on the same facts (regulators, courts, professional associations, certifiers), findings cascade. One ruling becomes evidence in the next. For investigative work this matters because the question is not "will any one of them act?" but "what is the cumulative probability of at least N findings within M months?" In the CELTA case: eighteen authorities, twelve binding; the threshold for a credible outcome was two findings, not eighteen. The same logic applies to corruption investigations, regulatory series, and any beat where parallel proceedings exist.

### 2.6 Verify at the moment of statement

Training data and prior knowledge are *hypotheses to check*, not facts to cite. Authority email addresses, current personnel, fees, article numbers, postal addresses, form availability — all change. In the CELTA work, an authoritative-looking email (`dreets-bretagne@dreets.gouv.fr`) bounced; the correct one (`bretag.controle-fp@dreets.gouv.fr`) only came from live search. Treat every verifiable specific as a search candidate before you put it in the text.

### 2.7 Triangulation: independent confirmation

A single source is a tip. Two independent sources is a working claim. Three is publishable in most outlets. "Independent" means not derived from the same upstream — two reporters citing the same press release are one source, not two. For documents: a document plus a corroborating witness plus a public record is the standard for serious allegations.

### 2.8 Recognise PR and spin language

Phrases that should trigger a re-read: "robust process," "we take this seriously," "in line with policy," "appropriate steps were taken," "an independent review found." Each is a signal that the document is doing PR work, not informational work. They are not lies, but they are not information. The information is what the document *does not say* in those phrases.

### 2.9 Read for what is *missing*

Absence is evidence when the document's own structure shows what should be there. A tutorial template with a blank "other issues" field two days after a disability disclosure says something. An assessor report covering TP4 and TP7 but not TP8 says something. A press release that names every executive except one says something. The missing entry method works any time you have a template, a list, or a comprehensive scope.

### 2.10 Administrative neutrality as a register

For regulatory submissions, complaint letters, and most investigative writing meant to survive legal review: drop emotional language. State facts. Cite articles by number. Concede legitimate scope. State consequences as facts ("if no substantive response within thirty days, this will be filed with X"). Threats weaken; stated next-steps strengthen. The CELTA Jane Ryder letter was deliberately silent on her conduct — her silence was allowed to speak for itself in the record.

### 2.11 Lead with the admission, not the grievance

Audiences engage differently with "the centre admitted in writing that four accommodations existed and none were offered" than with "I was treated unfairly." The first is a fact about the document; the second is a feeling about an event. Both may be true; only the first compels. This applies to features, op-eds, briefs, court filings, and pitches to editors.

### 2.12 Length discipline

Authorities, editors, and most readers do not finish long pieces. Target your decisive facts to survive every cut. The narrative is what you trim. Email body 300–500 words; feature 1,200–2,500; longform 4,000–8,000; investigative report 6,000–12,000. Going longer requires either subject-matter density that genuinely demands it or a narrative arc that earns the reader's time minute by minute.

### 2.13 Externalise method to a portable file

Whenever a working pattern stabilises across a case, write it down as a standalone skill/memo file. The CELTA work produced three such files. The logic: agents (and human collaborators) reset across sessions; files don't. For an investigator, this is the "case file methodology" doc that survives staff turnover. For an AI agent it is literally a SKILL.md.

### 2.14 Restraint when the case is structurally complete

There is a point at which adding more material weakens the dossier. Once the central admission is in writing and the documentary core is locked, further pushing dilutes the position. Recognise this point. In the CELTA case it came when Jane Ryder confirmed the UK data transfer — after that, the agent told the user to *stop sending material*. For reporters: there is a point past which more reporting does not improve the story and starts to risk it. Editors call this "killing it with kindness."

### 2.15 Recognise stalling tactics

When a subject responds with "I don't understand what you're asking, please simplify" — this is rarely sincere confusion. It is delay. The professional response: re-state the demand in three to five specific items, tie each to a piece of evidence, state the consequence of non-response, end the exchange. The clock continues to run regardless of whether the subject "understands."

## PART 3 — TOOLS, BY CATEGORY

A non-exhaustive map of what the work actually requires. AI agents should know which to call; human writers should know which to subscribe to or learn.

### 3.1 Search and retrieval

Web search (current, not training-data); Google Scholar for academic literature; Archive.org / Wayback Machine for vanished pages; the Internet Archive's TV News Archive for broadcast claims; reverse image search (TinEye, Google Images, Yandex) for image provenance; specialised legal databases (Legifrance for France, BAILII for UK, CourtListener for US).

### 3.2 Public records and corporate registries

France: SIRENE / Infogreffe for company data; Légifrance for statute; CADA process for administrative documents; HATVP for officials' declarations. UK: Companies House; Charity Commission; Land Registry; FOI requests via WhatDoTheyKnow. US: PACER for federal courts; SEC EDGAR for filings; state SOS for company registration; MuckRock for FOIA. EU: OpenCorporates aggregates many; Transparency Register for lobbying.

### 3.3 Document analysis

PDF text extraction (`pdftotext`, `extract-text`); OCR for scans (Tesseract, ABBYY); metadata extraction (`exiftool` for images, `pdfinfo` for PDFs); structured-data extraction from tables; diff tools for comparing document versions; the document forensics suite for redaction detection.

### 3.4 Source protection and secure communication

Signal for messaging; ProtonMail or Tutanota for encrypted email; SecureDrop for whistleblower intake at supported outlets; Tor Browser for source-side anonymity; air-gapped machines for the most sensitive material; physical key control for the documents.

### 3.5 Verification

EXIF data for image timestamps and location; ShadowDragon and similar OSINT suites; flight tracking (FlightRadar24); ship tracking (MarineTraffic); satellite imagery (Sentinel Hub, Planet, Google Earth historical); blockchain explorers for crypto claims.

### 3.6 Structuring and timeline-building

Timeline JS for public-facing timelines; Aeon Timeline for case work; spreadsheets (the most powerful single tool — Excel/Google Sheets for cross-reference matrices); link-analysis tools (Maltego, i2 Analyst's Notebook) for relationship mapping; mind-mapping for early-phase exploration.

### 3.7 AI-specific tools (for agents serving writers)

`web_search` and `web_fetch` for current information; `conversation_search` and `recent_chats` for context restoration across sessions; document-reading skills for PDFs, DOCX, XLSX; code execution for data work; specialised skills (`pdf-reading`, `xlsx`) for format-specific work.

## PART 4 — FORMAT SELECTION

The format determines what the piece can carry. Pick deliberately.

| Format               | When it fits                                                                     | Word range      | Evidence load                       |
|----------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------------|-----------------|-------------------------------------|
| News brief           | One new fact, public interest, time-sensitive                                    | 200–500         | One T1 + one T3                     |
| Backgrounder         | Reader needs context to understand running story                                 | 600–1,500       | Multiple T3 + T4                    |
| Feature              | Human-anchored, narrative arc, deeper analysis                                   | 1,200–3,000     | T1 anchor + T2 corroboration + T3   |
| Investigative report | New wrongdoing, multi-source, legal review needed                                | 2,500–8,000     | T1 dominant, T2 critical, T3 framing|
| Longform / longread  | Story large enough to sustain narrative across days of reading                   | 4,000–15,000    | Layered all tiers + scenes          |
| Explainer            | Concept or process the reader needs to understand                                | 800–2,000       | T3 + T4, structured for clarity     |
| Op-ed                | Argument, public-interest position, named voice                                  | 600–1,200       | T3/T4 with named author authority   |
| Q&A                  | Subject's own voice carries the piece                                            | 800–3,000       | T1 (the interview itself)           |
| Sidebar              | Adjacent fact-set that supports a main piece without diluting it                 | 200–600         | Tight T1 or T4                      |
| Live blog            | Developing event, multiple updates, low individual claim-weight                  | Cumulative      | Source per item, low individual bar |

Pick the smallest format that carries the evidence. Inflating format weakens the piece; under-formatting buries it.

## PART 5 — SOURCE TIERING FOR JOURNALISM

A re-statement of §2.4 with journalism-specific examples, because tiering matters most where it is most often muddled.

- **T1.** A document you obtained and authenticated; a recording you made; an on-record interview; a public filing you read; a press conference you attended.
- **T2.** The subject's prior public statements; their social media; their press releases; their internal communications (when obtained legitimately).
- **T3.** Other reporters' work; experts you consulted; policy frameworks; scholarly literature; NGO reports.
- **T4.** Statutes; regulations; case law; datasets; dictionaries of record; encyclopaedias.

The most common professional failure is letting T3 do the work of T1 — citing other reporters' findings as if you had verified them yourself. The reader cannot tell, but legal review will, and so will subsequent reporting that disproves the chain.

## PART 6 — LOGICS AND REASONING PATTERNS

### 6.1 Burden scaling

The burden of proof scales with the damage the claim does. Naming someone as a witness: low burden. Naming someone as having committed wrongdoing: very high burden. A piece that mixes claim-weights without scaling its evidence is legally and ethically fragile.

### 6.2 Triangulation hierarchy

A document corroborates a witness. A witness corroborates a document. A second independent witness elevates the claim further. Forensic data (timestamps, metadata, sensor records) anchors all of the above. Anonymous sources hold lowest weight unless multiple independent ones converge on the same fact-set.

### 6.3 The cliquet / cascade logic

In any environment with parallel adjudicators (regulators, courts, professional bodies, certifiers, editorial boards), findings compound. Once one body has ruled, every subsequent decision-maker treats that ruling as evidence. Plan the *sequence* of submissions, not just their content. Sometimes the right move is to file the easiest one first to generate a finding that strengthens the harder ones.

### 6.4 Pattern induction vs canon

Some methods (admissions reading, timestamp discipline, document tiering) are externally grounded and generalise. Others (the cliquet calculus as applied to the CELTA case, the ten-element submission structure) were induced from one case. Label them as induced patterns when you teach them; reapply with care; do not treat them as canon.

### 6.5 Right of reply

Before publication, every named subject of significant claims gets the chance to respond. Send specific allegations, not vague gestures. Give a deadline that is fair (typically 48–72 hours for breaking stories, a week for non-urgent investigations). Document the request and any response, in full, in the file. If the subject does not respond, say so in the piece. This is both ethical and legally protective.

## PART 7 — TIME AND INVESTMENT, REALISTICALLY

Where time actually goes, by piece type:

| Piece type            | Active research  | Drafting    | Verification + legal | Total realistic |
|-----------------------|------------------|-------------|----------------------|-----------------|
| News brief            | 1–4 hours        | 1 hour      | 1 hour               | 3–6 hours       |
| Backgrounder          | 2–5 days         | 1–2 days    | 1 day                | 1 week          |
| Feature               | 1–3 weeks        | 3–5 days    | 2–4 days             | 3–5 weeks       |
| Investigation         | 1–6 months       | 2–4 weeks   | 2–6 weeks            | 3–12 months     |
| Longform book chapter | 2–6 months       | 1–2 months  | 1 month              | 6–9 months      |

Where the time goes that surprises new investigators: document acquisition (waiting for FOI responses, chasing down originals), source cultivation (most sources need three to five contacts before useful conversation), verification cycles (each new fact opens new verification needs), legal review (a single contested paragraph can cost a week), and editor-driven restructuring (the second half of any major piece is rewriting, not writing).

The CELTA case is in the *investigation* band. Approximately five months from disclosure (25 January) to a structurally complete dossier (mid-May). That timeline is *normal* for this kind of work, not slow.

## PART 8 — HICCUPS: A TYPOLOGY AND RESPONSES

Things that go wrong, with their corrective patterns.

**H1 — A source goes silent.** Most common cause: pressure from their employer or counsel. Do not chase. Document the timing. Note that silence is itself evidence if it follows a specific question. Cultivate a parallel source on the same fact.

**H2 — A document is denied or heavily redacted.** Appeal the redaction (CADA in France, ICO in UK, FOIA appeal in US). Document the denial. Sometimes the denial itself is the story (over-redaction signals importance).

**H3 — Legal threat / cease-and-desist.** Read carefully. Most are bluffs. A real legal threat will be specific about which claim, which jurisdiction, which cause of action. Respond formally, factually, and only after legal review. Do not pull the piece on the threat alone; pull only if the underlying claim is factually wrong.

**H4 — The subject mounts a PR counter-campaign.** Stay on document. Do not engage with the PR. Continue publishing on the original evidence base. Counter-PR strengthens *your* position by making the subject's anxiety visible.

**H5 — Discovery of error after publication.** Correct quickly, prominently, and specifically. The audience trusts corrections more than denials. Note the error, the correction, and the date. Do not bury it.

**H6 — Bounced contact / wasted lead.** (The DREETS bounce, the QUALITIA candidate-list.) Verify at the point of use. When a public entity is legally required to display information, search for the actual display before listing candidates.

**H7 — Memory loss across sessions or staff turnover.** Externalise method into portable files. The agent's SKILL.md is the journalist's case-file methodology document. Both exist because human and AI memory both reset.

**H8 — Editor restructures the piece.** Expect it. The piece you deliver and the piece that publishes will differ. Hold the line on factual claims; concede on structure, ordering, and tone within reason.

**H9 — Source identity is exposed.** Catastrophic if avoidable. Plan source protection from first contact. Never email a sensitive source from a work account. Use single-use channels. Compartmentalise.

**H10 — You catch yourself inventing a specific to keep momentum.** Stop. Verify or flag. The "eagerness anti-pattern" — known from the AI context but identical in human writers under deadline pressure — is the single most common cause of corrections.

## PART 9 — THE METHODS THE AI USED IN THESE TWO CHATS

For agents reading this guideline, a transparent log of what worked in the source sessions.

The agent ran `recent_chats` and `conversation_search` repeatedly to rebuild context — never assumed continuity. It read both chats before responding to a synthesis request, not just the most recent one. It performed live `web_search` before stating any authority address, fee, or article number, and produced a verified contact table only after multiple search confirmations. When the user provided a Claude.ai share link, the agent acknowledged it could not fetch the link and asked for context restoration in another form, rather than confabulating prior content. The agent externalised its working method into SKILL.md files mid-case, before the session ended, so the method would survive. The agent maintained administrative-neutral register across all drafted correspondence, and refused to characterise the opposing party's conduct in writing even when the user implicitly invited it. The agent named the inductive nature of certain patterns (the cliquet calculus) rather than presenting them as established canon.

The agent's main self-correction during the case: an authoritative-sounding DREETS email turned out to be wrong; the lesson was codified into the Phase 9 verification discipline in the SKILL files.

## PART 10 — DECISION DICE FOR THE WRITER

A compact lookup, oriented to investigative work.

| Situation                                                              | Reach for                                              |
|------------------------------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------|
| New tip, unclear whether story                                         | T1 acquisition first; do not draft yet                 |
| Adversarial correspondence in hand                                     | Read for admissions, build cross-reference matrix      |
| Multiple regulators / parallel adjudicators                            | Cliquet logic; plan sequence of filings or queries     |
| Claim that ruins reputation                                            | Triple-source; legal review; right of reply           |
| Subject says "we take this seriously"                                  | Search for the *specific actions* they describe       |
| Document with a blank field where content was expected                 | Absence-as-evidence; cite the template structure       |
| Story arc not yet visible                                              | Build timeline before drafting                         |
| Tempted to cite another reporter's finding as if your own              | Don't; verify or attribute clearly                     |
| About to publish without giving subject a chance to respond            | Stop; send specific allegations with deadline          |
| Subject responds "please simplify, I don't understand"                 | 3–5 items, tied to evidence, with deadline             |
| Tempted to add one more lead before publication                        | If admission already in writing, stop and publish      |
| Method has stabilised across the case                                  | Write it down as a portable file                       |
| Verifiable specific about to enter your draft (address, fee, article)  | Search at the moment of use                            |
| Editor's restructure changes a factual claim                           | Push back; concede on structure, not facts             |
| You feel yourself reaching to invent a specific                        | Stop; verify or flag uncertainty                       |

## PART 11 — CASE STUDY (THE ANCHORING EXAMPLE)

For pattern-recognition purposes, the CELTA dossier in compressed form.

**Trigger:** A candidate on a Cambridge CELTA course in Brittany disclosed a recognised disability (ADHD + depression, French ALD status) by email on 25 January 2026 at 16:00. The course tutor acknowledged at 16:12.

**What the documents subsequently showed:** A pre-decision meeting (assessor + tutors) on 30 January, four days before the final teaching practice (TP8), without the candidate (WhatsApp 31 January 13:55 documents this). A Stage-Three tutorial signed two days after the disclosure with no reference to the disclosure. An assessor report covering TP4 and TP7 but not TP8. A summative record amended without annotation. The centre's named disability contact, Jane Ryder, never contacted the candidate after the disclosure although she was the named disability contact in the signed Candidate Agreement. Jane Ryder's 1 March email listed four categories of accommodation that existed (document format, audio, extra tutor time, deadline extensions), conceding by implication that none had been offered.

**Third-party confirmation:** Cambridge's own Stage One appellate report (23 April 2026) confirmed the unannotated amendment and described "only two components of assessment" against a syllabus that names six holistic factors.

**The decisive five documents:** the disclosure email + 16:12 acknowledgement; the Ryder 1 March admission; the signed Candidate Agreement; the WhatsApp message from 31 January; the Stage One report. Plus the medical synthesis from Dr Pauvert (11 February).

**The investigative pattern this case fits:** institutional defendant; protected-characteristic disclosure unaccommodated; assessment defects; internal appeal that confirmed rather than examined; multiple regulators with overlapping jurisdiction; documents that admit elements when read for admissions rather than arguments. This pattern recurs in education, healthcare, employment, financial services, prisons, immigration adjudication, and disability access cases. Any reporter or writer working in those areas can apply the same method.

**The central lesson:** the case was not won by acquiring more evidence. It was won by *moving the existing admissions, with documentary discipline, through parallel threshold-meeting submissions*. The documents did the work. The writer's job was organisation, not amplification.
